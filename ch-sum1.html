1. Matter & Its Classification

  • Matter: Anything that has mass and occupies space
  • Three states: Solid (fixed shape & volume), Liquid (fixed volume), Gas (neither)
  • Physical classification: Solid, Liquid, Gas
  • Chemical classification: Element, Compound, Mixture
  • Element: Cannot be subdivided (92 natural + 26 synthetic)
  • Compound: Fixed ratio, homogeneous, different properties
  • Mixture: Variable composition → Homogeneous (solution), Heterogeneous
  • Pure substance: Fixed composition (element/compound)
  • Impure substance: Mixture
  • Precision: Closeness of readings | Accuracy: Closeness to true value

2. Laws of Chemical Combinations (5 Laws)

  • Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier): Mass neither created nor destroyed
  • Law of Definite Proportions (Proust): Fixed ratio by mass (CO₂ always 12:32)
  • Law of Multiple Proportions (Dalton): Same elements form compounds in simple whole-number ratio (CO & CO₂ → 1:2)
  • Law of Reciprocal Proportions (Richter): Equivalent weights combine in same/inverse ratio
  • Gay Lussac’s Law of Gaseous Volumes: Gases combine in simple ratio by volume at same T,P (H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl : 1:1:2)
  • Avogadro’s Law: Equal volumes of gases contain equal molecules at same T,P
  • All laws explained by Dalton’s theory
  • JEE loves numericals on Multiple Proportions
  • Conservation of mass holds even in nuclear reactions (E=mc²)
  • Foundation of stoichiometry

3. Dalton’s Atomic Theory (Modified)

  • Matter made of indivisible atoms
  • Atoms of same element identical in mass & properties
  • Atoms of different elements have different mass & properties
  • Compounds formed by whole-number ratio of atoms
  • Atoms neither created nor destroyed
  • Limitations: Doesn’t explain isotopes, isobars, allotropes
  • Modified: 1: Atoms divisible (subatomic particles)
  • Modified 2: Isotopes exist (same Z, different A)
  • Modified 3: Isobars exist (different Z, same A)
  • JEE asks: Which postulate explains Law of Multiple Proportions?

4. Atomic & Molecular Mass

  • Atomic mass unit (amu): 1/12th mass of C-12 atom
  • 1 u = 1.66054 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
  • Relative atomic mass: Mass compared to 1/12th C-12
  • H = 1.008, O = 16.00, Na = 22.99, Cl = 35.45
  • Molecular mass: Sum of atomic masses (H₂O = 18)
  • Formula mass: For ionic compounds (NaCl = 58.5)
  • Average atomic mass: Weighted average (Cl = 35.5)
  • Gram atomic mass = Atomic mass in grams
  • Gram molecular mass = Molecular mass in grams
  • JEE asks: Atomic mass of element with 75% X-24, 25% X-26 → 24.75

5. Mole Concept – The Heart of Chemistry

  • Mole: Amount containing 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro’s number Nₐ)
  • 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ atoms/molecules/formula units
  • Molar mass (M): Mass of 1 mole in grams (numerically = molecular mass)
  • Number of moles n = mass / molar mass = N / Nₐ = V(lit)/22.4 (STP)
  • 22.4 L = Molar volume of ideal gas at STP (0°C, 1 atm)
  • At STP: 1 mole gas = 22.4 L = M grams
  • Mole triangle: n ↔ mass/M ↔ particles/Nₐ ↔ volume/22.4
  • GAY-LUSSAC + AVOGADRO = Volume ratio = Mole ratio
  • JEE asks: 11.2 L of gas at STP has how many molecules? → 3.011 × 10²³
  • 1 mole electrons = 96500 C (Faraday)

6. Percentage Composition & Empirical/Molecular Formula

  • % of element = (Atomic mass × No. of atoms × 100) / Molecular mass
  • Empirical formula: Simplest whole-number ratio
  • Molecular formula = n × Empirical formula
  • n = Molecular mass / Empirical formula mass
  • Steps: % → moles → divide by smallest → ratio → empirical → n → molecular
  • Example: 40% C, 6.7% H, 53.3% O → CH₂O (empirical), C₆H₁₂O₆ (molecular)
  • JEE favourite: Combustion analysis (C,H,O compound)
  • If % not given: Assume 100 g sample
  • Limiting reagent + % yield also asked
  • Minimum 2–3 questions every year

7. Stoichiometry & Calculations

  • Balanced equation → Mole ratio → Stoichiometry
  • 4 types: Mass-Mass, Mass-Volume, Volume-Volume, Mixed
  • Volume-Volume: Direct mole ratio (Gay Lussac)
  • Mass-Volume: Use molar mass & 22.4 L
  • POAC (Principle of Atomic Conservation) for complex reactions
  • Excess reagent = Given – Required
  • % Yield = (Actual / Theoretical) × 100
  • % Purity = (Mass of pure / Mass of sample) × 100
  • JEE asks: 80% pure 125 g Na₂CO₃ reacts with HCl → how much CO₂?
  • Always convert to moles first!

8. Limiting Reagent – Most Important

  • Reagent that is completely consumed
  • Decides amount of product formed
  • Method: Divide given moles by coefficient → smallest is limiting
  • Theoretical yield based on limiting reagent
  • Excess % = [(given – required)/required] × 100
  • JEE asks every year (direct or indirect)
  • Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, if 3 moles H₂ & 1 mole O₂ → O₂ limiting
  • Product = 2 moles H₂O, excess H₂ = 1 mole
  • Never assume excess reagent!
  • POAC also used in limiting problems

9. Concentration Terms

  • Mass % = (solute/soln)×100
  • Volume % = (vol solute/vol soln)×100
  • Molarity (M) = moles solute / litre solution
  • Molality (m) = moles solute / kg solvent (temp independent)
  • Mole fraction (x) = moles component / total moles (sum = 1)
  • ppm = (mass solute / mass soln) × 10⁶
  • Normality (N) = M × acidity/basicity (less used now)
  • Dilution: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂
  • JEE asks: Molality vs Molarity in density problems
  • For dilute solutions: M ≈ m (if density ≈ 1 g/mL)
