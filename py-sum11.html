1. Electric Charge & Its Conservation

  • Charge is quantized: q = ±ne (n = integer, e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
  • Charge is conserved: Total charge remains constant in any process
  • Two types: Positive (proton) & Negative (electron)
  • Like charges repel, unlike charges attract
  • SI unit of charge: Coulomb (C)
  • Additivity: Total charge = algebraic sum
  • Charge is invariant (does not depend on speed of body)
  • Charging methods: Friction, conduction, induction
  • Gold leaf electroscope detects presence & nature of charge
  • Minimum charge possible = ±e (except quarks)

2. Coulomb’s Law

  • F = k |q₁q₂| / r² (in vacuum), k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²/C²
  • Vector form: F₁₂ = (k q₁q₂ / r²) r̂₁₂
  • In medium: F = k |q₁q₂| / (K r²), K = dielectric constant
  • Coulomb’s law is inverse square law
  • Valid only for point charges and at rest
  • Force is central and conservative
  • 1 Coulomb is a very large charge (10¹⁸ electrons)
  • k = 1/(4πε₀), ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/Nm²
  • Coulomb’s law obeys Newton’s third law
  • Force along the line joining the charges

3. Force on Multiple Charges & Continuous Distribution

  • Principle of superposition: F_total = F₁ + F₂ + … + Fₙ
  • Net force on a charge due to others is vector sum
  • For continuous distribution: dF = k dq₁ dq₂ / r² r̂
  • Linear charge density λ = q/L, Surface σ = q/A, Volume ρ = q/V
  • Force on charge due to continuous body requires integration
  • Equilibrium: Net force = 0 on each charge
  • Stable equilibrium: Small displacement brings back
  • Unstable equilibrium: Small displacement increases deviation
  • Superposition valid only for electrostatic forces
  • Continuous distribution often solved using symmetry + Gauss law

4. Electric Field & Torque on Dipole

  • E = F/q₀ (test charge), unit: N/C or V/m
  • E due to point charge: E = kq/r² r̂
  • Superposition applies: E_total = Σ Eᵢ
  • Electric dipole moment: p = q × d (vector from –q to +q)
  • Torque on dipole in uniform field: τ = p × E = pE sinθ
  • Direction of torque: tends to align dipole with field
  • Maximum torque when θ = 90°, τ_max = pE
  • Zero torque when θ = 0° or 180° (stable & unstable equilibrium)
  • Work done to rotate dipole: W = pE (cosθ₁ – cosθ₂)
  • Electric field lines never form closed loops

5. Electric Flux & Gauss’s Theorem

  • Electric flux φ = ∫ E ⋅ dA (through a surface)
  • For uniform field: φ = E A cosθ
  • Flux through closed surface: positive (out), negative (in)
  • Gauss’s law: ∮ E ⋅ dA = q_enclosed / ε₀
  • Gauss’s law is more fundamental than Coulomb’s law
  • Applicable only when symmetry exists
  • Gaussian surface is imaginary closed surface
  • Flux depends only on enclosed charge, not size/shape
  • For no charge inside, total flux = 0
  • Gauss’s law proves inverse square nature of Coulomb’s law

6. Applications of Gauss’s Law

  • Infinite plane sheet: E = σ/(2ε₀) (independent of distance)
  • Two parallel sheets (same σ): E = σ/ε₀ between, 0 outside
  • Thin spherical shell: E = 0 inside, E = kQ/r² outside
  • Solid sphere (uniform): E = kQr/R³ (r < R), E = kQ/r² (r ≥ R)
  • Infinite line charge: E = λ/(2πε₀r)
  • Charged conductor: E = 0 inside material, E = σ/ε₀ just outside
  • Charge resides only on outer surface of hollow conductor
  • Inside cavity of conductor (no charge): E = 0
  • Electric field is always perpendicular to conductor surface
  • Sharp points have very high field (lightning conductors)

7. Electric Potential & Potential Energy

  • V = W/q₀, unit: Volt, V = –∫ E ⋅ dl (from ∞)
  • Potential due to point charge: V = kq/r
  • Potential is scalar, superposition: V_total = Σ Vᵢ
  • Equipotential surfaces are perpendicular to field lines
  • Potential energy of two charges: U = k q₁q₂ / r
  • U of dipole in uniform field: U = –p ⋅ E = –pE cosθ
  • Minimum energy (stable) at θ = 0°, U = –pE
  • Potential difference V_A – V_B = –∫_B^A E ⋅ dl
  • Electron volt (eV): 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
  • Potential at infinity is taken as zero

8. Conductors, Insulators & Dielectrics

  • Conductors: Free electrons, E = 0 inside, excess charge on surface
  • Insulators: No free charge movement
  • Dielectrics: Polar (water) or non-polar (N₂), get polarized in field
  • Polarization P = χₑ ε₀ E, K = 1 + χₑ
  • Induced charge on dielectric surface reduces net field inside
  • Electric field inside dielectric: E = E₀ / K
  • Bound charges appear due to polarization
  • Electrostatic shielding: Inside hollow conductor, E = 0 (Faraday cage)
  • Charge given to hollow conductor goes to outer surface
  • If inner charged body, induced charge appears on inner surface

9. Capacitors & Combinations

  • Capacitance C = Q/V, unit: Farad (F)
  • Parallel plate: C = ε₀A/d (without dielectric)
  • With dielectric: C = K ε₀A/d
  • Series combination: 1/C_eq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + …
  • Parallel combination: C_eq = C₁ + C₂ + …
  • When dielectric inserted (battery connected): Q increases, V same
  • When dielectric inserted (isolated): V decreases, Q same
  • Spherical capacitor: C = 4πε₀ ab/(b–a)
  • Cylindrical capacitor: C = 2πε₀L / ln(b/a)
  • Common potential in parallel: V_com = (C₁V₁ + C₂V₂)/(C₁+C₂)

10. Energy Stored in Capacitor & Van de Graaff

  • Energy stored U = (1/2) CV² = (1/2) QV = (1/2) Q²/C
  • Energy density u = (1/2) ε₀ E² (in field region)
  • Energy lost when capacitors connected = (C₁C₂/(C₁+C₂))(V₁–V₂)²/2
  • Force on dielectric slab: pulls inside capacitor
  • Van de Graaff generator produces very high potential (millions of volts)
  • Principle: Charge given to hollow conductor goes to outer surface
  • Charge sprayed on moving belt → collected inside hollow sphere
  • Maximum voltage limited by corona discharge/leakage
  • Used in particle accelerators
  • Spark occurs when E exceeds dielectric strength of air (~3 × 10⁶ V/m)
