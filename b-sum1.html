1. What is Living?

  • Living organisms show definite growth, metabolism, reproduction, consciousness & cellular organisation.
  • Growth is increase in mass & number of cells (intrinsic/intracellular).
  • Non-living objects can also grow by accumulation (extrinsic), e.g., mountains, crystals.
  • Metabolism: Sum total of all chemical reactions occurring in body (absent in non-living).
  • Reproduction is not essential for individual survival (mules, worker bees are sterile).
  • Response to stimuli (consciousness) is the most defining property of life.
  • Living organisms are self-replicating, self-regulating & interacting systems.
  • Isolated metabolic reactions in vitro are not living but “living reactions”.
  • Homeostasis: Maintenance of constant internal environment.
  • All living organisms are made up of cells (except viruses).

2. Biodiversity & Need for Classification

  • Biodiversity: Total number & types of organisms on Earth (~1.7–1.8 million species described).
  • India has ~45,000 plant & ~90,000 animal species.
  • Exact number unknown; estimated 7–8 million species exist.
  • Classification: Identification, nomenclature & grouping of organisms.
  • Helps in easy identification & study of organisms.
  • Shows evolutionary relationships (phylogeny).
  • Essential for systematic study of millions of organisms.
  • Without classification, biology would be chaotic.
  • Provides universal scientific names accepted worldwide.
  • Helps in placing new species in proper taxonomic group.

3. Taxonomy & Systematics

  • Taxonomy: Science of identification, nomenclature & classification.
  • Systematics: Study of diversity & evolutionary relationships (Linnaeus to present).
  • Taxonomy includes characterization, identification, classification & nomenclature.
  • Systematics = Taxonomy + Phylogeny.
  • Modern systematics uses morphology, anatomy, cytology, biochemistry, genetics & evolution.
  • ICBN (plants) & ICZN (animals) govern scientific naming.
  • Herbarium: Storehouse of dried, pressed plant specimens.
  • Botanical gardens, Zoological parks, Museums, Keys are taxonomic aids.
  • Taxonomic key: Based on pair of contrasting characters (couplet).
  • Monograph: Detailed account of one taxon.

4. Concept of Species & Taxonomic Hierarchy

  • Species: Group of interbreeding natural populations reproductively isolated from other groups.
  • Taxonomic hierarchy: Kingdom → Phylum/Division → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species.
  • Binomial nomenclature by Carolus Linnaeus.
  • Scientific name: Generic name (capital) + specific epithet (small), italicized or underlined.
  • Example: Homo sapiens, Mangifera indica.
  • Genus comprises related species with common characters.
  • Family has related genera, Order has related families.
  • Universal rules governed by ICBN & ICZN.
  • Same name cannot be given to two different species.
  • Name based on Latin or Latinised words.

5. Five Kingdom Classification

  • Proposed by R.H. Whittaker (1969).
  • Criteria: Cell structure, body organisation, mode of nutrition, reproduction, phylogenetic relationships.
  • Five kingdoms: Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia.
  • Monera: Prokaryotic (bacteria, archaea, cyanobacteria).
  • Protista: Unicellular eukaryotes (protozoa, algae, slime moulds).
  • Fungi: Heterotrophic, cell wall of chitin, saprophytic/parasitic.
  • Plantae: Autotrophic, cell wall of cellulose, multicellular.
  • Animalia: Heterotrophic, no cell wall, multicellular, holozoic nutrition.
  • Accepted worldwide for NEET.
  • Most scientific classification before six-kingdom system.

6. Kingdom Monera

  • Prokaryotic, no nuclear membrane, 70S ribosomes.
  • Cell wall of peptidoglycan (except archaea).
  • Unicellular, colonial or filamentous.
  • Shapes: coccus, bacillus, vibrio, spirillum.
  • Nutrition: autotrophic (photosynthetic/chemosynthetic) or heterotrophic.
  • Reproduction: mainly asexual (binary fission), genetic recombination via conjugation, transformation, transduction.
  • Examples: Bacteria, Cyanobacteria (Nostoc, Anabaena), Mycoplasma, Archaebacteria.
  • Mycoplasma: smallest living cells, no cell wall, pathogenic.
  • Archaebacteria: live in extreme conditions (halophiles, thermoacidophiles).
  • Cyanobacteria: important decomposers, nitrogen fixers, pathogens.

7. Kingdom Protista & Fungi

  • Protista: Unicellular eukaryotes, aquatic.
  • Locomotion: cilia, flagella or pseudopodia.
  • Nutrition: photosynthetic (diatoms), holozoic (Amoeba), parasitic (Plasmodium).
  • Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium, Euglena, Diatoms, Slime moulds.
  • Fungi: Heterotrophic, cell wall of chitin.
  • Saprophytic, parasitic or symbiotic nutrition.
  • Mycelium: network of hyphae (septate or aseptate).
  • Reproduction: vegetative, asexual (spores), sexual.
  • Examples: Agaricus (mushroom), Aspergillus, Penicillium, Rhizopus, Yeast (unicellular).
  • Yeast: unicellular fungus, used in fermentation.

8. Lichens, Viruses & Viroids

  • Lichens: Symbiotic association between algae (phycobiont) & fungi (mycobiont).
  • Algae prepares food, fungi provides shelter & absorbs water.
  • Indicator of air pollution (sensitive to SO₂).
  • Three types: crustose, foliose, fruticose.
  • Viruses: Obligate parasites, no cellular structure.
  • Contain either DNA or RNA, covered by protein coat.
  • Discovered by Ivanovsky & Beijerinck.
  • Viroids: Smaller than viruses, only free RNA, no protein coat (Potato spindle tuber disease).
  • Prions: Infectious proteins (cause mad cow disease).
  • Viruses & viroids are acellular, not placed in any kingdom.

9. Kingdom Plantae – Algae, Bryophytes, Pteridophytes, Gymnosperms

  • Algae: Aquatic, chlorophyll-bearing, autotrophic, thallus body. Examples: Chlamydomonas, Spirogyra, Chlorella.
  • Bryophytes: Amphibians of plant kingdom, gametophyte dominant, no vascular tissue. Examples: Moss (Funaria), Marchantia.
  • Pteridophytes: First vascular plants, sporophyte dominant, seeds absent. Examples: Ferns (Pteris), Club moss (Selaginella).
  • Gymnosperms: Naked seeds, perennial, evergreen, woody. Examples: Pinus, Cycas, Ginkgo.
  • Algae: Simple body, sexual reproduction by gametes.
  • Bryophytes: Require water for fertilization, rhizoids present.
  • Pteridophytes: Have true roots, stem, leaves; homosporous or heterosporous.
  • Gymnosperms: No flowers/fruits, pollination by wind.
  • Embryophytes: Bryophytes + Tracheophytes (vascular plants).
  • Evolutionary trend: Thallus → Gametophyte → Sporophyte dominance.

10. Kingdom Animalia – Non-Chordates & Chordates

  • Porifera: Cellular level, canal system, choanocytes. Ex: Sycon, Spongilla.
  • Coelenterata (Cnidaria): Tissue level, cnidoblasts, polyp & medusa. Ex: Hydra, Jellyfish.
  • Platyhelminthes: Flatworms, acoelomate, flame cells. Ex: Tapeworm, Liver fluke.
  • Aschelminthes: Roundworms, pseudocoelomate, dioecious. Ex: Ascaris, Wuchereria.
  • Annelida: True coelom, metamerism, closed circulation. Ex: Earthworm, Leech.
  • Arthropoda: Largest phylum, jointed legs, exoskeleton. Ex: Cockroach, Butterfly.
  • Mollusca: Soft body, muscular foot, mantle. Ex: Pila, Octopus.
  • Echinodermata: Spiny skin, water vascular system. Ex: Starfish, Sea urchin.
  • Chordates: Notochord, dorsal nerve cord, pharyngeal gill slits. Classes: Pisces, Amphibia, Reptilia, Aves, Mammalia.
  • Non-chordates up to phylum, chordates up to class level in NEET syllabus.
