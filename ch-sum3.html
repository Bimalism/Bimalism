1. Kossel-Lewis Approach & Types of Bonds

  • Octet rule: Atoms combine to attain 8 electrons in valence shell
  • Lewis dot structure: Valence electrons shown as dots
  • Ionic bond: Complete transfer of e⁻ (metal + non-metal)
  • Covalent bond: Sharing of electrons (non-metal + non-metal)
  • Coordinate bond: Both electrons from one atom (→)
  • Formal charge = Valence e⁻ – (Non-bonding + ½ Bonding e⁻)
  • Most stable structure has lowest formal charges (ideally zero)
  • Exceptions to octet: BF₃ (6e⁻), PCl₅ (10e⁻), SF₆ (12e⁻), ICl₄⁻ (14e⁻)
  • Odd electron molecules: NO, NO₂ (paramagnetic)
  • JEE loves formal charge & Lewis structure of polyatomic ions

2. Ionic Bonding & Lattice Enthalpy

  • Formation: Cation + Anion → electrostatic attraction
  • Lattice enthalpy (U): Energy released when 1 mole ionic crystal forms from gaseous ions
  • Higher lattice energy → stronger ionic bond
  • Factors: Charge (U ∝ q₁q₂) & Size (U ∝ 1/(r⁺ + r⁻))
  • Born-Haber cycle: Calculates lattice enthalpy indirectly
  • LiF > LiI, MgO > NaCl (due to charge & size)
  • Ionic compounds: High MP, soluble in water, conduct in molten/aqueous state
  • 100% ionic character is theoretical (maximum in CsF)
  • JEE asks Born-Haber cycle & comparison of lattice energy
  • Ionic character increases with electronegativity difference > 1.7

3. Covalent Bond, Fajans Rule, Dipole Moment

  • Electronegativity: Pauling scale (F = 4.0)
  • Dipole moment μ = q × d (Debye unit)
  • μ = 0 for symmetrical molecules (CO₂, BF₃, CCl₄)
  • Fajans Rule: Covalent character ∝ (charge/size of cation) & (size/charge of anion)
  • Small cation + large anion → high polarizing power → more covalent (AlCl₃, LiI)
  • Polarization → distortion of anion electron cloud
  • % ionic character = [1 – e^(-(Δχ/2)²)] × 100 (Pauling)
  • Bond length: Covalent > Ionic (due to partial charges)
  • JEE favourite: Order of covalent character LiCl < BeCl₂ < BCl₃ < CCl₄
  • Dipole moment direction: From electropositive to electronegative

4. VSEPR Theory & Shapes

  • Electron pairs repel → maximum separation
  • Order of repulsion: lp–lp > lp–bp > bp–bp
  • AX₂: Linear (BeCl₂, CO₂)
  • AX₃: Trigonal planar (BF₃), AX₃E: Trigonal pyramidal (NH₃)
  • AX₄: Tetrahedral (CH₄), AX₅: Trigonal bipyramidal (PCl₅), AX₆: Octahedral (SF₆)
  • AX₄E₂: Square planar (XeF₄), AX₃E₂: T-shaped (ClF₃), AX₂E₃: Linear (XeF₂)
  • Bond angle: 109°28′ (tetrahedral), 120° (trigonal), 90°/180° (octahedral)
  • NH₃ (107°), H₂O (104.5°) due to lone pairs
  • JEE asks shape of XeF₆ (distorted octahedral), ICl₄⁻ (square planar)
  • Back bonding reduces bond angle (BF₃ = 120° exact)

5. Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

  • Overlap of atomic orbitals → bond formation
  • Maximum overlap → stronger bond
  • Sigma (σ) bond: Head-on overlap
  • Pi (π) bond: Sideways overlap (weaker)
  • Overlap types: s-s, s-p, p-p
  • Cannot explain paramagnetic O₂ (VBT fails)
  • Explains directional nature of covalent bond
  • Bond energy ∝ extent of overlap
  • Introduced concept of hybridization
  • JEE compares VBT vs MOT

6. Hybridization – Most Important

  • Mixing of atomic orbitals → new hybrid orbitals
  • sp: Linear (180°), sp²: Trigonal (120°), sp³: Tetrahedral (109.5°)
  • sp³d: Trigonal bipyramidal, sp³d²: Octahedral, sp³d³: Pentagonal bipyramidal
  • BeCl₂ (sp), BF₃ (sp²), CH₄ (sp³), PCl₅ (sp³d), SF₆ (sp³d²)
  • dsp²: Square planar (Ni(CN)₄²⁻)
  • Hybridization depends on geometry, not vice versa
  • Lone pairs occupy hybrid orbitals (NH₃: sp³, one lone pair)
  • JEE asks hybridization of I₃⁻ (sp³d, linear), XeF₄ (sp³d², square planar)
  • Involvement of d orbitals only in elements ≥ 3rd period
  • Hybridization changes in excited state (e.g., carbon in CH₄)

7. Resonance

  • Actual structure is hybrid of two or more Lewis structures
  • Resonance hybrid has lower energy → extra stability
  • O₃, CO₃²⁻, SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻, Benzene
  • Bond order in resonance = Total bonds / Number of positions
  • CO₃²⁻: C–O bond order = 1.33
  • Benzene: C–C bond order = 1.5, all bonds equal
  • Resonance energy = Experimental – Calculated from one structure
  • JEE asks bond length in ozone, carbonate
  • Greater the resonance structures → more stable
  • Delocalization of π electrons

8. Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT) – JEE Advanced Favourite

  • LCAO: Molecular orbitals from linear combination of atomic orbitals
  • Bonding MO (lower energy), Antibonding MO (higher energy)
  • Bond order = (N₆ – Nₐ)/2
  • Bond order > 0 → stable molecule
  • O₂: KK (σ2s)² (σ*2s)² (σ2p)² (π2p)⁴ (π*2p)² → BO = 2, paramagnetic
  • N₂: BO = 3 (diamagnetic), strongest bond
  • F₂: BO = 1, B₂: BO = 1 (paramagnetic)
  • Energy order for O₂, F₂: σ2p < π2p < π*2p < σ*2p
  • For N₂ and lighter: σ2p > π2p
  • JEE asks MO configuration & magnetic nature of O₂⁺, N₂⁺, etc.

9. Hydrogen Bonding & Metallic Bonding

  • H-bonding: H attached to N, O, F → attraction with another N/O/F
  • Intermolecular (H₂O, NH₃, HF) & Intramolecular (o-nitrophenol)
  • Explains: High BP of H₂O > H₂S, solubility of NH₃, ice density
  • Ice has open cage structure → less dense than water
  • Strength: 5–10 kcal/mol (stronger than van der Waals)
  • Metallic bonding: Delocalized electrons in metal lattice
  • Explains conductivity, malleability, luster
  • Strength ∝ number of valence electrons & charge on nucleus
  • JEE asks: Why HF has highest BP among hydrogen halides?
  • Abnormal properties of H₂O due to extensive H-bonding
