1. Alpha-Particle Scattering Experiment

  • Geiger-Marsden experiment (1909) under Rutherford
  • Alpha particles (He²⁺) from radium bombarded thin gold foil
  • Most α-particles passed straight (empty space)
  • Few deflected by large angles (>90°)
  • Very few (1 in 20,000) bounced back
  • Concluded positive charge & mass concentrated in tiny nucleus
  • Distance of closest approach ≈ 10⁻¹⁴ m
  • Impact parameter b = (Ze² cot(θ/2))/(4πε₀ E)
  • Smaller b → larger scattering angle
  • Proved existence of atomic nucleus

2. Rutherford Atomic Model

  • Atom has tiny, massive, positively charged nucleus
  • Nucleus size ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ m, atom ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m
  • Electrons revolve around nucleus like planets
  • Centrifugal force balances Coulomb attraction
  • Could not explain stability of atom
  • Predicted continuous radiation → energy loss → collapse
  • Failed to explain discrete line spectra
  • Success: Explained large angle scattering
  • Drawback: Violated classical electromagnetism
  • Paved way for Bohr’s quantized model

3. Bohr’s Model of Atom (1913)

  • Electrons revolve in stationary circular orbits
  • Angular momentum quantized: m v r = n h/(2π)
  • Only orbits where L = n ħ are allowed
  • No radiation in stationary orbits
  • Radiation when electron jumps between levels
  • Centripetal force = Coulomb force
  • Radius rₙ = n² a₀ (a₀ = 0.529 Å for H)
  • Velocity vₙ = α c / n (α ≈ 1/137)
  • Explained stability and line spectrum
  • Applicable mainly to hydrogen-like atoms

4. Energy Levels in Bohr Model

  • Total energy Eₙ = – (13.6 Z²)/n² eV
  • E₁ = –13.6 eV (ground state for H)
  • Eₙ ∝ –1/n² (negative → bound state)
  • n = ∞ → E = 0 (ionization)
  • Ionization energy of H = 13.6 eV
  • Energy difference ΔE = hν (photon energy)
  • KEₙ = –Eₙ, PEₙ = 2 Eₙ
  • KE decreases, PE becomes more negative with n
  • Binding energy = |Eₙ|
  • Energy level diagram crucial for spectrum

5. Hydrogen Spectrum Series

  • 1/λ = R (1/n₁² – 1/n₂²) (R = Rydberg constant)
  • Lyman: UV (n₁=1, n₂>1)
  • Balmer: Visible (n₁=2, n₂>2)
  • Paschen: IR (n₁=3, n₂>3)
  • Brackett: IR (n₁=4), Pfund (n₁=5)
  • Series limit: n₂ → ∞ (maximum frequency)
  • R = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹
  • H-alpha: 656.3 nm (red), H-beta: 486.1 nm (blue-green)
  • Explained perfectly by Bohr model
  • Proof of quantized energy levels

6. Isotopes, Isobars, Isotones

  • Isotopes: Same Z, different A (e.g., ₁H¹, ₁H², ₁H³)
  • Isobars: Same A, different Z (e.g., ₆C¹⁴, ₇N¹⁴)
  • Isotones: Same N (neutrons), different Z (e.g., ₆C¹³, ₇N¹⁴)
  • Isotopes have same chemical properties
  • Used in radiocarbon dating (C-14)
  • U-235 and U-238 are isotopes
  • Deuterium (H-2) used in heavy water moderator
  • Isobars lie on diagonal in N vs Z plot
  • Most elements have multiple stable isotopes
  • Mass number determines isotope name

7. Radioactivity – α, β, γ

  • Alpha (α): ₂He⁴ nucleus, +2e charge, highly ionizing
  • Beta-minus (β⁻): electron, moderate penetration
  • Beta-plus (β⁺): positron
  • Gamma (γ): EM radiation, highly penetrating
  • α changes A by –4, Z by –2
  • β⁻ changes Z by +1, A unchanged
  • γ: no change in A or Z
  • α stopped by paper, β by Al foil, γ by thick lead
  • Discovered by Becquerel (1896)
  • Natural for A > 209

8. Radioactive Decay Law

  • N = N₀ e^{–λt} (exponential decay)
  • Activity A = λ N = A₀ e^{–λt}
  • Half-life T₁/₂ = ln2 / λ ≈ 0.693 / λ
  • Mean life τ = 1/λ
  • Unit of activity: becquerel (Bq), curie (Ci)
  • 1 Ci = 3.7 × 10¹⁰ Bq
  • After n half-lives: N = N₀ / 2ⁿ
  • Decay constant λ independent of external conditions
  • Successive decay: Bateman equations
  • Carbon dating uses C-14 half-life ≈ 5730 years

9. Mass-Energy Equivalence

  • E = mc² (Einstein, 1905)
  • 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c²
  • 1 kg mass ≡ 9 × 10¹⁶ J
  • Mass and energy are equivalent
  • Mass defect Δm converted to energy
  • Explains energy release in nuclear reactions
  • Used in pair production & annihilation
  • Rest mass energy of proton ≈ 938 MeV
  • Fundamental principle of nuclear physics
  • Verified in every nuclear reaction

10. Binding Energy per Nucleon Curve

  • Binding energy B = [Z m_p + (A–Z) m_n – M] c²
  • B/A maximum near A = 56 (Fe-56 most stable)
  • Curve rises sharply till A ≈ 30, then slowly to peak
  • Falls slowly for heavier nuclei
  • High B/A → more stable nucleus
  • He-4, C-12, O-16 have high B/A
  • Fission: heavy nucleus → two medium (energy release)
  • Fusion: light nuclei → heavier (up to Fe)
  • Plateau around 8.8 MeV/nucleon for A=50–60
  • Key to understanding nuclear power

11. Nuclear Fission & Fusion

  • Fission: Heavy nucleus splits into two + neutrons + energy
  • U-235 + n → Ba + Kr + 3n + ~200 MeV
  • Chain reaction possible (k>1 critical)
  • Atom bomb: uncontrolled chain reaction
  • Nuclear reactor: controlled with moderator & control rods
  • Fusion: Light nuclei combine → heavier + energy
  • H-2 + H-3 → He-4 + n + 17.6 MeV
  • Sun & stars powered by fusion
  • H-bomb: uncontrolled fusion
  • Fusion needs extremely high temperature (10⁷ K)
