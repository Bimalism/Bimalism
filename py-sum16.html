1. Photoelectric Effect - Fundamental Concepts

17 Key Points
  • 1Ejection of electrons from metal surface by electromagnetic radiation
  • 2Occurs only if incident frequency > threshold frequency (ν₀)
  • 3Threshold frequency depends on metal's work function
  • 4Photoelectrons emitted almost instantaneously (<10⁻⁹ s)
  • 5Maximum kinetic energy independent of radiation intensity
  • 6Number of photoelectrons ∝ intensity of incident radiation
  • 7Stopping potential (V₀) required to stop fastest photoelectrons
  • 8Maximum kinetic energy: KE_max = e V₀
  • 9Completely unexplained by classical wave theory of light
  • 10Established particle nature of light (photons)
  • 11Saturation current reached at high intensity
  • 12No time lag even at very low intensities
  • 13Work function φ = hν₀ (minimum energy needed)
  • 14Photoelectric current increases with accelerating voltage
  • 15Frequency dependence stronger than intensity dependence
  • 16Monochromatic light produces electrons with energy distribution
  • 17Different metals have different threshold frequencies

2. Hertz & Lenard's Experimental Observations

15 Key Points
  • 1Hertz (1887): UV light facilitates spark discharge across gap
  • 2UV light ejects electrons from negative electrode (cathode)
  • 3Lenard (1900): Systematic study with evacuated tube
  • 4Observed photocurrent directly proportional to light intensity
  • 5Found stopping potential independent of light intensity
  • 6Discovered threshold frequency phenomenon
  • 7Electrons emitted only above certain frequency (ν > ν₀)
  • 8Maximum kinetic energy increases linearly with frequency
  • 9KE independent of intensity (contradicted wave theory)
  • 10No measurable time lag for electron emission
  • 11Classical wave theory predicted energy accumulation over time
  • 12Wave theory couldn't explain frequency threshold
  • 13Experiments conducted with different metal surfaces
  • 14Used variable voltage to measure stopping potential
  • 15Observations provided crucial data for Einstein's theory

3. Einstein's Photoelectric Equation & Quantum Theory

16 Key Points
  • 1Light consists of discrete energy packets: photons
  • 2Photon energy: E = hν (h = Planck's constant)
  • 3Einstein (1905): One photon ejects one electron
  • 4Einstein's equation: hν = φ + KE_max
  • 5φ = hν₀ (work function = minimum energy needed)
  • 6KE_max = h(ν - ν₀) = hν - φ
  • 7Stopping potential relation: e V₀ = h(ν - ν₀)
  • 8V₀ vs ν graph is straight line with slope = h/e
  • 9X-intercept gives threshold frequency (ν₀)
  • 10Y-intercept gives -φ/e (negative)
  • 11Successfully explained all photoelectric observations
  • 12Confirmed light has particle nature (photon concept)
  • 13Einstein received Nobel Prize in 1921 for this work
  • 14Millikan's experiments (1916) verified Einstein's equation
  • 15Photoelectric effect demonstrated quantum nature of light
  • 16Bridge between classical and quantum physics

4. Matter Waves & de Broglie Hypothesis

17 Key Points
  • 1de Broglie (1924): Matter exhibits wave nature
  • 2de Broglie wavelength: λ = h/p = h/(m v)
  • 3h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
  • 4Wave-particle duality: All entities have both properties
  • 5Macroscopic objects: λ extremely small → wave nature negligible
  • 6Electrons, protons: λ significant → observable wave effects
  • 7Faster moving particle → smaller de Broglie wavelength
  • 8Explained Bohr's quantization: electron orbits as standing waves
  • 9Condition: nλ = 2πr (standing wave around nucleus)
  • 10Foundation of wave mechanics and quantum mechanics
  • 11For non-relativistic particles: λ = h/√(2mE)
  • 12Thermal de Broglie wavelength at temperature T
  • 13Applied to electrons in crystals → energy bands
  • 14Matter waves are probability waves (Born interpretation)
  • 15Group velocity of matter wave = particle velocity
  • 16Phase velocity > speed of light (not physically significant)
  • 17Concept extended to all particles: neutrons, atoms, molecules

5. Davisson-Germer Experiment (1927)

16 Key Points
  • 1First direct experimental confirmation of electron waves
  • 2Electrons accelerated through potential difference V
  • 3Theoretical wavelength: λ = h/√(2m_e eV)
  • 4Scattered from nickel crystal (natural diffraction grating)
  • 5Observed diffraction pattern with intensity maxima
  • 6Maxima at same angles as X-ray diffraction patterns
  • 7Peak intensity at specific angle (54V, 50° scattering)
  • 8Experimental wavelength = 0.165 nm
  • 9Theoretical de Broglie wavelength = 0.167 nm
  • 10Excellent agreement confirmed wave nature of electrons
  • 11Proved electrons behave as waves under suitable conditions
  • 12Direct evidence supporting de Broglie hypothesis
  • 13Established wave-particle duality for matter particles
  • 14Davisson and Germer shared Nobel Prize (1937)
  • 15Independent confirmation by G.P. Thomson (1928)
  • 16Foundation for electron microscopy and diffraction techniques

6. Wave-Particle Duality Concepts

18 Key Points
  • 1Complementarity principle (Bohr): Both wave and particle aspects
  • 2Cannot observe both aspects simultaneously in single experiment
  • 3Light: Shows wave nature in diffraction/interference
  • 4Light: Shows particle nature in photoelectric effect
  • 5Matter: Shows particle nature in track detectors
  • 6Matter: Shows wave nature in diffraction experiments
  • 7De Broglie relation connects wave and particle properties
  • 8Wavefunction ψ describes quantum state
  • 9Probability density = |ψ|² (Born interpretation)
  • 10Uncertainty principle consequence of duality
  • 11Matter waves are not electromagnetic waves
  • 12Matter waves are probability amplitude waves
  • 13Dual behavior depends on experimental setup
  • 14Macroscopic objects: Particle behavior dominates
  • 15Quantum objects: Both behaviors significant
  • 16Wave packets used to represent localized particles
  • 17Superposition principle for matter waves
  • 18Foundation of quantum field theory

7. Photon Properties & Characteristics

15 Key Points
  • 1Quantum of electromagnetic radiation
  • 2Zero rest mass, always moves at speed c
  • 3Energy: E = hν = hc/λ
  • 4Momentum: p = h/λ = E/c
  • 5Spin = 1 (integer spin → boson)
  • 6Electrically neutral
  • 7Can be created/absorbed in interactions
  • 8Exhibits both wave and particle properties
  • 9Photon number not conserved
  • 10Different types: radio, microwave, IR, visible, UV, X-ray, gamma
  • 11Higher frequency → higher energy per photon
  • 12Intensity = photon flux × energy per photon
  • 13Photon can transfer entire energy to electron
  • 14Photon in medium has reduced speed but same frequency
  • 15Virtual photons mediate electromagnetic force
