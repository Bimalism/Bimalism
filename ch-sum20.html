1. Detection of Extra Elements (N, S, Halogens)

  • Lassaigne’s test: Organic compound + Na → NaCN, Na₂S, NaX (X = Cl, Br, I)
  • Nitrogen: Na + C + N → NaCN → FeSO₄ → Fe₄[Fe(CN)₆]₃ (Prussian blue)
  • Sulphur (two tests): (i) Lead acetate → black PbS, (ii) Sodium nitroprusside → violet
  • If N & S both present → Na + C + N + S → NaSCN → blood red with Fe³⁺
  • Halogens: Na + X → NaX → AgNO₃ → AgX ppt (Cl-white, Br-pale yellow, I-yellow)
  • Cl⁻: Soluble in NH₄OH; Br⁻: Partially soluble; I⁻: Insoluble
  • Layer test for Br & I: CS₂ or CHCl₃ layer → orange (Br₂), violet (I₂)
  • Phosphorus detected separately by ammonium molybdate → yellow ppt
  • Fuse with Na only in dry test tube (wet tube → explosion due to H₂)
  • NEET favourite: Blood red colour = N + S both present

2. Detection of Functional Groups

  • Alcoholic –OH: Ceric ammonium nitrate → red colour
  • Phenolic –OH: FeCl₃ → violet/blue/green/red colour; Libermann’s nitroso → blue/green
  • Aldehyde: Tollens’ → silver mirror; Fehling → red ppt; Schiff’s → pink
  • Ketone: 2,4-DNP → orange ppt; NaHCO₃ → no effervescence (distinguishes from acid)
  • Carboxylic acid: NaHCO₃ → brisk effervescence of CO₂; litmus blue → red
  • Amino group (–NH₂): Carbylamine test → foul smell; Hinsberg → soluble sulphonamide in alkali
  • Aldehyde vs Ketone: Aldehyde reduces Tollens’ & Fehling; Ketone does not
  • Iodoform test: CH₃–CO– or CH₃–CH(OH)– → yellow ppt of CHI₃
  • Both aldehyde & phenolic give neutral FeCl₃ test → false positive avoided by distillation
  • NEET point: Acetaldehyde, acetophenone, ethanol, 2-propanol give iodoform test

3. Preparation of Inorganic Compounds

  • Mohr’s salt: FeSO₄ + FeSO₄ → FeSO₄·(NH₄)₂SO₄·6H₂O (double salt)
  • Mohr’s salt resists oxidation in air (used in KMnO₄ titration)
  • Potash alum: K₂SO₄ + Al₂(SO₄)₃ → KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O (crystallisation)
  • Alum prepared by adding K₂SO₄ to Al₂(SO₄)₃ solution slowly
  • Both are double salts, not complex salts
  • Mohr’s salt → pale green crystals
  • Potash alum → octahedral crystals
  • Reaction for Mohr’s salt: Fe²⁺ + 2NH₄⁺ + 2SO₄²⁻ + 6H₂O → crystal
  • Alum used as mordant in dyeing
  • NEET: Both give tests of individual ions in solution

4. Preparation of Organic Compounds

  • Acetanilide: Aniline + (CH₃CO)₂O → C₆H₅NHCOCH₃ (acetylation)
  • p-Nitroacetanilide: Acetanilide + HNO₃/H₂SO₄ (0–10°C) → para isomer major
  • Aniline yellow (p-aminoazobenzene): Aniline + NaNO₂/HCl → diazonium → + aniline in acetic acid
  • Iodoform: Acetone or ethanol + I₂ + NaOH → CHI₃ (yellow ppt + haloform smell)
  • Acetanilide prepared to protect –NH₂ group during nitration
  • Iodoform reaction also given by acetaldehyde & methyl ketones
  • Aniline yellow is an azo dye
  • Iodoform used as antiseptic due to liberation of free iodine
  • Recrystallisation solvent: water for acetanilide, ethanol for iodoform
  • NEET favourite: Iodoform test positive for CH₃CH(OH)– group

5. Titrimetric Exercises

  • Oxalic acid vs KMnO₄ (hot acidic medium): 5(COOH)₂ + 2MnO₄⁻ → 10CO₂ + 2Mn²⁺ (self-indicator)
  • Mohr’s salt vs KMnO₄: 5Fe²⁺ + MnO₄⁻ → 5Fe³⁺ + Mn²⁺ (pink endpoint)
  • KMnO₄ acts as self-indicator (pink colour disappears)
  • Oxalic acid titration in hot condition only
  • Indicator for acid-base: phenolphthalein (colourless → pink), methyl orange (red → yellow)
  • Normality of KMnO₄ in acidic medium = 5 × Molarity
  • Equivalent weight of oxalic acid = Mol. wt / 2
  • Mohr’s salt equivalent weight = Mol. wt / 1 (Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺)
  • Standard solution: usually oxalic acid or Mohr’s salt
  • NEET: KMnO₄ cannot be used in presence of Cl⁻ (Cl₂ liberated)

6. Qualitative Salt Analysis – Cations

  • Group I: Pb²⁺ → white ppt with dil. HCl (soluble in hot water)
  • Group II: Cu²⁺ → blue ppt with H₂S (black with excess)
  • Group III: Al³⁺ → white gelatinous ppt with NH₄OH in presence of NH₄Cl
  • Group IV: Ni²⁺ → apple green solution, dimethylglyoxime → scarlet red ppt
  • Group V: Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺, Ca²⁺ → carbonates white, flame test: Ba-green, Sr-red, Ca-brick red
  • Zn²⁺ → white ppt with H₂S in basic medium, dissolves in NaOH
  • Fe³⁺ → blood red with SCN⁻ test
  • NH₄⁺ → Nessler’s reagent → brown ppt
  • Mg²⁺ → white ppt with NH₄OH + Na₂HPO₄ (magnesium ammonium phosphate)
  • Confirmatory test for Cu²⁺: excess NH₄OH → deep blue [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺

7. Qualitative Salt Analysis – Anions

  • CO₃²⁻ & SO₃²⁻ → dil. H₂SO₄ → CO₂ & SO₂ gas
  • S²⁻ → lead acetate → black PbS; sodium nitroprusside → violet
  • SO₄²⁻ → BaCl₂ → white ppt insoluble in HCl
  • NO₂⁻ → brown fumes with dil. H₂SO₄, KI + starch → blue
  • NO₃⁻ → brown ring test with FeSO₄ + conc. H₂SO₄
  • Cl⁻ → AgNO₃ → white ppt soluble in NH₄OH
  • Br⁻ → AgNO₃ → pale yellow ppt partially soluble in NH₄OH
  • I⁻ → AgNO₃ → yellow ppt insoluble in NH₄OH
  • CH₃COO⁻ → neutral FeCl₃ → blood red colour + acetic acid smell on heating
  • NEET favourite: Brown ring complex is [Fe(H₂O)₅NO]SO₄

8. Important Experiments

  • Enthalpy of solution of CuSO₄: exothermic (ΔHₛₒₗ negative)
  • Enthalpy of neutralization (HCl + NaOH): ≈ –57.1 kJ/mol
  • Lyophilic sol (starch, gelatin): stable, reversible
  • Lyophobic sol (Fe(OH)₃, As₂S₃): unstable, prepared by condensation
  • Peptization: conversion of ppt into sol by electrolyte
  • Kinetic study: I⁻ + H₂O₂ → rate depends on [I⁻] & [H₂O₂]
  • Order w.r.t I⁻ & H₂O₂ is 1 each
  • Clock method used with sodium thiosulphate & starch
  • Enthalpy of neutralization of strong acid–strong base is constant
  • NEET: Lyophobic sols show Tyndall effect strongly
