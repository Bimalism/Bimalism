1. Electromagnetic Induction

15 Key Points
  • 1Production of induced emf due to change in magnetic flux
  • 2Discovered independently by Faraday (1831) and Henry
  • 3Requires change in magnetic flux (Φ) through a circuit
  • 4Flux change occurs via motion of magnet, coil, or changing current
  • 5Induced emf exists only while magnetic flux is changing
  • 6Closed conducting loop needed for induced current to flow
  • 7Two main types: self-induction and mutual induction
  • 8Basis for electric generators, transformers, inductors
  • 9No physical contact required between magnetic source and circuit
  • 10Foundation of alternating current (AC) power technology
  • 11Magnetic flux linkage = NΦ (for N turns)
  • 12Relative motion between magnet and coil induces emf
  • 13Induced emf magnitude depends on rate of flux change
  • 14Works in both directions - moving magnet or moving coil
  • 15First experimental evidence linking electricity and magnetism

2. Faraday's Laws of Electromagnetic Induction

16 Key Points
  • 1First law: Changing magnetic flux induces emf in a circuit
  • 2Second law: ε = -dΦ/dt (instantaneous induced emf)
  • 3For N turns: ε = -N dΦ/dt
  • 4Magnetic flux Φ = B⋅A = BA cosθ (scalar product)
  • 5Unit of magnetic flux: weber (Wb)
  • 61 Wb = 1 tesla × 1 m²
  • 71 volt = 1 weber/second
  • 8Negative sign indicates opposition (Lenz's law)
  • 9Flux change possible through: B change, A change, θ change
  • 10Motional emf formula: ε = Blv (conductor moving ⊥ to B)
  • 11General form includes both transformer and motional emf
  • 12Differential form: ∮E·dl = -dΦ/dt (integral around loop)
  • 13Average emf = -N ΔΦ/Δt
  • 14Flux can be changed by changing current in nearby circuit
  • 15Time-varying magnetic field creates electric field
  • 16Faraday's law is one of Maxwell's four equations

3. Lenz's Law & Direction of Induced Current

14 Key Points
  • 1Induced current flows to oppose the change producing it
  • 2Statement of conservation of energy for EMI
  • 3Determines direction of induced emf/current
  • 4Negative sign in Faraday's law represents Lenz's law
  • 5Magnet approaching coil → induced current creates repelling pole
  • 6Magnet receding from coil → induced current creates attracting pole
  • 7Prevents perpetual motion machines (energy conservation)
  • 8Explains electromagnetic braking systems
  • 9Always opposes the change in magnetic flux
  • 10Applied to determine current direction in moving conductors
  • 11Right-hand rule variations for different configurations
  • 12Induced magnetic field opposes change in original B-field
  • 13Makes electromagnetic systems inherently stable
  • 14Combined with Faraday gives complete EMI description

4. Eddy Currents & Applications

13 Key Points
  • 1Circulating currents induced in bulk conductors by changing B
  • 2Flow in closed loops perpendicular to magnetic field
  • 3Cause I²R heating losses (eddy current losses)
  • 4Reduced by laminating core with insulating layers
  • 5Used in induction heating furnaces for metals
  • 6Applied in electromagnetic damping (dead-beat galvanometers)
  • 7Basis of metal detectors and security systems
  • 8Energy loss in transformer cores minimized by lamination
  • 9Direction opposes flux change (follows Lenz's law)
  • 10Also called Foucault currents after discoverer
  • 11Stronger in solid conductive cores than laminated
  • 12Used in eddy current brakes for trains and rollercoasters
  • 13Can cause unwanted heating in motors and transformers

5. Self and Mutual Inductance

17 Key Points
  • 1Self inductance: L = NΦ/I (henry)
  • 2Back emf: ε = -L dI/dt
  • 3For solenoid: L = μ₀N²A/l
  • 4Energy stored in inductor: U = ½LI²
  • 5Mutual inductance: M = N₂Φ₂₁/I₁
  • 6Induced emf in secondary: ε₂ = -M dI₁/dt
  • 7For coaxial solenoids: M = μ₀N₁N₂A/l
  • 8Relation: M ≤ √(L₁L₂)
  • 9Coefficient of coupling: k = M/√(L₁L₂)
  • 10Inductor opposes sudden changes in current
  • 11Time constant in RL circuit: τ = L/R
  • 12Self inductance depends on geometry and core material
  • 13Mutual inductance depends on relative position/orientation
  • 14Energy density in magnetic field: u = B²/(2μ₀)
  • 15Inductors store energy in magnetic field
  • 16Mutual inductance symmetric: M₁₂ = M₂₁
  • 17Inductance in series: L_eq = L₁ + L₂ ± 2M

6. Alternating Current Fundamentals

18 Key Points
  • 1Instantaneous current: i = I₀ sin(ωt + φ)
  • 2Instantaneous voltage: v = V₀ sin(ωt + φ)
  • 3RMS value: I_rms = I₀/√2 ≈ 0.707I₀
  • 4Average value over full cycle = 0
  • 5Domestic supply: 220V RMS, 311V peak, 50Hz frequency
  • 6Inductive reactance: X_L = ωL = 2πfL
  • 7Capacitive reactance: X_C = 1/(ωC) = 1/(2πfC)
  • 8Impedance: Z = √[R² + (X_L - X_C)²]
  • 9In pure L: Current lags voltage by 90° (π/2)
  • 10In pure C: Current leads voltage by 90° (π/2)
  • 11Phase angle: φ = tan⁻¹[(X_L - X_C)/R]
  • 12Power factor: cosφ = R/Z
  • 13Form factor = RMS/Average = 1.11 for sine wave
  • 14Peak factor = Peak/RMS = √2 = 1.414 for sine wave
  • 15AC can be stepped up/down using transformers
  • 16Phasor representation simplifies AC circuit analysis
  • 17Average power: P_avg = V_rms I_rms cosφ
  • 18AC meters read RMS values

7. LC Oscillations & Tank Circuit

15 Key Points
  • 1Energy oscillates between L (magnetic) and C (electric)
  • 2Natural frequency: f = 1/(2π√LC)
  • 3Angular frequency: ω = 1/√LC
  • 4Charge oscillation: q = Q₀ cos(ωt + φ)
  • 5Current oscillation: i = -ωQ₀ sin(ωt + φ) = I₀ sin(ωt + φ)
  • 6Total energy constant: U = ½Q₀²/C = ½LI₀²
  • 7Ideal LC circuit (no R) → undamped oscillations
  • 8Analogous to mechanical SHM: L↔m, 1/C↔k
  • 9Used in radio tuning circuits (resonant frequency)
  • 10Differential equation: d²q/dt² + (1/LC)q = 0
  • 11Phase difference between q and i = 90°
  • 12When q = max, i = 0; when q = 0, i = max
  • 13Energy in capacitor: U_C = q²/(2C)
  • 14Energy in inductor: U_L = ½Li²
  • 15With resistance → damped oscillations

8. LCR Series Circuit Analysis

17 Key Points
  • 1Impedance: Z = √[R² + (X_L - X_C)²]
  • 2Circuit current: I = V/Z
  • 3Voltage across R: V_R = IR (in phase with I)
  • 4Voltage across L: V_L = IX_L (leads I by 90°)
  • 5Voltage across C: V_C = IX_C (lags I by 90°)
  • 6Phasor diagram: V = V_R + V_L + V_C (vector sum)
  • 7Average power: P = VI cosφ = I²R
  • 8Power factor: cosφ = R/Z
  • 9At resonance: Z_min = R, I_max = V/R
  • 10Voltage magnification: V_L and V_C can exceed source V
  • 11Wattless current in purely reactive components
  • 12Apparent power: S = VI (VA)
  • 13Real power: P = VI cosφ (W)
  • 14Reactive power: Q = VI sinφ (VAR)
  • 15Complex impedance approach simplifies analysis
  • 16Admittance: Y = 1/Z (siemens)
  • 17Phase angle φ positive for inductive, negative for capacitive

9. Resonance in LCR Circuits

15 Key Points
  • 1Occurs when X_L = X_C → Z_min = R
  • 2Resonant frequency: ω₀ = 1/√LC
  • 3Resonant frequency: f₀ = 1/(2π√LC)
  • 4At resonance: current maximum, power factor = 1
  • 5V_L and V_C equal in magnitude, 180° out of phase
  • 6Quality factor: Q = ω₀L/R = 1/(ω₀CR)
  • 7Higher Q → sharper resonance peak
  • 8Bandwidth: Δω = R/L or Δf = f₀/Q
  • 9Half-power frequencies: ω₁, ω₂ = ω₀ ± R/(2L)
  • 10Used in radio/TV tuning circuits
  • 11Voltage magnification factor = Q at resonance
  • 12Series resonance → voltage amplification
  • 13Parallel resonance → current amplification
  • 14Resonance curve shows response vs frequency
  • 15Selectivity ∝ Q (higher Q = more selective)

10. AC Generator & Transformer Principles

20 Key Points
  • 1AC generator emf: ε = NBAω sinωt
  • 2Slip rings provide AC output (commutator for DC)
  • 3Transformer works on mutual induction principle
  • 4Voltage ratio: V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p = k (turns ratio)
  • 5Current ratio: I_p/I_s = N_s/N_p (ideal, no losses)
  • 6Step-up: N_s > N_p → V_s > V_p
  • 7Step-down: N_s < N_p → V_s < V_p
  • 8Ideal transformer: P_out = P_in, efficiency 100%
  • 9Core laminated to minimize eddy current losses
  • 10Copper losses: I²R losses in windings
  • 11Hysteresis loss due to core magnetization reversal
  • 12High voltage transmission reduces I²R line losses
  • 13AC generator frequency: f = N/60 (N in rpm, for 2 poles)
  • 14Flux leakage reduces transformer efficiency
  • 15Transformer efficiency = (P_out/P_in) × 100%
  • 16Autotransformer: single winding with tap
  • 17Three-phase generators more efficient for power generation
  • 18Transformer oil provides insulation and cooling
  • 19Rotating magnetic field principle used in alternators
  • 20Load matching possible with transformers
