1. Concept of Magnetic Field

  • Magnetic field B: Force experienced by moving charge or current
  • Unit: Tesla (T), 1 T = 1 N/(A·m)
  • Magnetic field lines: Closed loops, never intersect
  • Direction given by north pole of compass
  • Magnetic field is a vector quantity
  • Force on charge q: F = q (v × B)
  • Magnetic field due to moving charge or current
  • No magnetic monopole exists (unlike electric)
  • Magnetic field lines emerge from N and enter S
  • Tangents to field lines give direction of B

2. Oersted’s Experiment

  • Current-carrying conductor produces magnetic field
  • Discovered by Hans Christian Oersted in 1820
  • Magnetic needle deflects near current-carrying wire
  • Deflection direction depends on current direction
  • Field lines are concentric circles around wire
  • Right-hand thumb rule: Thumb → I, Fingers → B
  • No effect when current is parallel to needle
  • Proved electricity and magnetism are related
  • Reversed current reverses deflection
  • Foundation of electromagnetism

3. Biot-Savart Law & Applications

  • dB = (μ₀/4π) (I dl sinθ / r²)
  • μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A (permeability of free space)
  • Direction by right-hand rule
  • At centre of circular loop: B = μ₀ I / (2a)
  • On axis of loop: B = μ₀ I a² / [2 (a² + x²)^{3/2}]
  • At equator of loop: B opposite to axial field
  • For straight wire: B = μ₀ I / (2π d)
  • Biot-Savart is magnetic analogue of Coulomb’s law
  • Valid for any current element
  • Used when symmetry is absent

4. Ampere’s Circuital Law & Applications

  • ∮ B · dl = μ₀ I_enclosed
  • Valid for steady currents and any closed path
  • Used when symmetry exists (cylindrical, planar)
  • For infinite straight wire: B = μ₀ I / (2π r)
  • Inside solid conductor: B ∝ r
  • Outside: B ∝ 1/r
  • For infinite sheet: B = μ₀ K / 2 (K = surface current density)
  • Analogous to Gauss’s law in electrostatics
  • Direction by right-hand thumb rule
  • Net current through Amperian loop is counted

5. Straight & Toroidal Solenoid

  • Solenoid: Long coil of insulated wire
  • Inside long solenoid: B = μ₀ n I (uniform)
  • n = N/l = turns per unit length
  • Outside long solenoid: B ≈ 0
  • Field lines inside are parallel and uniform
  • Toroid: Circular solenoid, B = μ₀ N I / (2π r)
  • For tightly wound toroid: B = μ₀ n I (nearly uniform)
  • No field outside ideal toroid
  • Used in transformers, electromagnets
  • End effects negligible in long solenoid

6. Force on Conductor & Cyclotron

  • Force on current-carrying wire: F = I (l × B)
  • F = B I l sinθ, maximum when θ = 90°
  • No force if I parallel to B
  • Cyclotron frequency: f = qB / (2π m)
  • Independent of speed and radius
  • Used to accelerate charged particles
  • Maximum energy limited by relativistic effects
  • Path is spiral due to increasing radius
  • Dees are semicircular hollow chambers
  • Magnetic field perpendicular to plane of dees

7. Moving Coil Galvanometer & Ammeter/Voltmeter

  • Torque τ = N I A B sinθ → deflecting torque
  • Current sensitivity: θ/I = N A B / k
  • Voltage sensitivity: θ/V
  • Conversion to ammeter: Small shunt S = (I_g G)/(I – I_g)
  • Conversion to voltmeter: High resistance R = V/I_g – G
  • Galvanometer resistance G
  • Ammeter has low resistance, connected in series
  • Voltmeter has high resistance, connected in parallel
  • Damping: Eddy currents in Al frame
  • Radial field for linear scale

8. Current Loop as Magnetic Dipole

  • Magnetic moment m = I A (vector ⊥ to plane)
  • Direction by right-hand thumb rule
  • On axis: B = (μ₀/4π) (2 m / r³)
  • On equatorial line: B = (μ₀/4π) (m / r³) opposite to m
  • For bar magnet, m = m × 2l (pole strength × length)
  • Torque on dipole: τ = m × B = m B sinθ
  • Potential energy U = –m · B = –m B cosθ
  • Stable equilibrium: θ = 0°, U = –mB
  • Unstable: θ = 180°
  • Work done to rotate: W = m B (cosθ₁ – cosθ₂)

9. Earth’s Magnetic Field & Elements

  • Earth acts as giant bar magnet
  • Geomagnetic poles near geographic poles
  • Horizontal component B_H, Vertical B_V
  • Total field B = √(B_H² + B_V²)
  • Angle of dip θ: tanθ = B_V / B_H
  • Angle of declination α: angle between geographic & magnetic meridian
  • At magnetic equator: θ = 0°, B_V = 0
  • At magnetic poles: θ = 90°, B_H = 0
  • B_H = B cosθ, B_V = B sinθ
  • Neutral points: where B_earth cancelled by external field

10. Magnetic Materials – Para, Dia, Ferro & Permanent Magnets

  • Diamagnetic: Weakly repelled, μ_r < 1 (Bi, Cu, H₂O)
  • Paramagnetic: Weakly attracted, μ_r > 1 (Al, Na, O₂)
  • Ferromagnetic: Strongly attracted (Fe, Co, Ni)
  • Curie temperature: Above T_c, ferro → para
  • Hysteresis: Energy loss per cycle in B-H loop
  • Retentivity: Remaining magnetism after removing H
  • Coercivity: H needed to demagnetize
  • Soft iron: Low coercivity → electromagnets
  • Steel/Alnico: High coercivity → permanent magnets
  • Magnetic susceptibility χ = M/H, μ_r = 1 + χ
