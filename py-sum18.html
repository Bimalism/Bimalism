1. Energy Bands in Solids

18 Key Points
  • 1Energy levels split into bands due to interatomic interaction
  • 2Valence band: occupied by valence electrons (highest occupied)
  • 3Conduction band: allows free movement of electrons
  • 4Forbidden gap (E_g): energy difference between VB & CB
  • 5Fermi level: energy level with 50% occupation probability
  • 6In conductors, Fermi level lies within conduction band
  • 7In semiconductors, Fermi level lies in middle of band gap
  • 8In insulators, Fermi level lies near valence band
  • 9Overlapping bands → conductor (no forbidden gap)
  • 10Large E_g (>3 eV) → insulator
  • 11Small E_g (≤1 eV) → semiconductor
  • 12At 0 K, semiconductors behave as insulators
  • 13Thermal excitation promotes electrons to CB
  • 14Hole: vacancy created when electron leaves VB
  • 15Electron-hole pair generation increases with temperature
  • 16Recombination: electron falls back to VB
  • 17Donor levels near conduction band (n-type)
  • 18Acceptor levels near valence band (p-type)

2. Conductors, Insulators & Semiconductors

19 Key Points
  • 1Conductors: E_g = 0, huge free electrons (Cu, Ag, Al)
  • 2Insulators: E_g > 3 eV, no conduction (diamond, glass, rubber)
  • 3Semiconductors: E_g ≈ 0.7–1.1 eV (Si: 1.1 eV, Ge: 0.7 eV)
  • 4Intrinsic semiconductor: pure, n_e = n_h (Si, Ge)
  • 5Extrinsic semiconductor: doped (n-type or p-type)
  • 6n-type: pentavalent impurity (P, As, Sb) → donor levels
  • 7p-type: trivalent impurity (B, Ga, In) → acceptor levels
  • 8Conductivity increases with temperature in semiconductors
  • 9Conductivity decreases with temperature in conductors
  • 10Majority carriers: electrons in n-type, holes in p-type
  • 11Minority carriers: holes in n-type, electrons in p-type
  • 12Mobility μ = v_d / E (electrons > holes in Si)
  • 13Drift current: due to applied electric field
  • 14Diffusion current: due to concentration gradient
  • 15Resistivity of Si ≈ 2300 Ω·m (intrinsic at 300K)
  • 16Carrier concentration: n_i² = n_e × n_h (law of mass action)
  • 17Fermi level shifts with doping
  • 18Compound semiconductors: GaAs, InP (direct band gap)
  • 19Temperature coefficient negative for semiconductors

3. Semiconductor Diode (p-n Junction)

17 Key Points
  • 1Formed by joining p-type and n-type semiconductors
  • 2Depletion region: immobile ions, no free carriers
  • 3Potential barrier: 0.7 V (Si), 0.3 V (Ge)
  • 4Built-in potential prevents further diffusion
  • 5Forward bias: p-side +ve → reduces barrier → high current
  • 6Reverse bias: p-side –ve → increases barrier → very low current
  • 7Diode acts as one-way valve for current
  • 8Knee voltage: minimum voltage for conduction
  • 9Dynamic resistance r_d = ΔV/ΔI (small in forward bias)
  • 10Breakdown in reverse bias at high voltage
  • 11Symbol: arrow shows conventional current direction
  • 12Space charge region width ~ √V (reverse bias)
  • 13Minority carrier injection in forward bias
  • 14Reverse saturation current depends on temperature
  • 15Junction capacitance: C_j = εA/W (depletion capacitance)
  • 16Diffusion capacitance in forward bias (stored charge)
  • 17Temperature coefficient ≈ -2 mV/°C for forward voltage

4. I-V Characteristics of p-n Diode

16 Key Points
  • 1Forward bias: exponential rise after knee voltage
  • 2Diode equation: I = I_s(e^(V/ηV_T) - 1)
  • 3I_s = reverse saturation current (nA-μA)
  • 4V_T = kT/e ≈ 26 mV at 300K (thermal voltage)
  • 5η = ideality factor (1-2)
  • 6Reverse bias: very small saturation current (μA)
  • 7Reverse current almost independent of voltage
  • 8At high reverse voltage → Zener or avalanche breakdown
  • 9Forward current increases rapidly with small voltage
  • 10Ideal diode: zero resistance forward, infinite reverse
  • 11Cut-in voltage: 0.7 V (Si), 0.3 V (Ge)
  • 12Reverse saturation current doubles every 10°C rise
  • 13Graph: non-linear in forward, nearly flat in reverse
  • 14Avalanche breakdown: due to impact ionization
  • 15Zener breakdown: due to tunneling (high doping)
  • 16Used to study diode behavior and parameters

5. Diode as Rectifier

18 Key Points
  • 1Converts AC to pulsating DC
  • 2Half-wave rectifier: only one half-cycle passes
  • 3Full-wave rectifier: both half-cycles utilized
  • 4Center-tap transformer FWR: 2 diodes + center tap
  • 5Bridge rectifier: 4 diodes, no center tap needed
  • 6Efficiency: HWR → 40.6%, FWR → 81.2%
  • 7Ripple factor: HWR → 1.21, FWR → 0.48
  • 8Form factor: HWR → 1.57, FWR → 1.11
  • 9Peak Inverse Voltage: HWR → V_m, FWR center-tap → 2V_m, bridge → V_m
  • 10Filter (C, LC, π) used to smooth output
  • 11Capacitor filter reduces ripple significantly
  • 12Output frequency = input frequency (HWR)
  • 13Output frequency = 2 × input frequency (FWR)
  • 14Ripple frequency same as output frequency
  • 15Transformer utilization factor: HWR → 0.287, FWR → 0.693
  • 16Used in power supplies for electronic devices
  • 17Voltage regulators used after rectification
  • 18Three-phase rectifiers for high power applications

6. Photodiode & Applications

15 Key Points
  • 1Operated in reverse bias for linear response
  • 2Reverse current proportional to light intensity
  • 3Depletion region widened for more sensitivity
  • 4Quantum efficiency: electrons generated per photon
  • 5Responsivity: current output per watt of light
  • 6Used in light detection, optical communication
  • 7Fast response (ns range), high sensitivity
  • 8Symbol has incoming light arrows
  • 9Current increases linearly with illumination
  • 10Used in smoke detectors, barcode scanners
  • 11Can work in photovoltaic mode (zero bias)
  • 12Materials: Si (visible), InGaAs (infrared)
  • 13Dark current: reverse current without light
  • 14PIN photodiode: intrinsic layer for better response
  • 15Avalanche photodiode: internal gain via avalanche

7. Solar Cell (Photovoltaic Cell)

16 Key Points
  • 1p-n junction converts light energy → electrical energy
  • 2Works in photovoltaic mode (no external bias)
  • 3Open circuit voltage V_oc ≈ 0.6 V (Si)
  • 4Short circuit current I_sc ∝ light intensity
  • 5Efficiency ≈ 15–22% (commercial silicon)
  • 6I-V curve similar to diode but in first quadrant
  • 7Fill factor = (V_m I_m)/(V_oc I_sc) (typically 0.7-0.8)
  • 8Maximum power point (knee of I-V curve)
  • 9Used in solar panels, calculators, satellites
  • 10Renewable & clean energy source
  • 11Anti-reflection coating increases efficiency
  • 12Series/parallel connections for voltage/current
  • 13Temperature coefficient negative for V_oc
  • 14Thin-film solar cells: CdTe, CIGS
  • 15Multi-junction solar cells for higher efficiency
  • 16Solar tracking improves energy collection

8. Zener Diode & Voltage Regulation

15 Key Points
  • 1Specially designed for reverse breakdown
  • 2Sharp breakdown at V_z (Zener voltage, 2V-200V)
  • 3Used in reverse bias as voltage regulator
  • 4V_out remains constant even if I or V_in changes
  • 5High doping → thin depletion → tunneling (Zener effect)
  • 6Power rating: P_z = V_z × I_z(max)
  • 7Used in voltage stabilizers, reference sources
  • 8Dynamic resistance very low in breakdown
  • 9Symbol has bent ends (like Z)
  • 10Operates safely in breakdown region
  • 11Temperature coefficient: positive for V_z > 6V
  • 12Series resistor R_s limits current
  • 13Line regulation: ΔV_out/ΔV_in (should be small)
  • 14Load regulation: ΔV_out/ΔI_L (should be small)
  • 15Zener impedance: slope of V-I curve in breakdown

9. Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT)

18 Key Points
  • 1Three layers: Emitter, Base, Collector
  • 2Two types: NPN or PNP
  • 3Two p-n junctions: E-B (forward), C-B (reverse)
  • 4Emitter heavily doped, base thin & lightly doped
  • 5Collector moderately doped, largest area
  • 6Current amplification due to transistor action
  • 7I_E = I_B + I_C (Kirchhoff's current law)
  • 8β = I_C / I_B (50–300 typically, current gain)
  • 9α = I_C / I_E (0.95–0.99, common-base current gain)
  • 10Relation: β = α/(1-α), α = β/(1+β)
  • 11Three configurations: Common Base, Common Emitter, Common Collector
  • 12CE most common (high power gain)
  • 13CB has no current gain but voltage gain
  • 14CC (emitter follower) has current gain, voltage gain ≈ 1
  • 15Transistor action: emitter injects, base controls, collector collects
  • 16Base width modulation (Early effect)
  • 17Thermal runaway: positive feedback at high temperature
  • 18Heat sink needed for power transistors

10. Characteristics of Transistor (CE Configuration)

16 Key Points
  • 1Input characteristics: I_B vs V_BE (like forward diode)
  • 2Output characteristics: I_C vs V_CE for different I_B
  • 3Three regions: Active, Cut-off, Saturation
  • 4Cut-off: both junctions reverse → I_C ≈ I_CEO
  • 5Saturation: both junctions forward → I_C max, V_CE ≈ 0.2V
  • 6Active: E-B forward, C-B reverse → amplification
  • 7Current gain β = ΔI_C / ΔI_B (ac beta)
  • 8Early effect: slight slope in output curve (finite output resistance)
  • 9Power gain = voltage gain × current gain
  • 10Transfer characteristics: I_C vs I_B (linear relationship)
  • 11Breakdown voltage: BV_CEO, BV_CBO, BV_EBO
  • 12Leakage currents: I_CBO, I_CEO = (1+β)I_CBO
  • 13Thermal voltage V_T = kT/e ≈ 26 mV at 300K
  • 14Transconductance g_m = I_C/V_T (≈ 38.5 mS for I_C=1mA)
  • 15Input impedance: h_ie = β/g_m (≈ 2.6kΩ for β=100, I_C=1mA)
  • 16Used to study transistor behavior and parameters

11. Transistor as Amplifier & Oscillator

17 Key Points
  • 1Amplifier: small change in I_B → large change in I_C
  • 2CE configuration gives both voltage & current gain
  • 3Voltage gain A_v = -g_m R_C (for CE with R_E=0)
  • 4Current gain A_i = β (approximately)
  • 5Power gain A_p = A_v × A_i
  • 6Biasing circuits: fixed bias, emitter bias, voltage divider
  • 7Q-point (quiescent point): DC operating point
  • 8Stability factor S = ΔI_C/ΔI_CO (should be small)
  • 9Oscillator: positive feedback (Barkhausen criterion)
  • 10Barkhausen: |Aβ| ≥ 1, phase shift = 0° or 360°
  • 11LC oscillators: Hartley, Colpitts
  • 12RC oscillators: Phase shift, Wien bridge
  • 13Crystal oscillators: high frequency stability
  • 14Feedback types: positive (oscillators), negative (amplifiers)
  • 15Gain-bandwidth product constant
  • 16Class A, B, AB, C amplifiers (efficiency differences)
  • 17Darlington pair: very high current gain (β²)

12. Logic Gates & Transistor as Switch

18 Key Points
  • 1Transistor as switch: Cut-off → OFF, Saturation → ON
  • 2Cut-off: V_out = V_CC (logic 1)
  • 3Saturation: V_out ≈ 0.2V (logic 0)
  • 4Small base current controls large collector current
  • 5NOT gate (inverter): single transistor
  • 6NAND gate: universal gate (can make all others)
  • 7AND, OR using diode-transistor logic (DTL)
  • 8TTL: Transistor-Transistor Logic (fast switching)
  • 9CMOS: Complementary MOS (low power)
  • 10Truth tables define gate behavior
  • 11Boolean algebra: A·B (AND), A+B (OR), A' (NOT)
  • 12De Morgan's theorems: (A·B)' = A' + B', (A+B)' = A'·B'
  • 13Fan-out: number of gates a gate can drive
  • 14Propagation delay: time from input to output
  • 15Noise margin: tolerance to voltage variations
  • 16Power dissipation: static + dynamic
  • 17Flip-flops: basic memory elements
  • 18Used in digital circuits, computers, microcontrollers

13. Integrated Circuits & MOSFET

16 Key Points
  • 1IC: Complete circuit on single silicon chip
  • 2SSI, MSI, LSI, VLSI, ULSI (scale of integration)
  • 3MOSFET: Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field Effect Transistor
  • 4MOSFET types: Enhancement, Depletion (n-channel, p-channel)
  • 5Voltage controlled device (gate controls channel)
  • 6High input impedance (almost infinite)
  • 7CMOS: Complementary MOS (n-MOS + p-MOS)
  • 8CMOS advantages: low power, high density
  • 9JFET: Junction Field Effect Transistor
  • 10Operational amplifier (Op-amp): high gain differential amplifier
  • 11Op-amp characteristics: high input Z, low output Z
  • 12Op-amp applications: adder, subtractor, integrator, differentiator
  • 13Digital IC families: TTL, CMOS, ECL
  • 14Microprocessor: CPU on single chip
  • 15Memory ICs: RAM, ROM, Flash
  • 16Moore's Law: transistor count doubles every 2 years

14. Communication Systems Basics

15 Key Points
  • 1Basic elements: Transmitter, Channel, Receiver
  • 2Modulation: superimposing signal on carrier wave
  • 3Types: AM, FM, PM (analog); ASK, FSK, PSK (digital)
  • 4Amplitude Modulation: m = (A_max - A_min)/(A_max + A_min)
  • 5Frequency Modulation: Δf ∝ modulating signal
  • 6Bandwidth: AM → 2f_m, FM → 2(Δf + f_m)
  • 7Need for modulation: antenna size, multiplexing, range
  • 8Demodulation: extracting signal from modulated wave
  • 9Transducers: convert one form of energy to electrical
  • 10Noise: unwanted signal interfering with transmission
  • 11Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR): quality measure
  • 12Attenuation: signal strength reduction with distance
  • 13Amplification: increasing signal strength
  • 14Propagation modes: ground wave, sky wave, space wave
  • 15Digital communication advantages: noise immunity, encryption
