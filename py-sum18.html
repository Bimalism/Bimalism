1. Energy Bands in Solids

  • Energy levels split into bands due to interatomic interaction
  • Valence band: occupied by valence electrons
  • Conduction band: allows free movement of electrons
  • Forbidden gap (E_g): energy difference between VB & CB
  • Overlapping bands → conductor
  • Large E_g (>3 eV) → insulator
  • Small E_g (≤1 eV) → semiconductor
  • At 0 K, semiconductors behave as insulators
  • Thermal excitation promotes electrons to CB
  • Fermi level lies in middle of band gap in intrinsic semiconductor

2. Conductors, Insulators & Semiconductors

  • Conductors: E_g = 0, huge free electrons (Cu, Ag)
  • Insulators: E_g > 3 eV, no conduction (diamond, glass)
  • Semiconductors: E_g ≈ 0.7–1.1 eV (Si: 1.1 eV, Ge: 0.7 eV)
  • Intrinsic: pure, n_e = n_h
  • Extrinsic: doped (n-type or p-type)
  • n-type: pentavalent impurity (P, As) → donor levels
  • p-type: trivalent impurity (B, Ga) → acceptor levels
  • Conductivity increases with temperature in semiconductors
  • Majority & minority carriers
  • Mobility μ = v_d / E

3. Semiconductor Diode (p-n Junction)

  • Formed by joining p-type and n-type semiconductors
  • Depletion region: immobile ions, no free carriers
  • Potential barrier: 0.7 V (Si), 0.3 V (Ge)
  • Forward bias: p-side +ve → reduces barrier → high current
  • Reverse bias: p-side –ve → increases barrier → very low current
  • Diode acts as one-way valve for current
  • Knee voltage: minimum voltage for conduction
  • Dynamic resistance r_d = ΔV/ΔI
  • Breakdown in reverse bias at high voltage
  • Symbol: arrow shows conventional current direction

4. I-V Characteristics of p-n Diode

  • Forward bias: exponential rise after knee voltage
  • Reverse bias: very small saturation current (μA)
  • Reverse current almost independent of voltage
  • At high reverse voltage → Zener or avalanche breakdown
  • Forward current increases rapidly with small voltage
  • Ideal diode: zero resistance forward, infinite reverse
  • Cut-in voltage: 0.7 V (Si), 0.3 V (Ge)
  • Reverse saturation current doubles every 10°C rise
  • Graph: non-linear in forward, nearly flat in reverse
  • Used to study diode behavior

5. Diode as Rectifier

  • Converts AC to pulsating DC
  • Half-wave rectifier: only one half-cycle passes
  • Full-wave rectifier: both half-cycles (center-tap or bridge)
  • Efficiency: HWR → 40.6%, FWR → 81.2%
  • Ripple factor: HWR → 1.21, FWR → 0.48
  • Bridge rectifier: no center tap, 4 diodes
  • PIV: HWR → V_m, FWR center-tap → 2V_m, bridge → V_m
  • Filter (C, LC, π) used to smooth output
  • Output frequency = 2 × input frequency (FWR)
  • Used in power supplies

6. Photodiode

  • Operated in reverse bias
  • Reverse current proportional to light intensity
  • Depletion region widened for more sensitivity
  • Used in light detection, optical communication
  • Fast response, high sensitivity in reverse bias
  • Symbol has incoming light arrows
  • Current increases linearly with illumination
  • Used in smoke detectors, barcode scanners
  • Can work in photovoltaic mode also
  • Made of materials sensitive to light (Si, InGaAs)

7. Solar Cell

  • p-n junction converts light energy → electrical energy
  • Works in photovoltaic mode (no external bias)
  • Open circuit voltage ≈ 0.6 V (Si)
  • Short circuit current ∝ intensity
  • Efficiency ≈ 15–22% (commercial)
  • I-V curve similar to diode but in first quadrant
  • Fill factor = (V_m I_m)/(V_oc I_sc)
  • Used in panels, calculators, satellites
  • Maximum power point (knee of I-V curve)
  • Renewable & clean energy source

8. Zener Diode

  • Specially designed for reverse breakdown
  • Sharp breakdown at V_z (Zener voltage)
  • Used in reverse bias as voltage regulator
  • V_out remains constant even if I or V_in changes
  • High doping → thin depletion → tunneling (Zener effect)
  • Power rating: P_z = V_z × I_z(max)
  • Used in voltage stabilizers, reference
  • Dynamic resistance very low
  • Symbol has bent ends
  • Operates safely in breakdown region

9. Junction Transistor (BJT)

  • Three layers: Emitter, Base, Collector
  • NPN or PNP types
  • Two p-n junctions: E-B (forward), C-B (reverse)
  • Emitter heavily doped, base thin & lightly doped
  • Current amplification due to transistor action
  • I_E = I_B + I_C
  • β = I_C / I_B (50–300 typically)
  • α = I_C / I_E (0.95–0.99)
  • Three configurations: CB, CE, CC
  • CE most common

10. Characteristics of Transistor (CE)

  • Input characteristics: I_B vs V_BE (like forward diode)
  • Output characteristics: I_C vs V_CE for different I_B
  • Three regions: Active, Cut-off, Saturation
  • Cut-off: both junctions reverse → I_C ≈ 0
  • Saturation: both junctions forward → I_C max
  • Active: E-B forward, C-B reverse → amplification
  • Current gain β = ΔI_C / ΔI_B
  • Early effect: slight slope in output curve
  • Power gain = β² × resistance gain
  • Used to study transistor behavior

11. Transistor as Amplifier, Oscillator & Logic Gates

  • Amplifier: small change in I_B → large change in I_C
  • CE configuration gives voltage & current gain
  • Voltage gain A_v = β (R_C / r_e)
  • Oscillator: positive feedback (Barkhausen criterion)
  • LC, RC, crystal oscillators
  • NOT gate: single transistor inverter
  • AND, OR, NAND, NOR using diode-transistor logic (DTL)
  • Digital circuits use saturation & cut-off
  • Transistor switch: fast on/off
  • Foundation of all digital electronics

12. Transistor as a Switch

  • Cut-off → OFF state (V_out = V_CC)
  • Saturation → ON state (V_out ≈ 0)
  • Small base current controls large collector current
  • Used in digital circuits, relays, LEDs
  • Switching time: rise & fall time
  • Low power consumption
  • High speed switching (ns range)
  • Inverter (NOT gate) is basic switch
  • LED driver circuit classic example
  • Base resistor limits I_B
