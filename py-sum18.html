1. Reflection of Light

  • Laws: i = r, incident ray, reflected ray, normal coplanar
  • Plane mirror: virtual, erect, laterally inverted, same size
  • Image distance = object distance
  • Field of view increases with mirror size
  • Deviation δ = 180° – 2i
  • Multiple reflections: number of images = (360°/θ) – 1
  • Velocity of image perpendicular to mirror = –v_object
  • Parallel component of velocity unchanged
  • Extended object: image same size only if object || mirror
  • Used in periscope, kaleidoscope

2. Spherical Mirror

  • Concave: converging, real/inverted or virtual/erect
  • Convex: diverging, always virtual/erect/diminished
  • Sign convention: New Cartesian (object distance –ve)
  • Pole P, Centre C, Focus F, Radius R = 2f
  • Paraxial rays: small angle approximation
  • Concave: f –ve, R –ve
  • Convex: f +ve, R +ve
  • Aperture affects spherical aberration
  • Focal length independent of medium (paraxial)
  • Mirror formula derived using similar triangles

3. Mirror Formula & Magnification

  • 1/v + 1/u = 1/f
  • Magnification m = –v/u = h_i/h_o
  • m negative → inverted image
  • |m| > 1 enlarged, |m| < 1 diminished
  • For real object u always negative
  • Concave mirror: beyond C → diminished real, at C → same size, between C-F → enlarged real
  • Between F-P → enlarged virtual erect
  • Power P = –1/f (in metre)
  • Velocity of image: v_i = –m² v_o
  • Standard ray diagrams: 3 rays

4. Refraction of Light

  • Snell’s law: n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
  • Absolute refractive index n = c/v
  • Relative n₂₁ = n₂/n₁
  • Real depth/apparent depth = n
  • Lateral shift t = d sin(i–r)/cos r
  • Critical angle sin c = 1/n (denser to rarer)
  • Refraction through slab: lateral shift, emergent || incident
  • Apparent shift = t(1–1/n)
  • n = tan i_p (polarising angle, Brewster)
  • Speed decreases in denser medium

5. Total Internal Reflection & Applications

  • Occurs when i > c and light goes rarer → denser
  • Optical fibre: core n₁, cladding n₂ < n₁
  • Acceptance angle sin i_max = √(n₁² – n₂²)
  • Numerical aperture NA = √(n₁² – n₂²)
  • Mirage due to TIR in hot road
  • Brilliance of diamond (high n, small c)
  • Endoscopy, periscope prism (45°)
  • 100% reflection, no loss
  • Fish-eye view in water
  • Porro prism erects image

6. Lens Maker Formula, Magnification & Power

  • 1/f = (n–1)(1/R₁ – 1/R₂)
  • Convex lens: R₁ +ve, R₂ –ve → f +ve
  • Concave lens: R₁ +ve, R₂ +ve → f –ve
  • Lens formula: 1/v – 1/u = 1/f
  • m = v/u = h_i/h_o
  • Power P = 1/f (metre) = 100/f (cm) dioptre
  • Convex converging, concave diverging
  • Air bubble in water acts as concave lens
  • f changes in different medium
  • Thin lens approximation

7. Combination of Thin Lenses

  • 1/F = 1/f₁ + 1/f₂ + …
  • Power adds: P = P₁ + P₂
  • Lenses in contact act as single lens
  • Separated: image of first acts as object for second
  • Achromatic doublet removes chromatic aberration
  • Two thin lenses separated by d: 1/F = 1/f₁ + 1/f₂ – d/(f₁f₂)
  • Used in compound microscope, telescope
  • Displacement method to find f
  • Convex + concave can make converging/diverging
  • Equivalent focal length calculation

8. Refraction & Dispersion through Prism

  • Deviation δ = i₁ + i₂ – A
  • Minimum deviation δ_m = 2i – A (i₁=i₂)
  • n = sin((A+δ_m)/2) / sin(A/2)
  • Dispersion: violet deviates most, red least
  • Angular dispersion = δ_v – δ_r = (n_v – n_r)A
  • Dispersive power ω = (n_v – n_r)/(n–1)
  • Direct vision spectroscope: flint + crown prism
  • Thin prism: δ = (n–1)A
  • Rainbow due to dispersion + TIR in droplets
  • Spectrum purity depends on ω

9. Scattering of Light & Human Eye

  • Rayleigh scattering ∝ 1/λ⁴ → sky blue, sunset red
  • Human eye: power 58–60 D, least distance 25 cm
  • Myopia: far point < ∞ → concave lens
  • Hypermetropia: near point > 25 cm → convex lens
  • Presbyopia: loss of accommodation in old age
  • Astigmatism: cylindrical lens
  • Retina acts as screen, fovea most sensitive
  • Persistence of vision 1/16 s
  • Accommodation: ciliary muscles change lens shape
  • Range of vision: 25 cm to ∞

10. Microscope & Astronomical Telescope

  • Astronomical telescope (normal): m = f_o/f_e, L = f_o + f_e
  • Final image inverted
  • Galilean telescope: erect image, concave eyepiece
  • Terrestrial: erecting lens, L = f_o + 4f + f_e
  • Magnifying power m = f_o/f_e (1 + D/f_e) for relaxed eye
  • Larger objective aperture → brighter image
  • Reflecting telescope avoids chromatic aberration
  • Newtonian, Cassegrain designs
  • Resolving power ∝ aperture
  • Used for distant objects

11. Compound Microscope

  • m = m_o × m_e = (v_o/u_o) (D/f_e or 1 + D/f_e)
  • L = v_o + f_e (tube length)
  • Image by objective inside focal length of eyepiece
  • Final image inverted
  • Maximum magnification ≈ 1500–2000
  • Higher NA → better resolution
  • Oil immersion increases NA
  • Resolving power d = 1.22λ/(2 n sinβ)
  • Used for magnified close objects
  • Intermediate image real & inverted

12. Wavefront & Huygens’ Principle

  • Wavefront: locus of points in same phase
  • Spherical, plane, cylindrical wavefronts
  • Huygens: every point acts as secondary source
  • Secondary wavelets spherical
  • Envelope gives new wavefront
  • Explains reflection, refraction, diffraction
  • Plane wave → plane wavefront
  • Backward wavelet cancelled by interference
  • Only forward propagation
  • Foundation of wave optics

13. Interference of Light

  • Superposition of two coherent waves
  • Constructive: path diff = nλ → bright
  • Destructive: path diff = (2n–1)λ/2 → dark
  • Coherent sources: constant phase difference
  • Incoherent: random phase → no sustained interference
  • Intensity I = 4 I₀ cos²(φ/2)
  • I_max = (√I₁ + √I₂)², I_min = (√I₁ – √I₂)²
  • Energy redistributed, total conserved
  • Seen only if fringe width > resolution of eye
  • Stable pattern needs coherence

14. Young’s Double Slit Experiment

  • Fringe width β = λD/d
  • Angular separation θ = λ/d
  • Central fringe bright (path diff = 0)
  • Bright fringes: x = nλD/d
  • Dark fringes: x = (2n–1)λD/2d
  • If one slit closed → uniform illumination
  • White light → coloured fringes, central white
  • Immersion in liquid → β decreases
  • Shift by thin sheet: Δx = (n–1)t D/d
  • Proof of wave nature of light

15. Diffraction due to Single Slit

  • Bending of light around obstacles
  • Central maximum brightest & widest
  • Angular width of central max = 2λ/a
  • Minima: a sinθ = nλ
  • Secondary maxima between minima
  • Intensity falls rapidly away from centre
  • Smaller slit → more spreading
  • Fresnel: near obstacles, Fraunhofer: far/infinity
  • Straight edge → fringes inside geometric shadow
  • Proof of wave nature

16. Width of Central Maximum

  • Linear width = 2λD/a
  • Angular width = 2λ/a
  • Inversely proportional to slit width a
  • Smaller a → larger diffraction
  • At a = λ → central max spreads 2 radians
  • Intensity I = I₀ (sin²β/β²), β = (π a sinθ)/λ
  • First minima at sinθ = ±λ/a
  • Half width = λD/a
  • Used to measure wavelength
  • Resolution limit depends on diffraction

17. Resolving Power of Instruments

  • Minimum angular separation resolvable
  • Telescope: RP = a/(1.22λ) (a = aperture)
  • Microscope: d_min = 1.22λ/(2 n sinβ) = 1.22λ/(2 NA)
  • Larger aperture → better resolution
  • Rayleigh criterion: just resolved when central max of one coincides with first min of other
  • Oil immersion increases NA → better resolution
  • Electron microscope: λ very small → high resolution
  • Human eye resolution ≈ 1 arc minute
  • Limit due to diffraction, not lens quality
  • Higher λ → poorer resolution

18. Polarisation & Plane Polarised Light

  • Transverse wave nature of light
  • Unpolarised: vibrations in all directions ⊥ propagation
  • Plane polarised: vibrations in one plane
  • Polaroids transmit one direction only
  • Brewster’s law: tan i_p = n
  • At i_p, reflected light fully polarised ⊥ to plane
  • Malus law: I = I₀ cos²θ
  • Optical activity: rotation by sugar solution
  • Dichroism, scattering also polarises
    • < UFO>Proof that light is transverse wave
