1. Plant Morphology & Modifications

  • Morphology: External structure of flowering plants – root, stem, leaf, flower, fruit, seed.
  • Root modifications: Tap root → storage (carrot, turnip), respiratory (pneumatophores in mangroves), prop (banyan).
  • Stem modifications: Underground – rhizome, bulb, corm, tuber (potato); Aerial – tendril, thorn, phylloclade.
  • Leaf modifications: Tendril (pea), spine (cactus), phyllode (Acacia), pitcher (Nepenthes), bladder (Utricularia).
  • Adventitious roots: Supporting (maize), storage (sweet potato), parasitic (Cuscuta).
  • Phyllotaxy: Alternate, opposite, whorled.
  • Venation: Reticulate (dicot), parallel (monocot).
  • Stem: Herb, shrub, tree, climber, creeper.
  • Underground stems have nodes, internodes, scale leaves, axillary & terminal buds.
  • Functions of modifications: Storage, support, photosynthesis, protection, reproduction.

2. Tissues in Plants

  • Meristematic tissue: Apical, lateral (cambium), intercalary – cells thin-walled, dense cytoplasm, large nucleus.
  • Permanent tissues: Simple (parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma) & complex (xylem, phloem).
  • Parenchyma: Living, thin-walled, storage, photosynthesis (chlorenchyma), buoyancy (aerenchyma).
  • Collenchyma: Living, thickened at corners, provides flexibility, present in leaf stalks.
  • Sclerenchyma: Dead, thick lignified walls, mechanical support, fibres & sclereids.
  • Xylem: Tracheids, vessels, xylem fibres, xylem parenchyma – conduction of water.
  • Phloem: Sieve tubes, companion cells, phloem parenchyma, phloem fibres – food conduction.
  • Epidermal tissue system: Epidermis, stomata, trichomes, root hairs.
  • Ground tissue system: Cortex, endodermis, pericycle, pith.
  • Vascular tissue system: Open (dicot) & closed (monocot) vascular bundles.

3. Anatomy of Root (Dicot & Monocot)

  • Dicot root: Epiblema (root hairs), cortex, endodermis (Casparian strip), pericycle, 2–6 xylem patches (exarch).
  • Monocot root: Similar but polyarch xylem (many patches), pith present.
  • Root hairs increase surface area for absorption.
  • Endodermis regulates entry of water & ions (Casparian strip).
  • Stele: All tissues inside endodermis.
  • Conjunctive tissue present between xylem & phloem.
  • No secondary growth in monocot roots.
  • Older roots have exodermis & bark.
  • Vascular bundles radial & exarch in roots.
  • Lateral roots arise endogenously from pericycle.

4. Anatomy of Stem & Leaf (Dicot vs Monocot)

  • Dicot stem: Epidermis, cortex, endodermis (starch sheath), pericycle, open vascular bundles in ring.
  • Monocot stem: Sclerenchymatous hypodermis, scattered closed vascular bundles, no cambium.
  • Vascular bundles conjoint, collateral, open (dicot), closed (monocot).
  • Dicot leaf (Dorsiventral): Upper palisade, lower spongy parenchyma, stomata on lower side.
  • Monocot leaf (Isobilateral): Palisade on both sides, stomata equal on both sides.
  • Bulliform cells in monocot leaves help in rolling during drought.
  • Bundle sheath present around vascular bundles.
  • Secondary growth present in dicot stem (cambium), absent in monocot.
  • Stem shows anomalous secondary growth in some monocots (Dracaena).
  • Leaf base, petiole, lamina are parts of leaf.

5. Inflorescence, Flower, Fruit & Seed

  • Inflorescence: Racemose (indefinite growth) – raceme, spike, corymb; Cymose (definite growth).
  • Flower: Complete (all 4 whorls), incomplete, incomplete, bisexual/androgynous, unisexual.
  • Placentation: Marginal, axile, parietal, basal, free central.
  • Aestivation: Valvate, twisted, imbricate, vexillary (papilionaceous).
  • Fruit: True (from ovary) vs false (thalamus), fleshy (drupe, berry) vs dry.
  • Seed: Albuminous (endosperm present – monocot) vs exalbuminous (dicot).
  • Pericarp: Epicarp, mesocarp, endocarp in drupe.
  • Hypogynous (superior ovary), perigynous, epigynous (inferior ovary).
  • Calyx, corolla, androecium, gynoecium are floral whorls.
  • Actinomorphic (radial) vs zygomorphic (bilateral) symmetry.

6. Five NEET Floral Families

  • Malvaceae: China rose – pentamerous, axile placentation, twisted aestivation, monadelphous.
  • Cruciferae (Brassicaceae): Mustard – tetradynamous stamens, parietal placentation.
  • Fabaceae (Leguminosae): Pea – papilionaceous corolla, vexillary aestivation, diadelphous.
  • Solanaceae: Tomato – bicarpellary, axile placentation, swollen placenta.
  • Liliaceae: Onion – trimerous, superior ovary, axile placentation.
  • Floral formula symbols: ⚥, ♂, ♀, K, C, A, G.
  • Fabaceae – marginal placentation, 10 stamens (9+1).
  • Cruciferae – 6 stamens (4+2 tetradynamous).
  • Malvaceae – numerous stamens fused into tube.
  • All are pentamerous except Liliaceae (trimerous).

7. Animal Tissues

  • Epithelial: Squamous, cuboidal, columnar, ciliated, glandular.
  • Connective: Areolar, adipose, skeletal (bone & cartilage), fluid (blood, lymph).
  • Blood: RBC, WBC, platelets, plasma – transport.
  • Cartilage: Hyaline, elastic, calcified, fibrous.
  • Bone: Compact & spongy, Haversian system.
  • Muscular: Skeletal (striated), smooth (non-striated), cardiac.
  • Nervous: Neurons (axon, dendron, myelin sheath, nodes of Ranvier.
  • Compound epithelium: Stratified, keratinized (skin).
  • Areolar tissue present beneath skin – loose connective.
  • Adipose tissue stores fat, acts as insulator.

8. Earthworm (Pheretima) – Morphology & Anatomy

  • Body metamerically segmented, clitellum 14–16 segments.
  • Setae help in locomotion (except first, last & clitellum).
  • Alimentary canal: Mouth → buccal cavity → pharynx → oesophagus → gizzard → intestine → anus.
  • Typhlosole increases absorption area in intestine.
  • Closed circulatory system, blood red due to haemoglobin in plasma.
  • Excretory organ: Nephridia (integumentary, septal, pharyngeal).
  • Nervous system: Nerve ring + ventral nerve cord.
  • Monoecious, cross fertilization, cocoon formation.
  • Segments 10–12: Spermathecae (store spermatozoa).
  • Locomotion by setae & body wall muscles.

9. Cockroach (Periplaneta americana) – Morphology & Anatomy

  • Body divided into head, thorax, abdomen; reddish-brown.
  • Head: Hypognathous, compound eyes, antennae.
  • Thorax: 3 pairs legs, 2 pairs wings (forewings tegmina).
  • Alimentary canal: Foregut (mouth → crop → gizzard), midgut, hindgut.
  • Malpighian tubules (100–150) – excretory organ.
  • Respiration by tracheae (10 pairs spiracles).
  • Open circulation, 13-chambered heart.
  • Nervous system: Supra-oesophageal ganglion (brain), ventral nerve cord.
  • Mosaic vision with ommatidia.
  • Paurometabolous development (nymph → adult).

10. Frog (Rana tigrina) – Morphology & Anatomy

  • Body: Head & trunk, no neck, skin moist & slippery.
  • Forelimbs 4 fingers, hindlimbs 5 toes with web.
  • Digestive: Mouth → buccal cavity → oesophagus → stomach → intestine → cloaca.
  • Liver 3 lobes, gall bladder present.
  • Respiratory: Skin (cutaneous), buccal cavity, lungs (pulmonary).
  • Heart 3-chambered (2 atria + 1 ventricle), RBC nucleated.
  • Brain: Forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain, 10 pairs cranial nerves.
  • Excretory: Kidney (mesonephric), urinary bladder, urea (ureotelic).
  • Male: Vocal sacs, copulatory pad; Female: oviduct.
  • Development: External fertilization, tadpole → frog (metamorphosis).
