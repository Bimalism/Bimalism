MOTION IN A STRAIGHT LINE

Complete Chapter Summary | JEE Physics

1.1 Introduction to Motion

Mechanics is the branch of physics that deals with the motion of objects. It has three branches:

  • Statics: Study of objects at rest
  • Kinematics: Description of motion without considering its cause
  • Dynamics: Study of motion in relation to forces

Rest and Motion

Rest

An object is at rest if its position does not change with respect to its surroundings with time.

Examples: Book on a table, person sitting on a chair

Motion

An object is in motion if its position continuously changes with respect to its surroundings.

Examples: Moving train, flowing water, walking person

1.2 Types of Motion

Based on Path

Rectilinear Motion

Motion along a straight line

Example: Sliding body on an inclined plane

Circular Motion

Motion along a circular path

Example: Stone tied to a string whirled in a circle

Oscillatory Motion

To and fro motion about a fixed point

Example: Simple pendulum

Based on Dimensions

One-Dimensional (1D)

Only one coordinate needed to specify position

Object moves along a straight line

Example: Boy running on a straight road

Two-Dimensional (2D)

Two coordinates needed to specify position

Object moves in a plane

Example: Satellite revolving around Earth

Three-Dimensional (3D)

Three coordinates needed to specify position

Object moves in space

Example: Butterfly flying in a garden

1.3 Point Object

An object is considered a point object if its size is much smaller than the distance it moves in a reasonable time.

Examples

  • A train on a journey of several hundred kilometers
  • Earth for studying its motion around the Sun
  • A railway carriage moving between stations

Non-example: A spinning cricket ball that turns sharply on hitting the ground

1.4 Scalar and Vector Quantities

Scalar Quantities Vector Quantities
Only magnitude required Both magnitude and direction required
Examples: Mass, length, time, speed Examples: Displacement, velocity, acceleration
Follow ordinary algebra Follow vector algebra

1.5 Position, Path Length and Displacement

Position

The point where an object is situated

Determined by coordinate axes

Can be positive or negative

Path Length (Distance)

Total length of path covered by an object

Always positive

Scalar quantity

Displacement

Change in position in a particular direction

Can be positive, negative or zero

Vector quantity

Displacement: Δx = x₂ - x₁
JEE TIP: The magnitude of displacement may or may not be equal to path length. For a course of motion, displacement can be zero but path length is never zero.

1.6 Speed and Velocity

Speed Velocity
Distance covered per unit time Displacement per unit time
Speed = Distance / Time Velocity = Displacement / Time
Scalar quantity Vector quantity
Always positive Can be positive, negative or zero

Types of Speed and Velocity

Uniform Speed/Velocity

Equal distances/displacements covered in equal time intervals

Non-uniform Speed/Velocity

Unequal distances/displacements covered in equal time intervals

Average Speed/Velocity

Total distance/displacement divided by total time

Instantaneous Speed/Velocity

Speed/velocity at a particular instant

Average Speed: vav = Total distance / Total time
Average Velocity: vav = Δx / Δt
Instantaneous Velocity: vi = limΔt→0 (Δx/Δt) = dx/dt

1.7 Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with time.

Acceleration: a = (v - u) / t
Average Acceleration: aav = Δv / Δt
Instantaneous Acceleration: ainst = limΔt→0 (Δv/Δt) = dv/dt

Types of Acceleration

Uniform Acceleration

Equal changes in velocity in equal time intervals

Non-uniform Acceleration

Unequal changes in velocity in equal time intervals

Positive Acceleration

Velocity increases with time

Negative Acceleration (Retardation)

Velocity decreases with time

NOTE: Acceleration can result from a change in speed (magnitude), a change in direction, or both.

1.8 Kinematic Equations for Uniformly Accelerated Motion

1. Velocity-Time Relation: v = u + at
2. Position-Time Relation: s = ut + ½at²
3. Position-Velocity Relation: v² = u² + 2as
Displacement in nth second: sn = u + a/2 (2n - 1)

Example: Car Acceleration

A car starts from rest and accelerates at 2 m/s² for 10 seconds. Find its final velocity and distance covered.

Solution:

u = 0, a = 2 m/s², t = 10 s

v = u + at = 0 + 2×10 = 20 m/s

s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½×2×100 = 100 m

1.9 Motion Under Gravity

When an object is thrown upwards or falls towards Earth, its motion is called motion under gravity.

Acceleration due to gravity (g) = 9.8 m/s² (approximately 10 m/s² for calculations)

Equations of motion under gravity:
v = u ± gt
h = ut ± ½gt²
v² = u² ± 2gh
CONVENTION: For upward motion, g is negative. For downward motion, g is positive.

Example: Ball Thrown Upwards

A ball is thrown vertically upwards with 20 m/s from ground. Find maximum height and time to return.

Solution:

At maximum height, v = 0

v² = u² + 2as ⇒ 0 = 400 - 2×10×h ⇒ h = 20 m

Time to reach max height: v = u + at ⇒ 0 = 20 - 10t ⇒ t = 2 s

Total time of flight = 2 × 2 = 4 s

1.10 Graphical Analysis of Motion

Position-Time Graphs

Interpretation: Slope gives velocity

  • Straight line: Uniform velocity
  • Curved line: Non-uniform velocity
  • Horizontal line: Zero velocity (at rest)

Velocity-Time Graphs

Interpretation:

  • Slope gives acceleration
  • Area under curve gives displacement
  • Straight line: Uniform acceleration
  • Curved line: Non-uniform acceleration
  • Horizontal line: Zero acceleration (uniform velocity)

Acceleration-Time Graphs

Interpretation: Area under curve gives change in velocity

1.11 Relative Motion

Relative velocity is the velocity of one object as observed from another object.

Relative velocity of A with respect to B: vAB = vA - vB

Example: Two Trains

Two trains A and B are moving in the same direction with speeds 72 km/h and 54 km/h respectively. Find the relative velocity of A with respect to B.

Solution:

vA = 72 km/h = 20 m/s

vB = 54 km/h = 15 m/s

vAB = vA - vB = 20 - 15 = 5 m/s

Quick Formula Sheet

Essential Formulas

  • Displacement: Δx = x₂ - x₁
  • Average velocity: vav = Δx / Δt
  • Average speed: vav = Total distance / Total time
  • Instantaneous velocity: v = dx/dt
  • Acceleration: a = dv/dt
  • Kinematic equations: v = u + at, s = ut + ½at², v² = u² + 2as
  • Displacement in nth second: sn = u + a/2 (2n - 1)
  • Relative velocity: vAB = vA - vB
EXAM STRATEGY: Always draw diagrams for motion problems. For graphs, remember slope gives velocity (x-t) or acceleration (v-t), and area gives displacement (v-t). Pay attention to signs (+/-) for direction.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Displacement and Distance

A boy moves from -20 m to +40 m, then turns and moves to -50 m. Find the displacement and distance covered.

Solution:
Distance = Path length = |40 - (-20)| + |(-50) - 40| = 60 + 90 = 150 m
Displacement = Final position - Initial position = (-50) - (-20) = -30 m

Problem 2: Kinematic Equations

A car accelerates from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 5 seconds. Find the acceleration and distance covered.

Solution:
u = 10 m/s, v = 30 m/s, t = 5 s
a = (v - u)/t = (30 - 10)/5 = 4 m/s²
s = ut + ½at² = 10×5 + ½×4×25 = 50 + 50 = 100 m

Problem 3: Motion Under Gravity

A ball is thrown vertically upwards with 25 m/s from a building 50 m high. Find the maximum height reached and time to hit the ground.

Solution:
Maximum height above building: h = u²/(2g) = 625/(2×10) = 31.25 m
Total height = 50 + 31.25 = 81.25 m
Time to reach max height: t₁ = u/g = 25/10 = 2.5 s
Time to fall from max height: s = ½gt² ⇒ 81.25 = ½×10×t₂² ⇒ t₂ = 4.03 s
Total time = t₁ + t₂ = 2.5 + 4.03 = 6.53 s
