1. Electric Current

  • Electric current I = dq/dt (rate of flow of charge)
  • SI unit: Ampere (A), 1 A = 1 C/s
  • Current is a scalar quantity (though has direction)
  • Conventional current: from positive to negative terminal
  • Electron flow: opposite to conventional current
  • Steady current: constant with time (dc)
  • In metals, current is due to flow of free electrons
  • Current density J = I/A (vector), unit: A/m²
  • J = n e v_d (microscopic expression)
  • Current is same in series connection

2. Flow of Charge, Drift Velocity & Mobility

  • Drift velocity v_d = (e E τ)/m (under electric field)
  • Average thermal speed >> drift speed (10⁵ vs 10⁻⁴ m/s)
  • Relaxation time τ ≈ 10⁻¹⁴ s
  • Mobility μ = v_d / E = e τ / m
  • Unit of mobility: m²/V·s
  • Current I = n e A v_d
  • v_d is proportional to applied electric field
  • Drift velocity is independent of time in steady state
  • Higher mobility → better conductor
  • In semiconductors, both electrons and holes contribute

3. Electrical Resistance & Resistivity

  • Ohm’s law: V = I R (for ohmic conductors)
  • Resistance R = ρ L / A
  • Resistivity ρ = m / (n e² τ) (from Drude model)
  • Unit of resistivity: Ω·m
  • Conductivity σ = 1/ρ = n e μ
  • Series: R_eq = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
  • Parallel: 1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + …
  • Carbon resistors have color bands for value & tolerance
  • Resistors are ohmic if I-V graph is straight line through origin
  • Non-ohmic: filament lamp, diode, thermistor

4. Electrical Energy & Power

  • Power P = V I = I²R = V²/R
  • Energy E = P t = V I t (joule)
  • Commercial unit: kWh (1 unit = 3.6 MJ)
  • Heating effect: H = I² R t (Joule’s law)
  • Maximum power transfer when R_load = r (internal resistance)
  • Fuse wire: high resistivity, low melting point
  • Power dissipated is maximum in resistance
  • Battery supplies chemical energy → electrical → heat/light
  • Efficiency of bulb = (light energy/output) × 100%
  • Power is same in series if resistances are equal

5. Temperature Dependence of Resistance

  • R_t = R₀ (1 + α ΔT) for metals (α > 0)
  • α = temperature coefficient of resistance
  • For semiconductors & insulators: α is negative
  • Superconductors: R = 0 below critical temperature
  • Thermistor (NTC): resistance decreases with temperature
  • For alloys (constantan, manganin): α ≈ 0
  • Resistivity ρ also increases with temperature
  • At high T, resistance increases due to more collisions
  • Filament lamp resistance increases when hot
  • Carbon: negative temperature coefficient

6. Cells, EMF, Potential Difference & Internal Resistance

  • EMF (ε): maximum potential difference when I = 0
  • Terminal voltage V = ε – I r (during discharge)
  • V = ε + I r (during charging)
  • Internal resistance r causes voltage drop
  • Primary cells: not rechargeable (Daniel, Leclanché)
  • Secondary cells: rechargeable (lead-acid, Li-ion)
  • Current I = ε / (R + r)
  • Power to load maximum when R = r
  • Open circuit: V = ε
  • Short circuit: I_max = ε / r

7. Kirchhoff’s Laws & Applications

  • KCL: Σ I_in = Σ I_out at junction (charge conservation)
  • KVL: Algebraic sum of ΔV = 0 in closed loop
  • Sign convention: +ve when moving from – to + in battery
  • Used to solve complex circuits
  • KCL based on conservation of charge
  • KVL based on conservation of energy
  • Number of independent equations = number of loops
  • Always assign current direction initially
  • Works for both dc and ac (instantaneous values)
  • Symmetry often reduces number of variables

8. Wheatstone Bridge & Metre Bridge

  • Wheatstone bridge balanced when P/Q = R/S
  • At balance, no current through galvanometer
  • Condition: P/Q = R/S (ratio arms equal)
  • Metre bridge is modified Wheatstone bridge
  • In metre bridge: R/S = l / (100 – l)
  • Unknown resistance X = R × (l / (100 – l))
  • Jockey tapped to get null point
  • End corrections may be applied for accuracy
  • Sensitive to small change in resistance
  • Used to measure medium range resistance

9. Potentiometer – Principle & Sensitivity

  • Potentiometer works on null deflection method
  • Fall of potential per unit length = k = ε / L
  • At balance: ε = k l (no current from test cell)
  • Highly sensitive, accurate, no energy loss at null
  • Longer wire → higher sensitivity
  • Thicker wire → lower resistance → higher current → better
  • Uniform cross-section wire is essential
  • Driver cell EMF > test cell EMF
  • Positive terminal of driver and test cell connected together
  • Null point shifts if polarity reversed

10. Potentiometer – Measurement of Internal Resistance

  • Internal resistance r = (l₁ – l₂)/l₂ × R
  • l₁ = balancing length with high resistance R (open)
  • l₂ = balancing length when R is shorted (closed)
  • With R: ε = k l₁
  • With switch closed: V = ε – I r = k l₂
  • Also used to compare EMFs: ε₁/ε₂ = l₁/l₂
  • Can measure very small EMF (thermocouple)
  • Can calibrate voltmeter and ammeter
  • More accurate than voltmeter (no current drawn)
  • Standard cell (Weston cell) used for calibration
