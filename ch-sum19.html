1. General Introduction and Importance of Biomolecules

  • Biomolecules are organic compounds present in living organisms.
  • Major classes: carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, nucleic acids, vitamins and hormones.
  • They are mostly made up of C, H, O, N, P, S.
  • Carbohydrates and lipids mainly provide energy.
  • Proteins act as enzymes, hormones, antibodies, structural components.
  • Nucleic acids (DNA & RNA) carry genetic information.
  • Vitamins act as co-enzymes or precursors of co-enzymes.
  • Hormones act as chemical messengers.
  • Biomolecules are mostly polymeric (except lipids and vitamins).
  • They show stereoisomerism (D/L and α/β forms are common).

2. Carbohydrates

  • Carbohydrates are optically active polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones or compounds that give these on hydrolysis.
  • General formula: Cx(H₂O)y (hydrates of carbon).
  • Classification: Monosaccharides, Oligosaccharides, Polysaccharides.
  • Monosaccharides classified as aldose (aldehyde) or ketose (ketone).
  • Glucose (aldohexose) and fructose (ketohexose) are most important monosaccharides.
  • Disaccharides: Sucrose (glucose + fructose), Lactose (glucose + galactose), Maltose (glucose + glucose).
  • Sucrose is non-reducing, lactose & maltose are reducing sugars.
  • Polysaccharides: Starch (amylose + amylopectin), Cellulose, Glycogen (animal starch).
  • Glucose shows mutarotation, open chain ↔ cyclic (α & β) forms.
  • Important tests: Molisch test (all carbs), Fehling/ Benedict/ Tollens test (reducing sugars).

3. Proteins

  • Proteins are polymers of α-amino acids linked by peptide bonds (–CO–NH–).
  • 20 standard α-amino acids in proteins; all except glycine are chiral.
  • Peptide bond has partial double bond character (resonance), hence planar.
  • Primary structure: sequence of amino acids.
  • Secondary structure: α-helix & β-pleated sheet held by H-bonds.
  • Tertiary: 3D folding due to H-bond, ionic, disulfide, hydrophobic interactions.
  • Quaternary: assembly of multiple polypeptide chains (e.g., haemoglobin).
  • Denaturation: loss of 2°, 3°, 4° structure by heat, pH, urea etc. (biological activity lost).
  • Enzymes are biological catalysts, highly specific, work on lock-and-key or induced-fit model.
  • Co-factors: non-protein part; co-enzymes are organic co-factors (many vitamins).

4. Vitamins – Classification and Functions

  • Vitamins are accessory food factors required in small amounts.
  • Classification: Fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) and Water-soluble (B-complex & C).
  • Vitamin A (retinol): vision, deficiency → night blindness, xerophthalmia.
  • Vitamin D (calciferol): Ca & P absorption, deficiency → rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults).
  • Vitamin E (tocopherol): antioxidant, protects cell membrane.
  • Vitamin K (phylloquinone): blood clotting (synthesis of prothrombin).
  • Vitamin B1 (thiamine): co-enzyme in decarboxylation, deficiency → beriberi.
  • Vitamin B2 (riboflavin): part of FAD/FMN, deficiency → cheilosis, glossitis.
  • Vitamin B12 (cyanocobalamin): RBC formation, deficiency → pernicious anaemia.
  • Vitamin C (ascorbic acid): collagen synthesis, antioxidant, deficiency → scurvy.

5. Nucleic Acids (DNA & RNA)

  • Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides.
  • Nucleotide = nitrogenous base + pentose sugar + phosphate.
  • Bases: Purines (Adenine, Guanine), Pyrimidines (Cytosine, Thymine, Uracil).
  • DNA: deoxyribose sugar, bases A, T, G, C; double helix (Watson-Crick model).
  • RNA: ribose sugar, bases A, U, G, C; single stranded (mRNA, tRNA, rRNA).
  • Base pairing: A=T (2 H-bonds), G≡C (3 H-bonds) in DNA; A=U in RNA.
  • DNA stores genetic information, RNA helps in protein synthesis.
  • Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein.
  • Denaturation of DNA: separation of strands by heat/pH (hyperchromic effect).
  • Important test: Dische diphenylamine test gives blue colour with DNA.

6. Hormones (General Introduction)

  • Hormones are chemical messengers secreted by endocrine glands.
  • Act in very low concentration at distant target organs.
  • Chemically: steroid (e.g., cortisol, testosterone), peptide (insulin, glucagon), amino acid derivatives (thyroxine, adrenaline).
  • Insulin: peptide hormone, decreases blood glucose (diabetes on deficiency).
  • Glucagon: increases blood glucose.
  • Thyroxine (T₃, T₄): regulates metabolism, deficiency → cretinism (child), myxoedema (adult).
  • Adrenaline (epinephrine): fight-or-flight hormone.
  • Sex hormones: Testosterone (male), Estrogen & Progesterone (female).
  • Hormones do not catalyse reactions but regulate them.
  • Feedback mechanism controls hormone secretion.
