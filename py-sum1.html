Need of Measurement

Why do we measure?
To express laws of physics quantitatively
To compare unknown quantity with standard
For prediction, verification and technology development
Example: Without measuring speed, we cannot design cars or rockets
Measurement is the foundation of all empirical science
Essential for trade, construction, medicine, sports
Enables reproducibility of experiments globally
Forms basis for international standards and agreements
Physical Quantity = n × u
Q = n × u

If unit u₁ becomes u₂ = k u₁ → n₂ = n₁/k

Example: 1 kg = 1000 g → 5 kg = 5000 g

Unit Conversion Derivation:

Let Q = n₁ u₁ = n₂ u₂

Given u₂ = k u₁

Then n₁ u₁ = n₂ (k u₁)

n₁ = k n₂ → n₂ = n₁/k

Latest SI Units (2019 Definitions)

2019 Redefinition: All SI units now defined by fundamental constants rather than physical artifacts.
Metre (m)

Distance travelled by light in vacuum in 1/299,792,458 second

Based on: Speed of light c = 299,792,458 m/s (exact)

Old definition: 1 m = distance between two marks on Pt-Ir bar

Kilogram (kg)

Based on Planck's constant h = 6.62607015 × 10⁻³⁴ J s

Defined via Kibble balance or X-ray crystal density

Old definition: Mass of International Prototype Kilogram (IPK)

Second (s)

9192631770 periods of radiation of Cesium-133 atom

Based on: Hyperfine transition frequency of Cs-133

Accuracy: 1 part in 10¹⁶ (loses 1 second in 300 million years)

Ampere (A)

Based on elementary charge e = 1.602176634 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

1 A = flow of 1/(1.602176634×10⁻¹⁹) elementary charges per second

Old definition: Force between two parallel wires

Kelvin (K)

Based on Boltzmann constant k = 1.380649 × 10⁻²³ J/K

Triple point of water now 273.16 K exactly (not definition)

Mole (mol)

Based on Avogadro constant Nₐ = 6.02214076 × 10²³ mol⁻¹

1 mole contains exactly Nₐ elementary entities

Candela (cd)

Based on luminous efficacy K_cd = 683 lm/W at 540 THz

Monochromatic radiation at 540×10¹² Hz

Error Formulas & Calculations
Absolute error: Δa = |aᵢ - ā|
Mean absolute error: Δā = (Σ|aᵢ - ā|)/n
Relative error: Δā/ā
Percentage error: (Δā/ā) × 100%

Combination of Errors:

Addition/Subtraction: Z = A ± B → ΔZ = ΔA + ΔB

Multiplication: Z = A × B → ΔZ/Z = ΔA/A + ΔB/B

Division: Z = A/B → ΔZ/Z = ΔA/A + ΔB/B

Power: Z = Aⁿ → ΔZ/Z = n(ΔA/A)

General function: Z = f(A,B) → ΔZ = |∂f/∂A|ΔA + |∂f/∂B|ΔB

Statistical Treatment of Errors
Mean: ā = (Σaᵢ)/n
Variance: σ² = Σ(aᵢ - ā)²/(n-1)
Standard deviation: σ = √variance
Standard error of mean: σ/√n
Gaussian/Normal distribution: Bell curve
68-95-99.7 rule for normal distribution
Confidence intervals: 95% CI = ā ± 1.96σ/√n
Weighted mean: When measurements have different precision
χ² (Chi-squared) test: Goodness of fit
Least squares fitting: For linear relationships

Significant Figures (30+ Examples)

Golden Rules:
1. All non-zero digits are significant
2. Zeros between significant digits are significant
3. Trailing zeros after decimal are significant
4. Leading zeros are never significant
5. Exact numbers (counting, defined constants) have infinite significant figures

Operations with Significant Figures
Addition/Subtraction: Least decimal places
Multiplication/Division: Least significant figures
Logarithms: Decimal places in result = sig figs in input
Trig functions: Match input precision
Mixed operations: Apply step-by-step

Examples:

Addition: 12.11 (2 decimal) + 3.2 (1 decimal) = 15.3 (1 decimal)

Multiplication: 2.1 (2 sf) × 3.00 (3 sf) = 6.3 (2 sf)

Division: 10.0 (3 sf) ÷ 2.0 (2 sf) = 5.0 (2 sf)

Mixed: (12.1 + 3.28) × 2.0 = (15.4) × 2.0 = 31 (2 sf)

Rounding Rules
If digit to be dropped < 5: Leave preceding digit unchanged
If digit to be dropped > 5: Increase preceding digit by 1
If digit to be dropped = 5: Make preceding digit even
Round only at final answer, not intermediate steps
For scientific notation: Round coefficient, adjust exponent

Rounding Examples:

3.14159 to 3 sf → 3.14 (next digit 1 < 5)

2.71828 to 3 sf → 2.72 (next digit 8 > 5)

4.65 to 2 sf → 4.6 (next digit 5, make preceding even)

4.75 to 2 sf → 4.8 (next digit 5, make preceding even)

123456 to 3 sf → 1.23 × 10⁵

Applications of Dimensional Analysis
Check equation consistency: Both sides same dimensions
Derive formulas: Time period of pendulum, etc.
Unit conversion: n₂ = n₁ (M₁/M₂)ᵃ (L₁/L₂)ᵇ (T₁/T₂)ᶜ
Find dimensions of constants: G, h, k, ε₀, μ₀
Predict relationship between variables
Identify dimensionless quantities: Reynolds number, etc.
Scale modeling: Similitude in engineering
Buckingham π theorem: For complex systems

Dimensional Formula Derivation:

Force: F = ma → [F] = [M][LT⁻²] = [MLT⁻²]

Energy: E = Fd → [E] = [MLT⁻²][L] = [ML²T⁻²]

Pressure: P = F/A → [P] = [MLT⁻²]/[L²] = [ML⁻¹T⁻²]

Gravitational constant: F = Gm₁m₂/r²

[MLT⁻²] = [G][M][M]/[L²] → [G] = [M⁻¹L³T⁻²]

Limitations of Dimensional Analysis
Cannot determine dimensionless constants
Cannot distinguish between quantities with same dimensions
Cannot derive formulas involving trigonometric, exponential functions
Cannot determine if a physical quantity is scalar or vector
Cannot derive exact form when multiple combinations possible
Cannot account for dimensionless parameters
Doesn't work for equations with more than 3 fundamental quantities without additional info

Examples of Same Dimensions:

Work & Torque: Both [ML²T⁻²]

Frequency & Angular velocity: Both [T⁻¹]

Surface tension & Spring constant: Both [MT⁻²]

Impulse & Linear momentum: Both [MLT⁻¹]
