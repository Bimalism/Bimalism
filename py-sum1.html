Need of Measurement

Why do we measure?
  • To express laws of physics quantitatively
  • To compare unknown quantity with standard
  • For prediction, verification and technology
  • Example: Without measuring speed, we cannot design cars or rockets
  • Measurement is the foundation of all science
  • Helps in trade, construction, medicine, sports
Physical Quantity = n × u
Q = n × u

If unit u₁ becomes u₂ = k u₁ → n₂ = n₁/k

Example: 1 kg = 1000 g → 5 kg = 5000 g

Measurement of Length

Small Distances
  • Atomic size (10⁻¹⁰ m) → X-ray diffraction
  • Nuclear size (10⁻¹⁵ m) → Particle accelerators
  • Vernier Callipers LC = 0.01 cm
  • Screw Gauge LC = 0.001 cm
Large Distances
  • Parallax method: d = b / θ (b = baseline, θ in radian)
  • Triangulation method for height of mountain
  • Radar: distance = (c × t)/2
  • LASER ranging to Moon: 3.84 × 10⁸ m
  • 1 AU = 1.496 × 10¹¹ m
  • 1 light year = 9.46 × 10¹⁵ m
  • 1 parsec = 3.26 light year

Mass & Time Measurement

Mass
  • Lab: Physical/Electronic balance
  • Planet mass → Kepler’s law
  • Sun mass → Gravitational force on Earth
  • Electron mass = 9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg
  • Inertial mass = Gravitational mass (Equivalence)
Time
  • Quartz clock: 1 part in 10⁸
  • Atomic clock (Cs-133): 1 second in 300 million years
  • Leap second added to UTC
  • 1 day = 86400 seconds

Error Formulas
Absolute error Δa = |measured − true|
Mean absolute error = average of Δaᵢ
Relative error = Δa / a_mean
Percentage error = Relative error × 100%

Addition: Δz = Δx + Δy

Multiplication: Δz/z = Δx/x + Δy/y

Power: Δz/z = n Δx/x

Example: R = 10.2 ± 0.2 Ω, L = 5.0 ± 0.1 cm

Significant Figures (15 Examples)

Rules: All non-zero digits significant • Zeros between significant • Trailing zeros after decimal significant • Leading zeros never significant
Examples
  • 2500 → 2 sig figs
  • 2500. → 4 sig figs
  • 2.500 → 4 sig figs
  • 0.0025 → 2 sig figs
  • 5.0 × 10³ → 2 sig figs
  • Addition: 12.1 + 3.28 = 15.4 (1 decimal)
  • Multiplication: 2.1 × 3.00 = 6.3 (2 sig figs)
