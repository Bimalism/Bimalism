1. Cell Theory & Cell as Basic Unit of Life

  • Cell Theory (Schleiden & Schwann, 1838–39): All organisms are made of cells; cell is basic unit of life.
  • Virchow (1855): Omnis cellula-e cellula (every cell arises from pre-existing cell).
  • Exceptions to cell theory: Viruses, viroids (acellular); coenocytic organisms (no cell walls).
  • Cell is the smallest unit capable of independent existence and performing all life functions.
  • Unicellular organisms (Amoeba) perform all functions in one cell.
  • Cell size ranges from 0.1 μm (Mycoplasma) to 15 cm (ostrich egg).
  • Shape: Spherical, cuboidal, polygonal, spindle, irregular (nerve cell).
  • Number: Unicellular (bacteria) to multicellular (humans ~10¹³ cells).
  • All metabolic reactions occur inside cell.
  • Cell is totipotent in plants – can regenerate whole plant.

2. Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cell

  • Prokaryotic: No nuclear membrane, 70S ribosomes, circular DNA, no membrane-bound organelles.
  • Eukaryotic: True nucleus, 80S ribosomes, linear DNA, membrane-bound organelles present.
  • Prokaryotes: Cell wall peptidoglycan, mesosome, no mitosis/meiosis.
  • Eukaryotes: Cell wall cellulose/chitin/fungus, mitosis & meiosis present.
  • Size: Prokaryotic 1–10 μm, eukaryotic 10–100 μm.
  • Chromosomes: Single circular (nucleoid), multiple linear.
  • Examples Prokaryotic: Bacteria, Cyanobacteria, Mycoplasma.
  • Examples Eukaryotic: Plants, animals, fungi, protists.
  • Flagella in prokaryotes: 9+2 absent, smaller.
  • Cell division in prokaryotes: Binary fission.

3. Plant Cell vs Animal Cell

  • Plant cell: Cell wall present, plastids present, large central vacuole, no centrioles.
  • Animal cell: No cell wall, no plastids, small vacuoles, centrioles present.
  • Plant cell: Chloroplasts for photosynthesis, glyoxysomes/peroxisomes present.
  • Animal cell: Lysosomes numerous, centrosome with centrioles.
  • Shape: Plant cell rectangular/fixed, animal cell irregular/round.
  • Cytokinesis in plant: Cell plate formation, in animal: cleavage furrow.
  • Storage: Starch in plants, glycogen in animals.
  • Plant cell larger than animal cell generally.
  • Plasmodesmata in plants, gap junctions in animals.
  • Plant cell totipotent, animal cells generally not.

4. Cell Envelope & Cell Membrane

  • Cell envelope (prokaryotes): Glycocalyx → cell wall → plasma membrane.
  • Plasma membrane: Singer-Nicolson fluid mosaic model (1972).
  • Fluid mosaic: Phospholipid bilayer with floating proteins.
  • Functions: Selective permeability, transport, cell signaling.
  • Transport: Passive (diffusion, osmosis), active (Na⁺/K⁺ pump), bulk (endocytosis/exocytosis).
  • Cell wall (plants): Cellulose, hemicellulose, pectin; middle lamella Ca & Mg pectate.
  • Plasmodesmata: Cytoplasmic connections between adjacent plant cells.
  • Cell membrane 7.5 nm thick, quasi-fluid nature.
  • Glycoproteins & glycolipids act as recognition sites.
  • Membrane proteins: Integral & peripheral.

5. Cell Wall & Major Cell Organelles – I

  • Cell wall: Primary (cellulose), secondary (lignin), plasmodesmata present.
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): Rough (RER) – protein synthesis, Smooth (SER) – lipid & steroid synthesis.
  • Golgi apparatus: Cis & trans face, formation of lysosomes & glycoproteins.
  • Lysosomes: Suicidal bags, contain hydrolases, active at pH 5.
  • Vacuoles: Tonoplast membrane, store pigments (anthocyanin), waste, water (90% in plant cell).
  • Microbodies: Peroxisomes (H₂O₂ metabolism), glyoxysomes (glyoxylate cycle in seeds).
  • Ribosomes: 70S (prokaryotes, chloroplast, mitochondria), 80S (eukaryotic cytoplasm).
  • Ribosomes attached to RER form polysomes.
  • Cytoplasm + nucleus = protoplasm.
  • Cell sap in vacuole maintains turgidity.

6. Endomembrane System

  • Endomembrane system: ER, Golgi, lysosomes, vacuoles – coordinate functioning.
  • RER synthesizes proteins → transported to Golgi.
  • Golgi modifies, sorts, packages proteins & lipids.
  • Golgi forms primary lysosomes.
  • Secretory proteins packed into vesicles by Golgi.
  • Vacuoles formed by fusion of vesicles from ER & Golgi.
  • Lysosomes formed by budding from trans face of Golgi.
  • Endomembrane system absent in mature RBCs.
  • SER detoxifies drugs in liver cells.
  • Golgi discovered by Camillo Golgi (1898).

7. Mitochondria, Plastids, Ribosomes

  • Mitochondria: Double membrane, cristae (70S, circular DNA), powerhouse of cell.
  • Oxysomes (F₀-F₁ particles) on inner membrane – ATP synthesis.
  • Plastids: Chloroplast (grana, stroma, 70S), chromoplast (coloured), leucoplast (storage).
  • Chloroplast: Site of photosynthesis, thylakoids contain chlorophyll.
  • Ribosomes: Protein factories, made of rRNA & proteins.
  • Mitochondria & chloroplasts are semi-autonomous (own DNA & ribosomes).
  • Number of mitochondria depends on cell activity (up to thousands).
  • Chloroplast DNA called cpDNA, mitochondrial DNA mtDNA.
  • Ribosomes attached to RER synthesize secretory proteins.
  • 70S ribosomes in prokaryotes, plastids, mitochondria.

8. Cytoskeleton, Cilia, Flagella, Centrioles

  • Cytoskeleton: Microtubules (tubulin), microfilaments (actin), intermediate filaments.
  • Functions: Shape, mechanical support, intracellular transport, cell division.
  • Cilia & flagella: 9+2 arrangement of microtubules, basal body 9+0.
  • Dynein arms cause sliding of microtubules → bending movement.
  • Centrioles: 9+0, form spindle in animal cells, basal bodies.
  • Centrosome: Two centrioles + pericentriolar material.
  • Cilia in respiratory tract, flagella in sperm.
  • Microtubules made of α & β tubulin dimers.
  • Cytoskeleton absent in prokaryotes.
  • Aster formation during mitosis by centrioles.

9. Nucleus & Biomolecules

  • Nucleus: Nuclear envelope (double), nuclear pores, nucleoplasm, chromatin, nucleolus.
  • Nucleolus: Site of rRNA synthesis & ribosome assembly.
  • Chromatin: DNA + histone proteins, condenses into chromosomes during division.
  • Nuclear lamina supports nuclear envelope.
  • Proteins: Made of amino acids, primary to quaternary structure, peptide bond.
  • Carbohydrates: Monosaccharides (glucose), storage (starch, glycogen), structural (cellulose).
  • Lipids: Phospholipids, triglycerides, steroids (cholesterol).
  • Nucleic acids: DNA (deoxyribose, A,T,C,G), RNA (ribose, A,U,C,G).
  • DNA double helix (Watson & Crick), antiparallel strands.
  • Enzymes are mostly proteins, few RNA (ribozymes).

10. Enzymes & Cell Cycle (Mitosis + Meiosis)

  • Enzymes: Biological catalysts, lower activation energy, highly specific.
  • Active site, induced fit model (Koshland), lock & key (Fischer).
  • Factors affecting: Temperature (optimum 37°C), pH, substrate concentration.
  • Classification: Oxidoreductase, transferase, hydrolase, lyase, isomerase, ligase.
  • Co-factors: Coenzymes (NAD), prosthetic groups (haem), metal ions.
  • Cell cycle: G₁ → S → G₂ → M (mitosis) → cytokinesis.
  • Mitosis: Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase, 2n → 2n.
  • Meiosis: Meiosis I (reductional) – 2n → n, Meiosis II (equational).
  • Significance Mitosis: Growth, repair; Meiosis: Gamete formation, variation.
  • Crossing over in pachytene of Prophase I → genetic recombination.
