1. Alpha-Particle Scattering Experiment (1909)

17 Key Points
  • 1Geiger-Marsden experiment directed by Rutherford
  • 2Alpha particles (He²⁺ nuclei) from radium source
  • 3Thin gold foil (≈ 0.00004 cm) as target
  • 4Most α-particles passed straight through (≈99.9%)
  • 5Few deflected by moderate angles (0.1°-90°)
  • 6Very few (1 in 20,000) scattered back (>90°)
  • 7Conclusion: Positive charge concentrated in tiny nucleus
  • 8Distance of closest approach: r₀ ≈ 10⁻¹⁴ m
  • 9Impact parameter: b = (Ze² cot(θ/2))/(4πε₀ K)
  • 10Smaller impact parameter → larger scattering angle
  • 11Scattering follows Coulomb's law (inverse square)
  • 12Fraction scattered ∝ 1/sin⁴(θ/2) (Rutherford formula)
  • 13Disproved Thomson's plum pudding model
  • 14Established nuclear model of the atom
  • 15Nucleus contains >99.9% of atomic mass
  • 16Electrons occupy most of atomic volume
  • 17Foundation for modern atomic physics

2. Rutherford Atomic Model (1911)

16 Key Points
  • 1Tiny, massive, positively charged nucleus at center
  • 2Nucleus size ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ m (femtometer scale)
  • 3Atomic size ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m (angstrom scale)
  • 4Electrons revolve around nucleus in circular orbits
  • 5Centrifugal force balances Coulomb attraction
  • 6Failed to explain atomic stability (classical EM)
  • 7Accelerated electrons should radiate continuously
  • 8Energy loss would cause spiral collapse into nucleus
  • 9Could not explain discrete line spectra
  • 10Should produce continuous emission spectrum
  • 11Successfully explained large-angle scattering
  • 12Correctly predicted existence of nucleus
  • 13Violated Maxwell's electromagnetic theory
  • 14Laid groundwork for Bohr's quantum model
  • 15Electrostatic model (no strong nuclear force)
  • 16Planetary model analogy (sun and planets)

3. Bohr's Model of Hydrogen Atom (1913)

18 Key Points
  • 1Electrons revolve in stationary circular orbits
  • 2Angular momentum quantized: L = nħ = nh/(2π)
  • 3n = principal quantum number (1, 2, 3...)
  • 4No radiation emitted in stationary orbits
  • 5Radiation occurs during quantum jumps between orbits
  • 6Centripetal force: mv²/r = Ze²/(4πε₀r²)
  • 7Radius of nth orbit: rₙ = n²a₀/Z (a₀ = 0.529 Å)
  • 8Bohr radius a₀ = 4πε₀ħ²/(m_e e²) = 0.529 × 10⁻¹⁰ m
  • 9Velocity: vₙ = αcZ/n (α = fine structure constant ≈ 1/137)
  • 10Frequency condition: hν = |E_f - E_i|
  • 11Explained stability of hydrogen atom
  • 12Successfully predicted hydrogen spectrum
  • 13Applicable to hydrogen-like ions (He⁺, Li²⁺)
  • 14Limitations: Couldn't explain fine structure
  • 15Couldn't explain Zeeman/Stark effects
  • 16Not applicable to multi-electron atoms
  • 17Bridge between classical and quantum physics
  • 18Nobel Prize to Bohr in 1922

4. Energy Levels in Bohr Model

17 Key Points
  • 1Energy of nth level: Eₙ = -13.6 Z²/n² eV
  • 2Ground state (n=1): E₁ = -13.6 eV for hydrogen
  • 3Energy inversely proportional to n²
  • 4Negative energy indicates bound state
  • 5n = ∞ corresponds to E = 0 (ionization limit)
  • 6Ionization energy = |E₁| = 13.6 eV for H
  • 7Kinetic energy: Kₙ = |Eₙ| = +13.6 Z²/n² eV
  • 8Potential energy: Uₙ = 2Eₙ = -27.2 Z²/n² eV
  • 9U = 2E, K = -E (virial theorem for 1/r potential)
  • 10Energy difference ΔE = hν = hc/λ
  • 11Binding energy = |Eₙ| (energy needed to remove electron)
  • 12Energy levels become closer as n increases
  • 13Energy in joules: Eₙ = -2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ Z²/n² J
  • 14For He⁺ (Z=2): Eₙ = -54.4/n² eV
  • 15For Li²⁺ (Z=3): Eₙ = -122.4/n² eV
  • 16Rydberg constant R = 13.6 eV/hc
  • 17Energy level diagram shows allowed transitions

5. Hydrogen Spectrum Series

16 Key Points
  • 1Rydberg formula: 1/λ = R(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²)
  • 2R = Rydberg constant = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹
  • 3Lyman series: UV, n₁=1, n₂≥2
  • 4Balmer series: Visible, n₁=2, n₂≥3
  • 5Paschen series: IR, n₁=3, n₂≥4
  • 6Brackett series: IR, n₁=4, n₂≥5
  • 7Pfund series: IR, n₁=5, n₂≥6
  • 8Humphreys series: IR, n₁=6, n₂≥7
  • 9Series limit (n₂→∞): λ_limit = n₁²/R
  • 10H-alpha line: 656.3 nm (red, Balmer n=3→2)
  • 11H-beta: 486.1 nm (blue-green, n=4→2)
  • 12H-gamma: 434.1 nm (violet, n=5→2)
  • 13First Lyman line: 121.6 nm (UV)
  • 14Perfect match with Bohr model predictions
  • 15R = m_e e⁴/(8ε₀² h³ c) (theoretical expression)
  • 16Used in astronomy to identify hydrogen in stars

6. Isotopes, Isobars & Isotones

15 Key Points
  • 1Isotopes: Same Z, different A (₁H¹, ₁H², ₁H³)
  • 2Isobars: Same A, different Z (₆C¹⁴, ₇N¹⁴)
  • 3Isotones: Same N (neutrons), different Z (₆C¹³, ₇N¹⁴)
  • 4Isomers: Same A and Z, different nuclear states
  • 5Isotopes have identical chemical properties
  • 6Isotopes differ in physical properties (density, radioactivity)
  • 7Hydrogen isotopes: Protium (¹H), Deuterium (²H), Tritium (³H)
  • 8Carbon dating uses C-14 (T₁/₂=5730 years)
  • 9Uranium isotopes: U-235 (0.7%), U-238 (99.3%)
  • 10Stable isotopes mostly even-even nuclei
  • 11Magic numbers: 2,8,20,28,50,82,126 (extra stability)
  • 12Isotope separation by mass spectrometry
  • 13Isotopes used in medicine (Tc-99m, I-131)
  • 14Isotopes used in industrial tracers
  • 15N/Z ratio increases with atomic number

7. Radioactive Decay Types

18 Key Points
  • 1Alpha decay: ₂He⁴ nucleus emission
  • 2α-decay: A→A-4, Z→Z-2
  • 3β⁻ decay: neutron → proton + electron + anti-neutrino
  • 4β⁻ decay: Z→Z+1, A unchanged
  • 5β⁺ decay: proton → neutron + positron + neutrino
  • 6β⁺ decay: Z→Z-1, A unchanged
  • 7Electron capture: proton + electron → neutron + neutrino
  • 8Gamma decay: EM radiation from excited nucleus
  • 9γ-decay: No change in A or Z
  • 10α particles: +2e charge, 4-8 MeV energy
  • 11β particles: Continuous energy spectrum
  • 12γ rays: Discrete energies, high penetration
  • 13Penetration: α stopped by paper, β by Al, γ by Pb
  • 14Ionizing power: α > β > γ
  • 15Discovered by Becquerel (1896) with uranium salts
  • 16Natural radioactivity for Z>82 (lead)
  • 17Artificial radioactivity induced by bombardment
  • 18Internal conversion: γ energy transfers to electron

8. Radioactive Decay Law & Half-Life

16 Key Points
  • 1Decay law: N = N₀e^{-λt} (exponential decay)
  • 2Activity: A = -dN/dt = λN = A₀e^{-λt}
  • 3Decay constant λ: probability per unit time
  • 4Half-life: T₁/₂ = ln2/λ ≈ 0.693/λ
  • 5Mean life: τ = 1/λ = T₁/₂/ln2 ≈ 1.44 T₁/₂
  • 6After n half-lives: N = N₀/2ⁿ
  • 7Unit of activity: Becquerel (Bq) = 1 decay/s
  • 8Curie (Ci) = 3.7 × 10¹⁰ Bq (activity of 1g Ra-226)
  • 9λ independent of temperature, pressure, chemical state
  • 10Carbon-14 dating: T₁/₂ = 5730 years
  • 11Uranium-238: T₁/₂ = 4.5 × 10⁹ years
  • 12Iodine-131: T₁/₂ = 8 days (medical use)
  • 13Successive decays: Bateman equations
  • 14Secular equilibrium when T₁(parent) >> T₁/₂(daughter)
  • 15Transient equilibrium when T₁/₂(parent) > T₁/₂(daughter)
  • 16Geiger-Muller counter measures activity

9. Mass-Energy Equivalence & Nuclear Mass

15 Key Points
  • 1Einstein's equation: E = mc² (1905)
  • 21 atomic mass unit (u) = 1.66054 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
  • 31 u = 931.5 MeV/c² (energy equivalent)
  • 4Proton mass: 1.007276 u = 938.272 MeV/c²
  • 5Neutron mass: 1.008665 u = 939.565 MeV/c²
  • 6Electron mass: 0.00054858 u = 0.511 MeV/c²
  • 7Mass defect Δm = (Zm_p + Nm_n) - M_nucleus
  • 8Binding energy B = Δm c²
  • 9Q-value = (initial mass - final mass)c²
  • 10Positive Q-value → exothermic reaction
  • 11Negative Q-value → endothermic reaction
  • 12Pair production: γ → e⁺ + e⁻ (E_γ > 1.022 MeV)
  • 13Annihilation: e⁺ + e⁻ → 2γ (each 0.511 MeV)
  • 14Mass-energy conserved in nuclear reactions
  • 15Verified in nuclear reactors and particle accelerators

10. Binding Energy per Nucleon Curve

16 Key Points
  • 1B.E./A maximum at A ≈ 56 (iron peak)
  • 2Fe-56 most stable: B.E./A ≈ 8.8 MeV
  • 3Rapid rise for light nuclei (A < 30)
  • 4Plateau region A = 40-120 (B.E./A ≈ 8.5 MeV)
  • 5Slow decrease for heavy nuclei (A > 120)
  • 6U-235: B.E./A ≈ 7.6 MeV
  • 7H-2: B.E./A ≈ 1.1 MeV
  • 8He-4 exceptionally stable: B.E./A ≈ 7.1 MeV
  • 9Even-even nuclei more stable than odd-odd
  • 10Fission: Heavy → medium (energy release)
  • 11Fusion: Light → medium (up to Fe, energy release)
  • 12Nuclear stability favored by N ≈ Z for light nuclei
  • 13For heavy nuclei: N > Z (Coulomb repulsion)
  • 14Semi-empirical mass formula (Weizsäcker)
  • 15Curve explains energy sources of stars
  • 16Basis for nuclear power and weapons

11. Nuclear Fission & Fusion Processes

17 Key Points
  • 1Fission: Heavy nucleus splits into medium + neutrons
  • 2U-235 + n → Ba-141 + Kr-92 + 3n + ~200 MeV
  • 3Chain reaction: n produced → causes more fission
  • 4Critical mass: Minimum for sustained chain reaction
  • 5Moderator (graphite, D₂O) slows neutrons
  • 6Control rods (Cd, B) absorb excess neutrons
  • 7Atom bomb: Uncontrolled chain reaction
  • 8Nuclear reactor: Controlled chain reaction
  • 9Fusion: Light nuclei combine to form heavier
  • 10Deuterium-tritium: ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + 17.6 MeV
  • 11Proton-proton chain in Sun: 4¹H → ⁴He + 2e⁺ + 2ν + 26.7 MeV
  • 12Fusion requires high temperature (≈10⁷ K) for Coulomb barrier
  • 13Plasma confinement needed (magnetic or inertial)
  • 14H-bomb: Uncontrolled fusion (thermonuclear)
  • 15ITER: International experimental fusion reactor
  • 16Cold fusion: Unverified at room temperature
  • 17Fission products radioactive, fusion products stable

12. Nuclear Reactions & Applications

15 Key Points
  • 1First artificial transmutation: Rutherford (1919)
  • 2¹⁴N + α → ¹⁷O + p (first induced nuclear reaction)
  • 3Neutron discovered by Chadwick (1932)
  • 4⁹Be + α → ¹²C + n
  • 5Nuclear cross section: Probability of reaction
  • 6Barn = 10⁻²⁸ m² (unit of cross section)
  • 7Radioisotopes in medicine: Tc-99m, I-131, Co-60
  • 8Radiotherapy: Cancer treatment with radiation
  • 9Food irradiation: Preservation using γ-rays
  • 10Industrial radiography: Non-destructive testing
  • 11Smoke detectors: α-source (Am-241)
  • 12Nuclear batteries: Long-lived isotopes (Pu-238)
  • 13Neutron activation analysis: Element detection
  • 14PET scan: Positron emission tomography
  • 15Carbon dating: Archaeology and geology

13. Particle Physics & Fundamental Forces

16 Key Points
  • 1Four fundamental forces: Strong, electromagnetic, weak, gravity
  • 2Strong force: Binds nucleons, range ≈ 1 fm
  • 3Weak force: Responsible for β-decay, very short range
  • 4Electromagnetic force: Infinite range, binds atoms
  • 5Gravity: Weakest, cosmic scale
  • 6Hadrons: Subject to strong force (baryons, mesons)
  • 7Baryons: Half-integer spin (proton, neutron)
  • 8Mesons: Integer spin (pions, kaons)
  • 9Leptons: Not subject to strong force (electron, neutrino)
  • 10Quarks: Fundamental constituents of hadrons
  • 11Proton = uud, Neutron = udd
  • 12Pions: π⁺(ud̄), π⁰(uū/dd̄), π⁻(dū)
  • 13Exchange particles: Gluons (strong), photons (EM), W/Z (weak)
  • 14Antiparticles: Same mass, opposite charge
  • 15Standard Model: Current theory of particle physics
  • 16Large Hadron Collider (LHC): Studies fundamental particles
