1. Displacement Current • Maxwell's Correction

Need for Displacement Current

Ampere-Maxwell Law

Maxwell introduced displacement current to resolve inconsistency in Ampere's circuital law for time-varying electric fields, particularly between capacitor plates.

∮ B·dl = μ₀(I_c + I_d) = μ₀(I_c + ε₀ dΦ_E/dt)
Ampere's law ∮ B·dl = μ₀I inconsistent for capacitor charging/discharging
Between capacitor plates: Conduction current I_c = 0 but magnetic field exists
Displacement current I_d = ε₀ dΦ_E/dt = ε₀ A dE/dt (for parallel plate)
I_d arises from changing electric field, not from flow of charge
During capacitor charging: I_d between plates = I_c in wires
Displacement current density: J_d = ε₀ ∂E/∂t
Total current density: J_total = J_c + J_d
I_d produces magnetic field just like conduction current
Essential for electromagnetic wave propagation in vacuum
Made Maxwell's equations symmetric between E and B fields

Displacement Current Calculations:

Parallel plate capacitor: A=0.01 m², dE/dt=10¹² V/m·s → I_d = ε₀ A dE/dt = 8.85×10⁻¹² × 0.01 × 10¹² = 0.0885 A

Capacitor charging: C=1μF, dV/dt=1000 V/s → I_d = C dV/dt = 10⁻⁶ × 1000 = 0.001 A

Sinusoidal field: E = E₀ sin(ωt) → I_d = ε₀ A ω E₀ cos(ωt)

Between spherical capacitor plates: I_d same at all radii (due to conservation)

2. Maxwell's Equations • Integral & Differential Forms

The Four Maxwell Equations

1. Gauss's Law for Electricity: ∮ E·dA = Q/ε₀ → ∇·E = ρ/ε₀
2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism: ∮ B·dA = 0 → ∇·B = 0
3. Faraday's Law: ∮ E·dl = -dΦ_B/dt → ∇×E = -∂B/∂t
4. Ampere-Maxwell Law: ∮ B·dl = μ₀(I_c + ε₀ dΦ_E/dt) → ∇×B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀ ∂E/∂t
Gauss's law for E: Electric field lines begin on +ve charges, end on -ve charges
Gauss's law for B: No magnetic monopoles exist (field lines closed)
Faraday's law: Changing magnetic field induces electric field
Ampere-Maxwell law: Current AND changing electric field produce magnetic field
In vacuum (ρ=0, J=0): ∇·E=0, ∇·B=0, ∇×E=-∂B/∂t, ∇×B=μ₀ε₀ ∂E/∂t
From these, wave equation derived: ∇²E = μ₀ε₀ ∂²E/∂t²
Speed of EM waves: c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) = 3×10⁸ m/s
Maxwell's equations are relativistic invariant (Lorentz invariant)
They unified electricity, magnetism, and optics
Predict existence of EM waves propagating at speed of light

Symmetry & Physical Interpretation

Symmetry in Maxwell's Equations

Maxwell's equations exhibit beautiful symmetry between electric and magnetic fields, broken only by the absence of magnetic monopoles.

Maxwell's Equations Applications:

Wave equation derivation: Take curl of ∇×E = -∂B/∂t and use ∇×B = μ₀ε₀ ∂E/∂t → ∇²E = μ₀ε₀ ∂²E/∂t²

Continuity equation: From ∇·(∇×B) = 0 and Ampere-Maxwell → ∇·J + ∂ρ/∂t = 0 (charge conservation)

Boundary conditions: At interface: E_tangential continuous, D_normal discontinuous by σ, etc.

Poynting theorem: From Maxwell's equations: ∇·S + ∂u/∂t = -J·E (energy conservation)

3. EM Wave Characteristics • Wave Equation • Polarization

Properties of EM Waves

EM waves are transverse: E and B perpendicular to propagation direction
E and B are perpendicular to each other: E ⊥ B
Direction of propagation given by E × B (right-hand rule)
E and B oscillate in phase (reach max/min together)
In vacuum: E₀/B₀ = c = 3×10⁸ m/s
Speed in vacuum: c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s (exact: 299,792,458 m/s)
Speed in medium: v = c/n, where n = √(με/μ₀ε₀) = √(μ_r ε_r)
For non-magnetic media: n = √ε_r = √(κ) (κ = dielectric constant)
Frequency remains same in different media, wavelength changes
No medium required: Can propagate in vacuum (unlike mechanical waves)
Wave equations: ∇²E = με ∂²E/∂t², ∇²B = με ∂²B/∂t²
Plane wave solutions: E = E₀ sin(k·r - ωt), B = B₀ sin(k·r - ωt)
Relation: E₀ = c B₀ (in vacuum), k = ω/v = 2π/λ, ω = 2πf
Impedance of free space: Z₀ = E₀/H₀ = √(μ₀/ε₀) ≈ 377 Ω

Optical activity: Rotation of polarization plane by chiral substances
LCD displays use polarization to control light transmission
Polaroid sunglasses reduce glare from horizontal surfaces
Polarization proves transverse nature of EM waves

4. Energy • Momentum • Radiation Pressure

Energy in EM Waves

Poynting Vector & Energy Density

The Poynting vector S represents the directional energy flux density of an electromagnetic field.

Poynting vector: S = (1/μ₀) E × B (W/m²)
Energy density: u = u_E + u_B = ½ ε₀ E² + ½ B²/μ₀
For plane wave: u_E = u_B, u = ε₀ E² = B²/μ₀
Intensity: I = ⟨S⟩ = ½ c ε₀ E₀² = ½ (E₀ B₀)/μ₀
Energy density equally divided between E and B fields
Instantaneous energy flux: S = energy crossing unit area per unit time
Time-averaged intensity for sinusoidal wave: I = ½ c ε₀ E₀²
For spherical wave from point source: I ∝ 1/r² (inverse square law)
Total power through area A: P = ∫ S·dA
Poynting theorem: ∇·S + ∂u/∂t = -J·E (energy conservation)
In conducting medium, energy dissipated as Joule heat
For standing EM waves, net energy flow = 0 (S averages to 0)
Momentum density: g = S/c² = u/c (for plane wave in vacuum)
EM waves carry both energy and momentum

Solar constant at Earth: S ≈ 1360 W/m² → Radiation pressure ≈ 4.5 μPa (absorbing)

Radiation Pressure Calculations:

Solar sail: Area 1000 m², reflectivity 0.9, solar constant 1360 W/m² → F = (1+0.9)×1360×1000/(3×10⁸) ≈ 0.0086 N

Laser propulsion: 1 MW laser on perfectly reflecting sail → F = 2×10⁶/(3×10⁸) ≈ 0.0067 N

Light bulb: 100W isotropic, at 1m distance: I = 100/(4π×1²) ≈ 7.96 W/m², P_rad = I/c ≈ 2.65×10⁻⁸ Pa (negligible)

Comet tail: Radiation pressure pushes dust tail away from Sun

5. EM Spectrum - Radio • Microwave • Infrared

Radio Waves (λ > 0.1 m, f < 3 GHz)

Longest wavelength, lowest frequency EM waves
Produced by: Accelerating electrons in antennas (LC circuits)
Propagation: Ground wave (long λ), sky wave (ionosphere reflection), space wave (line-of-sight)
AM radio: 530-1710 kHz (medium wave), propagates as ground wave at night
FM radio: 88-108 MHz (VHF), line-of-sight propagation
TV broadcast: VHF (54-216 MHz) and UHF (470-890 MHz)
Modulation: AM (amplitude), FM (frequency), PM (phase)
Antennas: Dipole (λ/2), monopole (λ/4), parabolic dish (directional)
Applications: Broadcasting, communication, radar, GPS, Wi-Fi
Harmless at normal intensities (non-ionizing)

Infrared (700 nm - 1 mm, 300 GHz - 430 THz)

IR divided: Near IR (0.7-1.4 μm), Mid IR (1.4-3 μm), Far IR (3 μm-1 mm)
Thermal radiation: All bodies emit IR proportional to T⁴ (Stefan-Boltzmann)
Blackbody spectrum peak: λ_max T = 2.9×10⁻³ m·K (Wien's law)
Human body: T≈310K → λ_max ≈ 9.4 μm (far IR)
Atmospheric windows: 3-5 μm and 8-14 μm (transparent to IR)
Greenhouse effect: IR trapped by CO₂, H₂O, CH₄, etc.
Applications: Night vision, thermal imaging, remote controls, fiber optics
IR spectroscopy: Molecular vibrations absorb specific IR frequencies
IR LEDs and lasers: Used in optical communications
Earth's energy balance: Absorbs visible from Sun, emits IR to space

8. Quick Revision • Formulas • Important Facts

Core Formula Summary

• Displacement current: I_d = ε₀ dΦ_E/dt
• Maxwell's equations: ∇·E=ρ/ε₀, ∇·B=0, ∇×E=-∂B/∂t, ∇×B=μ₀J+μ₀ε₀∂E/∂t
• Speed of light: c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s
• In medium: v = c/n, n = √(μ_r ε_r)
• Wave relation: c = fλ, E₀ = c B₀
• Energy density: u = ½ ε₀ E² + ½ B²/μ₀ = ε₀ E² = B²/μ₀ (for plane wave)
• Poynting vector: S = (1/μ₀) E × B, Intensity I = ⟨S⟩ = ½ c ε₀ E₀²
• Radiation pressure: P_rad = I/c (absorbing), 2I/c (reflecting)
• Photon energy: E = hf = hc/λ
• Wien's law: λ_max T = 2.9×10⁻³ m·K
• Malus's law: I = I₀ cos²θ
• Brewster's angle: tan θ_B = n₂/n₁

Problem Solving Strategy

Displacement current: Identify changing E-field, use I_d = ε₀ dΦ_E/dt
EM wave problems: Use c = fλ, E₀/B₀ = c, energy ∝ E₀²
Intensity calculations: I = P/A, I ∝ 1/r² for point source
Radiation pressure: Determine if surface absorbs or reflects
Spectrum problems: Remember order (radio→γ), know approximate λ, f ranges
Polarization: Use Malus's law I = I₀ cos²θ
Photon calculations: E = hf = hc/λ, 1 eV = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J
Check units: Hz, m, J, W/m², Pa (for pressure)
Boundary conditions: For reflection/transmission at interface
Conservation laws: Energy, momentum in radiation interactions

Common Pitfalls & Important Facts

  • Displacement current is NOT actual current flow, but changing E-field
  • EM waves are transverse: E ⊥ B ⊥ direction of propagation
  • In vacuum, all EM waves travel at same speed c regardless of frequency
  • Frequency remains constant when wave enters different medium, wavelength changes
  • Ionizing radiation: UV, X-rays, γ-rays (can remove electrons from atoms)
  • Non-ionizing: Radio, microwave, IR, visible (generally less harmful)
  • Polarization only possible for transverse waves, proves light is transverse
  • EM waves carry momentum → radiation pressure (tiny but measurable)
  • Maxwell's equations predict EM waves, Hertz experimentally confirmed
  • EM spectrum is continuous, boundaries between regions are approximate
