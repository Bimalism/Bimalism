1. Rate of Reaction & Factors

  • Rate = –d[R]/dt = +d[P]/dt (average or instantaneous)
  • Factors: Concentration, Temperature, Catalyst, Surface area, Light
  • Rate ∝ [Reactants]^order (rate law from experiment only)
  • Catalyst lowers activation energy → increases rate
  • Negative order possible (inhibition)
  • Rate constant k has units depending on order
  • Initial rate method used to find order
  • Pseudo first order: Excess reactant → order appears 1
  • Temperature ↑ → rate ↑ exponentially
  • JEE loves: Effect of catalyst on rate and Ea

2. Order vs Molecularity

  • Order = Sum of powers in rate law (experimental)
  • Molecularity = Number of molecules in elementary step (theoretical)
  • Order can be zero, fraction, negative; Molecularity 1, 2, 3 only
  • Complex reaction → overall order ≠ molecularity
  • Elementary reaction → order = molecularity
  • Unimolecular: 1st order, Bimolecular: 2nd order
  • Zero order: On solid surface or enzyme saturation
  • Rate determining step (RDS) decides overall order
  • JEE asks: Can order be zero? Yes (NH₃ on Pt)
  • Molecularity never fractional or zero

3. Zero Order Reactions

  • [A] = [A]₀ – kt (integrated rate law)
  • t½ = [A]₀ / 2k (half-life ∝ initial concentration)
  • Plot of [A] vs t → straight line (slope = –k)
  • Unit of k = mol L⁻¹ s⁻¹
  • Examples: Decomposition on hot metal surface
  • Enzyme catalysis (Michaelis-Menten at high [S])
  • Photochemical reactions sometimes zero order
  • Rate independent of reactant concentration
  • JEE favourite: Find k from half-life data
  • t₁₀₀ = [A]₀ / k (time for completion)
Zero Order: [A] = [A]₀ – kt
t½ = [A]₀ / 2k

4. First Order Reactions

  • ln[A] = ln[A]₀ – kt → k = (2.303/t) log([A]₀/[A])
  • t½ = 0.693 / k (independent of initial concentration)
  • Plot of ln[A] vs t → straight line (slope = –k)
  • Unit of k = s⁻¹ (time⁻¹)
  • All radioactive decay, inversion of cane sugar
  • Pseudo first order: Hydrolysis of ester (excess water)
  • Time for 99% completion = 6.93 / k ≈ 10 × t½
  • Successive half-lives are equal
  • JEE loves: Calculate k from half-life or concentration data
  • Rate = k[A] even if reaction is bimolecular
First Order: k = (2.303/t) log([A]₀/[A])
t½ = 0.693/k

5. Temperature Dependence

  • Rate increases 2–3 times for every 10°C rise
  • Temperature coefficient (Q₁₀) = k_(T+10)/k_T ≈ 2–3
  • Most reactions double rate per 10°C rise
  • Enzymes denature above 45°C → rate drops
  • High Ea reactions more sensitive to T
  • Arrhenius equation explains T dependence
  • Log(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/2.303R) (1/T₁ – 1/T₂)
  • Plot of ln k vs 1/T → straight line (slope = –Ea/R)
  • JEE asks: Effect of T on rate constant
  • Reaction with Ea = 50 kJ/mol → rate ×16 when T from 300→330 K

6. Arrhenius Equation & Activation Energy

  • k = A e^(-Ea/RT) → A = pre-exponential factor
  • Ea = Activation energy barrier
  • ln k = ln A – Ea/RT
  • Higher Ea → slower reaction
  • Catalyst provides alternate path with lower Ea
  • A = frequency of successful collisions
  • Unit of Ea = J/mol or kJ/mol
  • Typical Ea: 50–200 kJ/mol
  • JEE favourite: Calculate Ea from two k values
  • Negative catalyst (inhibitor) increases effective Ea
log(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/2.303R) (1/T₁ – 1/T₂)

7. Collision Theory

  • Rate = Z × f × e^(-Ea/RT)
  • Z = collision frequency
  • f = steric/orientation factor (P factor)
  • Only fraction of collisions have proper orientation
  • Complex molecules → low P factor
  • Gaseous reactions: Rate ∝ √T (from Z)
  • High Ea → very few molecules have sufficient energy
  • A = P × Z (Arrhenius factor)
  • JEE asks: Why actual rate < theoretical collision rate?
  • Energy distribution follows Maxwell-Boltzmann

8. Reaction Mechanisms & Rate Laws

  • Slowest step = Rate Determining Step (RDS)
  • Rate law written from RDS
  • Intermediates not in rate law
  • 2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂ → rate = k[NO]²[O₂] (termolecular RDS)
  • Decomposition of N₂O₅ → first order (unimolecular RDS)
  • H₂ + I₂ → 2HI → bimolecular elementary
  • Lindemann mechanism for unimolecular reactions
  • Steady state approximation for intermediates
  • JEE loves: Find order from mechanism
  • Pre-equilibrium approximation

9. JEE Most Important Points (Revise Daily)

  • First order t½ independent of [A]₀ → t½ = 0.693/k
  • Zero order t½ ∝ [A]₀ → t½ = [A]₀/2k
  • k = A e^(-Ea/RT) → Arrhenius equation
