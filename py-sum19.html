1. Reflection of Light

18 Key Points
  • 1First law: Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection (i = r)
  • 2Second law: Incident ray, reflected ray, normal lie in same plane
  • 3Plane mirror: Virtual, erect, laterally inverted image
  • 4Image distance = Object distance (u = -v)
  • 5Field of view increases with mirror size and proximity
  • 6Deviation angle: δ = 180° – 2i (for plane mirror)
  • 7Multiple images: N = (360°/θ) – 1 if 360°/θ is integer
  • 8Velocity of image perpendicular to mirror: v_i = –v_object
  • 9Parallel component of velocity remains unchanged
  • 10Extended object: Image same size only if object parallel to mirror
  • 11Rotation of mirror by θ causes reflected ray to rotate by 2θ
  • 12Minimum mirror height for full view = half person's height
  • 13Periscope uses two plane mirrors at 45°
  • 14Kaleidoscope uses multiple reflections for patterns
  • 15Lateral inversion: Left appears right, right appears left
  • 16No chromatic aberration in mirrors (achromatic)
  • 17Image formed by mirror is as far behind as object in front
  • 18Used in rear-view mirrors, dressing mirrors, optical instruments

2. Spherical Mirrors

16 Key Points
  • 1Concave mirror: Converging, real/inverted or virtual/erect image
  • 2Convex mirror: Diverging, always virtual/erect/diminished
  • 3New Cartesian sign convention: Object distance negative
  • 4Pole P, Centre of curvature C, Focus F, Radius R = 2f
  • 5Paraxial rays: Small angle approximation for formula derivation
  • 6Concave: f negative, R negative
  • 7Convex: f positive, R positive
  • 8Aperture affects spherical aberration (marginal vs paraxial rays)
  • 9Focal length independent of medium surrounding mirror
  • 10Mirror formula derived using similar triangles (geometry)
  • 11Principal axis: Line joining pole and centre of curvature
  • 12Parabolic mirrors eliminate spherical aberration
  • 13Spherical mirrors approximate paraboloid near pole
  • 14Magnification varies with object position
  • 15Concave mirror used in searchlights, shaving mirrors
  • 16Convex mirror used in rear-view mirrors, security mirrors

3. Mirror Formula & Magnification

18 Key Points
  • 1Mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f
  • 2Magnification: m = –v/u = h_i/h_o
  • 3m negative → inverted image, m positive → erect image
  • 4|m| > 1 → enlarged image, |m| < 1 → diminished image
  • 5For real object, u always negative (sign convention)
  • 6Concave mirror beyond C: diminished, real, inverted
  • 7Concave mirror at C: same size, real, inverted
  • 8Concave mirror between C and F: enlarged, real, inverted
  • 9Concave mirror between F and P: enlarged, virtual, erect
  • 10Convex mirror: always diminished, virtual, erect
  • 11Power: P = –1/f (in metres) dioptre
  • 12Velocity of image: v_i = –m² v_o (longitudinal)
  • 13Standard ray diagrams: 3 rays (parallel, through focus, through centre)
  • 14Graphical method: u-v graph hyperbola, 1/u-1/v graph straight line
  • 15Linear magnification = transverse magnification
  • 16Longitudinal magnification = m² (for small objects)
  • 17Area magnification = m²
  • 18Concave mirror at infinity: image at focus, highly diminished

4. Refraction of Light

17 Key Points
  • 1Snell's law: n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
  • 2Absolute refractive index: n = c/v (speed in vacuum/speed in medium)
  • 3Relative refractive index: n₂₁ = n₂/n₁ = v₁/v₂
  • 4Real depth/Apparent depth = n (normal viewing)
  • 5Lateral shift through slab: t = d sin(i–r)/cos r
  • 6Critical angle: sin c = 1/n (denser to rarer medium)
  • 7Refraction through parallel slab: emergent ray parallel to incident
  • 8Apparent shift = t(1 – 1/n) for normal incidence
  • 9Brewster's law: tan i_p = n (polarizing angle)
  • 10Speed decreases in denser medium (higher refractive index)
  • 11Frequency remains constant during refraction
  • 12Wavelength changes: λ_medium = λ_vacuum/n
  • 13Refractive index depends on wavelength (dispersion)
  • 14Refraction at spherical surfaces: (n₂/v) – (n₁/u) = (n₂ – n₁)/R
  • 15Apparent bending of objects in water due to refraction
  • 16Atmospheric refraction causes advanced sunrise, delayed sunset
  • 17Twinkling of stars due to atmospheric refraction variations

5. Total Internal Reflection & Applications

16 Key Points
  • 1Occurs when i > c and light travels from denser to rarer medium
  • 2Optical fibre: core n₁, cladding n₂ < n₁
  • 3Acceptance angle: sin i_max = √(n₁² – n₂²)
  • 4Numerical aperture NA = √(n₁² – n₂²) = n₀ sin i_max
  • 5Mirage: due to TIR in hot road (temperature gradient)
  • 6Brilliance of diamond: high n (2.42) gives small c (24.4°)
  • 7Endoscopy uses fibre bundles for internal body examination
  • 8100% reflection, no energy loss (ideal TIR)
  • 9Fish-eye view: fish sees outside world compressed within cone
  • 10Porro prism: right-angle prism used for image erection
  • 11Optical communication: fibres carry light signals over long distances
  • 12Step-index vs graded-index fibres
  • 13Pulse dispersion limits bandwidth in fibres
  • 14Binoculars use prisms for image erection and compact design
  • 15Sparkling of cut glass due to multiple TIR
  • 16Optical switches and sensors use TIR principle

6. Lens Maker Formula & Lens Power

18 Key Points
  • 1Lens maker formula: 1/f = (n–1)(1/R₁ – 1/R₂)
  • 2Convex lens: R₁ +ve (convex), R₂ –ve (concave) → f +ve
  • 3Concave lens: R₁ +ve (concave), R₂ +ve (convex) → f –ve
  • 4Lens formula: 1/v – 1/u = 1/f (sign convention important)
  • 5Magnification: m = v/u = h_i/h_o
  • 6Power: P = 1/f (metre) = 100/f (cm) in dioptres
  • 7Convex lens: converging, f positive, P positive
  • 8Concave lens: diverging, f negative, P negative
  • 9Air bubble in water acts as concave lens (n_lens < n_medium)
  • 10f changes in different medium: f_medium/f_air = (n_g – 1)/(n_g/n_m – 1)
  • 11Thin lens approximation: thickness << R₁, R₂
  • 12Principal focus: point where parallel rays converge or appear to diverge
  • 13Focal plane: plane perpendicular to axis through focus
  • 14Optical centre: point where ray passes undeviated
  • 15Displacement method for focal length: f = (D² – d²)/4D
  • 16Convex lens: real inverted or virtual erect depending on u
  • 17Concave lens: always virtual, erect, diminished
  • 18Lens immersed in liquid of higher n becomes diverging if n_lens < n_liquid

7. Combination of Thin Lenses

16 Key Points
  • 1Lenses in contact: 1/F = 1/f₁ + 1/f₂ + …
  • 2Power adds: P = P₁ + P₂
  • 3Lenses in contact act as single equivalent lens
  • 4Separated lenses: image of first acts as object for second
  • 5Achromatic doublet: crown + flint lens removes chromatic aberration
  • 6Two thin lenses separated by d: 1/F = 1/f₁ + 1/f₂ – d/(f₁f₂)
  • 7Used in compound microscope, telescope, camera lenses
  • 8Displacement method: f = (D² – d²)/4D for convex lens
  • 9Convex + concave combination can be converging or diverging
  • 10Equivalent focal length depends on separation and individual powers
  • 11Position of equivalent lens from first: x = F d/f₂
  • 12Magnification of combination: M = m₁ × m₂
  • 13Telephoto lens: large f in compact size using combination
  • 14Zoom lens: variable focal length using moving lens elements
  • 15Eye-piece of microscope/telescope often compound lens
  • 16Aberration correction using lens combinations

8. Prism Refraction & Dispersion

18 Key Points
  • 1Deviation: δ = i₁ + i₂ – A (A = prism angle)
  • 2Minimum deviation: δ_m = 2i – A (i₁ = i₂, r₁ = r₂ = A/2)
  • 3Refractive index: n = sin[(A+δ_m)/2] / sin(A/2)
  • 4Dispersion: Violet deviates most, red least (n_v > n_r)
  • 5Angular dispersion: θ = δ_v – δ_r = (n_v – n_r)A (for small A)
  • 6Dispersive power: ω = (n_v – n_r)/(n_y – 1) where n_y = yellow mean
  • 7Direct vision spectroscope: crown + flint prism combination
  • 8Thin prism approximation: δ = (n – 1)A
  • 9Rainbow: Primary (42°) and secondary (51°) due to dispersion + TIR
  • 10Spectrum purity depends on ω (small ω = less dispersion)
  • 11No dispersion for white light at minimum deviation position
  • 12Prism binoculars use Porro prisms for image erection
  • 13Critical angle in prism: important for TIR prisms
  • 14Prism periscope: uses right-angle prisms instead of mirrors
  • 15Constant deviation prism (60°): δ constant for all wavelengths
  • 16Achromatic prism: ω₁/ω₂ = –f₁/f₂ for no dispersion
  • 17Prism spectrometer measures refractive indices
  • 18Grazing incidence: i₁ = 90°, grazing emergence: i₂ = 90°

9. Scattering & Human Eye

17 Key Points
  • 1Rayleigh scattering: I ∝ 1/λ⁴ → explains blue sky
  • 2Blue light scattered more → sky appears blue
  • 3Sun appears red at sunrise/sunset due to longer path through atmosphere
  • 4Human eye: Power ≈ 58–60 D, least distance of distinct vision = 25 cm
  • 5Myopia (nearsightedness): far point < ∞, corrected by concave lens
  • 6Hypermetropia (farsightedness): near point > 25 cm, corrected by convex lens
  • 7Presbyopia: loss of accommodation with age, bifocal lens needed
  • 8Astigmatism: cornea not spherical, corrected by cylindrical lens
  • 9Retina acts as screen, fovea most sensitive (cones concentrated)
  • 10Persistence of vision ≈ 1/16 second (basis of cinema)
  • 11Accommodation: ciliary muscles change lens shape for focusing
  • 12Range of vision: 25 cm to infinity (normal eye)
  • 13Near point (25 cm), far point (infinity) for normal eye
  • 14Tyndall effect: scattering by colloidal particles
  • 15Why clouds white? Large water droplets scatter all colors equally
  • 16Danger signals red: red scatters least, visible from farthest
  • 17Rods: dim light vision (scotopic), cones: color vision (photopic)

10. Telescopes

16 Key Points
  • 1Astronomical telescope (normal): m = f_o/f_e, L = f_o + f_e
  • 2Final image inverted (not problem for astronomical objects)
  • 3Galilean telescope: convex objective + concave eyepiece, erect image
  • 4Terrestrial telescope: erecting lens between objective and eyepiece
  • 5Magnifying power: m = f_o/f_e (normal adjustment), m = f_o/f_e (1 + D/f_e) (relaxed eye)
  • 6Larger objective aperture → brighter image and better resolution
  • 7Reflecting telescope: concave mirror as objective, avoids chromatic aberration
  • 8Newtonian telescope: plane mirror to divert light sideways
  • 9Cassegrain telescope: convex secondary mirror, compact design
  • 10Resolving power ∝ aperture (diameter of objective)
  • 11Light gathering power ∝ (aperture)²
  • 12Field of view depends on eyepiece design
  • 13Used for distant celestial objects (planets, stars, galaxies)
  • 14Radio telescopes: very large apertures possible
  • 15Space telescopes (Hubble): above atmosphere for better resolution
  • 16Schmidt telescope: corrects spherical aberration with corrector plate

11. Compound Microscope

17 Key Points
  • 1Magnification: m = m_o × m_e = (v_o/u_o) × (D/f_e) or (1 + D/f_e)
  • 2Tube length: L = v_o + f_e (distance between lenses)
  • 3Image by objective inside focal length of eyepiece
  • 4Final image inverted (upside down and laterally inverted)
  • 5Maximum useful magnification ≈ 1500–2000×
  • 6Numerical aperture NA = n sin β (n = refractive index, β = half-angle)
  • 7Oil immersion increases NA → better resolution
  • 8Resolving power: d_min = 1.22λ/(2 n sin β) = 1.22λ/(2 NA)
  • 9Used for magnified close objects (biological specimens, etc.)
  • 10Intermediate image real and inverted
  • 11Simple microscope (magnifying glass): m = D/f (normal), m = 1 + D/f (max)
  • 12Electron microscope: uses electrons (λ very small) → very high resolution
  • 13Phase contrast microscope: visualizes transparent specimens
  • 14Fluorescence microscope: uses fluorescent dyes
  • 15Confocal microscope: eliminates out-of-focus light
  • 16Working distance: distance between specimen and objective
  • 17Parfocal: objectives stay in focus when changed

12. Wavefront & Huygens' Principle

15 Key Points
  • 1Wavefront: locus of points in same phase of vibration
  • 2Spherical wavefront: from point source
  • 3Plane wavefront: at large distance from point source
  • 4Cylindrical wavefront: from linear source
  • 5Huygens' principle: every point on wavefront acts as secondary source
  • 6Secondary wavelets spherical with speed of wave in medium
  • 7Envelope of secondary wavelets gives new wavefront
  • 8Explains reflection, refraction, diffraction
  • 9Plane wave → plane wavefront in homogeneous medium
  • 10Backward wavelet cancelled by destructive interference
  • 11Only forward propagation supported
  • 12Foundation of wave optics (geometrical optics as limiting case)
  • 13Explains laws of reflection and refraction from wave theory
  • 14Ray: perpendicular to wavefront, direction of energy propagation
  • 15Huygens-Fresnel principle: includes interference of secondary wavelets

13. Interference of Light

18 Key Points
  • 1Superposition of two coherent waves
  • 2Constructive interference: path difference = nλ → bright fringe
  • 3Destructive interference: path difference = (2n–1)λ/2 → dark fringe
  • 4Coherent sources: constant phase relationship (same frequency)
  • 5Incoherent sources: random phase difference → uniform illumination
  • 6Intensity: I = I₁ + I₂ + 2√(I₁I₂) cos δ
  • 7For equal intensities: I = 4I₀ cos²(δ/2)
  • 8I_max = (√I₁ + √I₂)², I_min = (√I₁ – √I₂)²
  • 9Energy redistributed, total energy conserved
  • 10Visible interference requires fringe width > eye resolution
  • 11Stable pattern needs temporal and spatial coherence
  • 12Phase difference δ = (2π/λ) × path difference
  • 13Thin film interference: colors in soap bubbles, oil films
  • 14Air wedge interference: for measuring small thickness
  • 15Newton's rings: interference between lens and glass plate
  • 16Michelson interferometer: precise length measurements
  • 17Lloyd's mirror: single source + reflection creates interference
  • 18Fresnel's biprism: creates two virtual coherent sources

14. Young's Double Slit Experiment

17 Key Points
  • 1Fringe width: β = λD/d
  • 2Angular fringe width: θ = λ/d
  • 3Central fringe bright (zero path difference)
  • 4Bright fringes: x = nλD/d (n = 0, ±1, ±2...)
  • 5Dark fringes: x = (2n–1)λD/2d
  • 6If one slit closed → single slit diffraction pattern
  • 7White light → colored fringes, central white (all colors constructively interfere)
  • 8Immersion in liquid → λ decreases → β decreases
  • 9Shift by thin transparent sheet: Δx = (μ–1)t D/d
  • 10Proof of wave nature of light (classical experiment)
  • 11Intensity distribution sinusoidal
  • 12Coherence length: maximum path difference for interference
  • 13Laser gives clear fringes due to high coherence
  • 14Fringe visibility V = (I_max – I_min)/(I_max + I_min)
  • 15For equal intensities, V = 1 (maximum contrast)
  • 16Used to measure wavelength of light
  • 17Angular position: θ_n = nλ/d for small angles

15. Diffraction of Light

16 Key Points
  • 1Bending of light around obstacles or through apertures
  • 2Central maximum brightest and widest
  • 3Angular width of central maximum = 2λ/a
  • 4Minima condition: a sinθ = nλ (n = ±1, ±2, ±3...)
  • 5Secondary maxima between minima (much dimmer than central)
  • 6Intensity falls rapidly away from centre
  • 7Smaller slit width → more spreading (diffraction)
  • 8Fresnel diffraction: near field (source/screen at finite distance)
  • 9Fraunhofer diffraction: far field (source/screen at infinity)
  • 10Straight edge diffraction: fringes inside geometric shadow
  • 11Proof of wave nature (cannot be explained by ray optics)
  • 12Circular aperture diffraction: Airy pattern (central bright disk)
  • 13Diffraction grating: multiple slits for high dispersion
  • 14X-ray diffraction: studies crystal structure
  • 15Babinet's principle: complementary screens give same diffraction pattern
  • 16Zone plate: alternative to lens using diffraction

16. Single Slit Diffraction Pattern

15 Key Points
  • 1Linear width of central maximum = 2λD/a
  • 2Angular width = 2λ/a (for small angles)
  • 3Width inversely proportional to slit width a
  • 4Smaller a → larger diffraction (more spreading)
  • 5At a = λ → central maximum spreads over 2 radians (≈114.6°)
  • 6Intensity: I = I₀ (sin²β/β²) where β = (π a sinθ)/λ
  • 7First minima at sinθ = ±λ/a
  • 8Half angular width = λ/a
  • 9Used to measure wavelength of light
  • 10Resolution limit of optical instruments depends on diffraction
  • 11Secondary maxima at β = 1.43π, 2.46π, 3.47π...
  • 12Intensity of first secondary maximum ≈ 4.7% of central
  • 13Intensity of second secondary maximum ≈ 1.6% of central
  • 14Most energy (≈85%) in central maximum
  • 15Diffraction sets fundamental limit to focusing

17. Resolving Power of Optical Instruments

16 Key Points
  • 1Minimum angular separation resolvable
  • 2Telescope: RP = a/(1.22λ) where a = aperture diameter
  • 3Microscope: d_min = 1.22λ/(2 n sinβ) = 1.22λ/(2 NA)
  • 4Larger aperture → better resolution (smaller d_min)
  • 5Rayleigh criterion: just resolved when central max of one coincides with first min of other
  • 6Oil immersion increases NA → better resolution
  • 7Electron microscope: λ very small (de Broglie wavelength) → high resolution
  • 8Human eye resolution ≈ 1 arc minute (≈ 3×10⁻⁴ rad)
  • 9Limit due to diffraction, not lens quality (theoretical limit)
  • 10Higher λ → poorer resolution (red light worse than blue)
  • 11Resolving power of grating: RP = Nm (N = lines, m = order)
  • 12Prism resolving power: RP = b dn/dλ (b = base length)
  • 13Airy disk: diffraction pattern from circular aperture
  • 14Spatial resolution: ability to distinguish closely spaced objects
  • 15Angular resolution: minimum angular separation distinguishable
  • 16Super-resolution techniques overcome diffraction limit

18. Polarisation of Light

17 Key Points
  • 1Proof of transverse wave nature of light
  • 2Unpolarised light: vibrations in all directions perpendicular to propagation
  • 3Plane polarised light: vibrations confined to one plane
  • 4Polaroids transmit light with electric vector parallel to transmission axis
  • 5Brewster's law: tan i_p = n (i_p = polarising angle)
  • 6At i_p, reflected light fully polarised perpendicular to plane of incidence
  • 7Malus law: I = I₀ cos²θ (θ between transmission axes)
  • 8Optical activity: rotation of plane of polarisation by chiral substances
  • 9Specific rotation [α] = α/(l × c) (l in dm, c in g/mL)
  • 10Dichroism: selective absorption of one polarization direction
  • 11Scattering also polarises light (sky light partially polarised)
  • 12Circular polarization: electric vector rotates
  • 13Elliptical polarization: general case
  • 14Quarter-wave plate converts plane to circular polarisation
  • 15Half-wave plate rotates plane of polarisation
  • 16Polarimeters measure concentration of optically active solutions
  • 17Applications: sunglasses (reduce glare), LCD displays, stress analysis

19. Optical Instruments & Applications

16 Key Points
  • 1Camera: convex lens forms real inverted image on film/sensor
  • 2f-number = f/D (aperture ratio), smaller f-number = brighter image
  • 3Depth of field: range of distances in acceptable focus
  • 4Simple microscope (magnifying glass): m = D/f (normal), m = 1 + D/f (max)
  • 5Projector: illuminated object → convex lens → enlarged real image
  • 6Overhead projector: works on same principle
  • 7Slide projector: magnifies small slides
  • 8Spectrometer: prism/grating to analyze spectra
  • 9Interferometer: measures small distances, refractive indices
  • 10Photometer: compares light intensities
  • 11Colorimeter: measures color intensity (concentration)
  • 12Optical fibers: total internal reflection for communication
  • 13Endoscope: medical imaging inside body
  • 14Periscope: see over obstacles (submarines)
  • 15Kaleidoscope: multiple reflections create patterns
  • 16Binoculars: two telescopes mounted side by side

20. Natural Optical Phenomena

15 Key Points
  • 1Rainbow: dispersion + TIR in water droplets
  • 2Primary rainbow: 42° angle, red outer, violet inner
  • 3Secondary rainbow: 51° angle, violet outer, red inner, fainter
  • 4Halos: refraction through ice crystals (22° and 46° halos)
  • 5Sun dogs (parhelia): bright spots beside sun due to ice crystals
  • 6Green flash: brief green appearance at sunrise/sunset
  • 7Fata Morgana: superior mirage due to temperature inversion
  • 8Glory: colored rings around shadow of observer
  • 9Corona: diffraction by water droplets around moon
  • 10Iridescence: thin film interference (soap bubbles, oil films)
  • 11Aurora: charged particles exciting atmospheric gases
  • 12Zodiacal light: sunlight scattered by interplanetary dust
  • 13Twinkling of stars: atmospheric refraction variations
  • 14Planets don't twinkle: extended source averages out variations
  • 15Blue moon: rare second full moon in calendar month
