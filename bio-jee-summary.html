Ch 1: The Living World
  • Living organisms: characterized by growth, reproduction, metabolism.
  • Taxonomy: science of classification based on characteristics.
  • Binomial nomenclature: two-part naming (genus, species).
  • Hierarchy: species, genus, family, order, class, phylum, kingdom.
  • Biodiversity: variety of life forms on Earth.
  • Taxonomic aids: herbarium, museums, botanical gardens.
  • Metabolism: sum of all chemical reactions in organisms.
  • Five kingdoms: Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia.
  • Species: group of interbreeding organisms with similar traits.
  • Systematics: study of evolutionary relationships among organisms.
Ch 2: Biological Classification
  • Five kingdom classification: based on cell structure, nutrition, organization.
  • Monera: prokaryotes (bacteria, archaebacteria), unicellular.
  • Protista: eukaryotic, unicellular, diverse nutrition modes.
  • Fungi: heterotrophic, cell wall of chitin, e.g., yeast, molds.
  • Plantae: autotrophic, cell wall of cellulose, multicellular.
  • Animalia: heterotrophic, no cell wall, multicellular.
  • Virus: acellular, parasitic, nucleic acid with protein coat.
  • Lichens: symbiotic association of algae and fungi.
  • Bacteria shapes: cocci, bacilli, vibrio, spirilla.
  • Viroids: smaller than viruses, only RNA, no protein coat.
Ch 3: Plant Kingdom
  • Algae: simple, aquatic, chlorophyll-containing, e.g., Chlamydomonas.
  • Bryophytes: non-vascular, moist habitats, e.g., mosses.
  • Pteridophytes: vascular, no seeds, e.g., ferns.
  • Gymnosperms: vascular, naked seeds, e.g., Pinus.
  • Angiosperms: vascular, enclosed seeds, monocots/dicots.
  • Alternation of generations: haploid gametophyte, diploid sporophyte.
  • Photosynthesis: converts light energy to chemical energy.
  • Plant classification: based on vascular tissue, seed type.
  • Life cycle: haplontic, diplontic, or haplo-diplontic.
  • Economic importance: food, timber, medicines from plants.
Ch 4: Animal Kingdom
  • Classification: based on symmetry, germ layers, coelom.
  • Porifera: sponges, no tissues, asymmetrical.
  • Cnidaria: radial symmetry, diploblastic, e.g., jellyfish.
  • Annelida: segmented, triploblastic, coelomate, e.g., earthworm.
  • Arthropoda: jointed legs, exoskeleton, e.g., insects.
  • Mollusca: soft-bodied, often shelled, e.g., snail.
  • Chordata: notochord, dorsal nerve cord, e.g., vertebrates.
  • Coelom: body cavity, true (annelids), pseudo (nematodes).
  • Notochord: flexible rod in chordates, present in embryos.
  • Economic importance: silk, honey, pearls from animals.
Ch 5: Morphology of Flowering Plants
  • Root: taproot (dicots), fibrous (monocots), anchors plant.
  • Stem: supports leaves, flowers, conducts water, nutrients.
  • Leaf: photosynthesis site, simple or compound, venation types.
  • Flower: reproductive organ, parts: sepals, petals, stamens, pistil.
  • Inflorescence: arrangement of flowers on stem, e.g., racemose.
  • Fruit: ripened ovary, protects seeds, aids dispersal.
  • Seed: contains embryo, monocot (1 cotyledon), dicot (2).
  • Modifications: roots (storage), stems (tendrils), leaves (spines).
  • Floral formula: symbolic representation of flower structure.
  • Economic uses: food (cereals), fibers (cotton), medicines.
Ch 6: Anatomy of Flowering Plants
  • Tissues: meristematic (growth), permanent (mature).
  • Simple tissues: parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma.
  • Complex tissues: xylem (water), phloem (food transport).
  • Root anatomy: epidermis, cortex, vascular bundle (radial).
  • Stem anatomy: dicot (vascular bundles in ring), monocot (scattered).
  • Leaf anatomy: epidermis, stomata, mesophyll, veins.
  • Vascular bundles: xylem and phloem, open/closed types.
  • Secondary growth: occurs in dicots, forms wood, bark.
  • Apical meristem: increases length, found in shoot/root tips.
  • Lateral meristem: increases girth, e.g., vascular cambium.
Ch 7: Structural Organisation in Animals
  • Epithelial tissue: covers surfaces, e.g., skin, lining organs.
  • Connective tissue: supports, binds, e.g., bone, blood.
  • Muscular tissue: movement, skeletal, smooth, cardiac types.
  • Nervous tissue: conducts impulses, neurons, glial cells.
  • Earthworm: segmented, nephridia for excretion, hermaphrodite.
  • Cockroach: exoskeleton, open circulation, tracheal respiration.
  • Frog: amphibian, moist skin, dual circulation, hibernation.
  • Organ systems: digestive, respiratory, nervous, reproductive.
  • Coelom: body cavity, true in vertebrates, absent in flatworms.
  • Tissue organization: increases complexity from sponges to mammals.
Ch 8: Cell - The Unit of Life
  • Cell theory: all organisms made of cells, basic unit of life.
  • Prokaryotic cells: no nucleus, e.g., bacteria, smaller size.
  • Eukaryotic cells: nucleus, organelles, e.g., plant, animal cells.
  • Cell membrane: fluid mosaic model, selective permeability.
  • Cytoplasm: site of metabolic activities, contains organelles.
  • Nucleus: controls cell functions, contains DNA in chromatin.
  • Mitochondria: powerhouse, ATP synthesis via respiration.
  • Endoplasmic reticulum: rough (protein synthesis), smooth (lipid).
  • Plant cells: cell wall, chloroplasts, large vacuole.
  • Lysosomes: digest waste, nicknamed suicide bags.
Ch 9: Biomolecules
  • Carbohydrates: sugars, starches, energy source, e.g., glucose.
  • Proteins: amino acids, structural/enzymatic roles, e.g., hemoglobin.
  • Lipids: fats, oils, energy storage, membrane component.
  • Nucleic acids: DNA (genetic info), RNA (protein synthesis).
  • Enzymes: biological catalysts, lower activation energy.
  • Primary structure: sequence of amino acids in proteins.
  • Secondary structure: alpha helix, beta sheet in proteins.
  • Metabolism: anabolism (synthesis), catabolism (breakdown).
  • Vitamins: organic, essential for metabolic functions.
  • Polysaccharides: cellulose (plant cell wall), glycogen (animal storage).
Ch 10: Cell Cycle and Cell Division
  • Cell cycle: interphase (G1, S, G2), M phase (mitosis, cytokinesis).
  • Mitosis: produces two identical daughter cells, somatic growth.
  • Meiosis: produces gametes, reduces chromosome number by half.
  • Prophase: chromosomes condense, spindle forms.
  • Metaphase: chromosomes align at equatorial plate.
  • Anaphase: sister chromatids separate to poles.
  • Telophase: nuclear envelope reforms, chromosomes decondense.
  • Cytokinesis: cytoplasm division, different in plants (cell plate).
  • Significance: growth, repair (mitosis), genetic diversity (meiosis).
  • Checkpoints: regulate cell cycle, prevent errors.
Ch 11: Transport in Plants
  • Diffusion: passive movement of molecules, no energy required.
  • Active transport: uses ATP, against concentration gradient.
  • Xylem: transports water, minerals from roots to leaves.
  • Phloem: transports organic nutrients (sucrose), bidirectional.
  • Transpiration: water loss via stomata, drives water uptake.
  • Root pressure: pushes water up in xylem, minor role.
  • Cohesion-tension: explains water movement in xylem.
  • Imbibition: water absorption by seeds, colloids.
  • Stomatal regulation: guard cells control water loss, gas exchange.
  • Phloem translocation: mass flow hypothesis, source to sink.
Ch 12: Photosynthesis in Higher Plants
  • Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂, light-driven.
  • Chloroplast: site of photosynthesis, contains chlorophyll.
  • Light reaction: photolysis, ATP, NADPH production in thylakoids.
  • Dark reaction: Calvin cycle, CO₂ fixation in stroma.
  • C3 plants: fix CO₂ via Rubisco, common in temperate regions.
  • C4 plants: Kranz anatomy, efficient in hot climates.
  • Photorespiration: reduces efficiency in C3 plants, uses O₂.
  • Factors: light intensity, CO₂ concentration, temperature affect rate.
  • Pigments: chlorophyll a, b, carotenoids absorb light.
  • Photophosphorylation: ATP synthesis using light energy.
Ch 13: Respiration in Plants
  • Respiration: breaks glucose to release energy, C₆H₁₂O₆ → CO₂ + H₂O.
  • Glycolysis: cytoplasm, converts glucose to pyruvate, 2 ATP.
  • Krebs cycle: mitochondria, produces NADH, FADH₂, 2 ATP.
  • Electron transport chain: ATP synthesis via oxidative phosphorylation.
  • Aerobic respiration: high ATP yield (36-38 ATP per glucose).
  • Anaerobic respiration: fermentation, low ATP yield (e.g., lactic acid).
  • Respiratory quotient: RQ = CO₂ released/O₂ consumed, varies by substrate.
  • Mitochondria: site of aerobic respiration, inner membrane folds (cristae).
  • Compensation point: photosynthesis equals respiration rate.
  • Factors: temperature, oxygen affect respiration rate.
Ch 14: Plant Growth and Development
  • Growth: irreversible increase in size, cell division, elongation.
  • Meristems: apical (length), lateral (girth), intercalary (internodes).
  • Phases: cell division, elongation, differentiation.
  • Plant hormones: auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins, abscisic acid, ethylene.
  • Auxins: promote cell elongation, apical dominance.
  • Gibberellins: stem elongation, seed germination.
  • Cytokinins: cell division, delay senescence.
  • Photoperiodism: response to day length, e.g., flowering.
  • Vernalization: cold treatment promotes flowering.
  • Seed dormancy: prevents germination under unfavorable conditions.
