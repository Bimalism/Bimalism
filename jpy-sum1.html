Physical Quantities

Definition and Classification

Physical quantities are measurable properties of physical systems that can be quantified by measurement and expressed as the product of a numerical value and a unit.

Fundamental Quantities (Base Quantities)

These are independent quantities that cannot be defined in terms of other physical quantities:

Quantity SI Unit Symbol Dimension
Length meter m [L]
Mass kilogram kg [M]
Time second s [T]
Electric Current ampere A [I]
Temperature kelvin K [θ]
Amount of Substance mole mol [N]
Luminous Intensity candela cd [J]

Derived Quantities

These are defined in terms of fundamental quantities through mathematical relationships:

Velocity
v = displacement/time
Dimension: [LT⁻¹]
SI Unit: m/s
Acceleration
a = velocity/time
Dimension: [LT⁻²]
SI Unit: m/s²
Force
F = mass × acceleration
Dimension: [MLT⁻²]
SI Unit: newton (N)
Energy
E = force × distance
Dimension: [ML²T⁻²]
SI Unit: joule (J)
JEE TIP: Always remember that dimensions represent the nature of physical quantity, while units represent its magnitude. Dimensions are independent of the system of units used.

SI Units System

International System of Units

The SI system provides a coherent system of units for international scientific communication. All SI units are based on fundamental constants of nature since the 2019 redefinition.

SI Base Units - Modern Definitions

Quantity Unit Symbol Definition (2019)
Length meter m Distance light travels in 1/299,792,458 seconds
Mass kilogram kg Defined by fixing Planck's constant h
Time second s Duration of 9,192,631,770 periods of Cs-133 radiation

SI Prefixes

Common SI Prefixes:

Tera (T) = 10¹²    Giga (G) = 10⁹    Mega (M) = 10⁶    Kilo (k) = 10³
Centi (c) = 10⁻²    Milli (m) = 10⁻³    Micro (μ) = 10⁻⁶    Nano (n) = 10⁻⁹

Example: Unit Conversion

Problem: Convert 5.6 × 10⁻⁷ m to nanometers.

Solution:
1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m
5.6 × 10⁻⁷ m = 5.6 × 10⁻⁷ / 10⁻⁹ = 560 nm

Dimensional Analysis

Principle of Homogeneity

For an equation to be physically correct, all terms must have the same dimensions.

If: A = B + C + D
Then: [A] = [B] = [C] = [D]

Applications

  • Checking dimensional consistency of equations
  • Deriving relationships between physical quantities
  • Converting units between different systems

Example: Dimensional Analysis

Problem: Check if T = 2π√(l/g) is dimensionally correct.

Solution:
LHS: [T] = [T]
RHS: [2π√(l/g)] = [√([L]/[LT⁻²])] = [√(T²)] = [T]
Equation is dimensionally correct.

JEE TIP: Dimensional analysis cannot determine dimensionless constants or trigonometric relations.

Error Analysis

Types of Errors

Type Description Example
Systematic Error Consistent, reproducible error Zero error in instruments
Random Error Unpredictable fluctuations Parallax error in reading

Error Propagation Formulas

Addition/Subtraction: ΔZ = ΔA + ΔB
Multiplication/Division: ΔZ/Z = ΔA/A + ΔB/B
Power: ΔZ/Z = n(ΔA/A)

Example: Error Propagation

Problem: T = 2π√(l/g). Errors in l and g are 2% and 3%. Find error in T.

Solution:
ΔT/T = ½(Δl/l) + ½(Δg/g) = ½(2%) + ½(3%) = 2.5%

Vernier Calipers

Screw Gauge

JEE Practice Questions

Question 1

The period of oscillation of a simple pendulum is T = 2π√(l/g). The measured value of l is 20.0 cm with 1 mm accuracy and time for 100 oscillations is 90 s with 1 s resolution. The accuracy in determination of g is:

Answer: 3%
Solution: Δg/g = Δl/l + 2ΔT/T = (0.1/20.0) + 2×(1/90) = 0.005 + 0.0222 = 0.0272 ≈ 2.72% ≈ 3%

Question 2

A screw gauge has a pitch of 1 mm and 100 divisions on circular scale. In measuring the diameter of a wire, the main scale reads 3 mm and the 45th division coincides with the reference line. If there is no zero error, the diameter of the wire is:

Answer: 3.45 mm
Solution: LC = 1/100 = 0.01 mm, Reading = 3 + (45 × 0.01) = 3.45 mm
EXAM STRATEGY: Always show your working clearly. Even if the final answer is wrong, you can get partial credit for correct methodology. Manage your time - spend about 2-3 minutes per question in JEE Main.
