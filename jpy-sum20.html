1. Earth's Atmosphere and Electromagnetic Waves

1.1 Composition of Earth's Atmosphere

The earth's atmosphere mainly consists of:

  • Nitrogen: 78%
  • Oxygen: 21%
  • Other gases: Argon, carbon dioxide, water vapor, hydrocarbons, sulfur compounds, and dust particles

1.3 Greenhouse Effect

The atmosphere is transparent to visible radiations, but most infrared (heat) radiations are not allowed to pass through. The energy from the sun heats the earth which then starts emitting radiations like any other hot body. However, since the earth is much colder than sun, its radiations are mainly in the infra-red region. These radiations are unable to cross the lower atmosphere and are reflected back. Low lying clouds also reflect back the infra-red radiations. As such, the earth's surface warms at night. This phenomenon is called the Greenhouse effect.

2. Propagation of Radio Waves

2.1 Low Frequency Waves (AM Band)

Radio waves at frequencies from 10 meters or more wavelengths (frequency less than 30 MHz) is called the formation of the AM band.

Key Points:

  • Lower atmosphere is transparent for these waves, but the ionosphere reflects them back.
  • A signal transmitted from a fixed point to another point can be received in two possible ways:
    • Ground wave: Immediately after reflection from the Earth's surface
    • Sky wave: Reflection from the ionosphere
  • Waves with frequency of about 1500 kHz (wavelengths above 200 m) are transmitted primarily through the ground.
  • Low-frequency sky waves lose their energy much quicker than higher frequency sky waves.
  • Higher frequencies are mainly transmitted through the sky.

2.2 High Frequency Waves (Television Transmission)

Above the frequency of approximately 40MHz, the ionosphere reflects the earth wave. The television signal is frequency of 100-200 MHz.

Key Points:

  • TV broadcasting through the sky is not possible — only direct reception through the ground is possible.
  • To have larger coverage, transmission has to be done through very long antennas.

2.3 Height of Transmitting Antenna for TV Telecast

\[h = \frac{d^2}{2R_e}\]

Where:

  • \( h \) = height of transmitting antenna
  • \( d \) = radius of the area to be covered for TV telecast
  • \( R_e \) = radius of the Earth

3. Types of Modulation

Modulation is the process of transmitting high frequency signals to long distances by superimposing on a high frequency wave which acts as carrier of the information.

3.1 For Sinusoidal Continuous Carrier Wave

(A) Amplitude Modulation (AM)

When the amplitude of high frequency carrier wave is changed in accordance with the intensity of the modulating signal, it is called amplitude modulation.

Modulation factor, (\(m\)) = \(\frac{\text{Amplitude change of carrier wave}}{\text{Amplitude of normal carrier wave}}\)

(B) Frequency Modulation (FM)

When the frequency of carrier wave is changed in accordance with the instantaneous value of the (modulating) signal it is called frequency modulation.

\[m_f = \frac{\text{maximum frequency deviation}}{\text{modulating frequency}}\]

(C) Phase Modulation (PM)

Here the phase angle \(\phi\) of the carrier signal varies in accordance with the modulating voltage.

3.2 For Pulsed Carrier Waves

Types of Pulse Modulation:

  • Pulse amplitude modulation (PAM)
  • Pulse width modulation (PWM) or pulse duration modulation
  • Pulse position modulation (PPM)
  • Pulse code modulation (PCM)

PCM is the preferred modulation scheme for digital communication, while others are more suited to analog systems.

4. Communication Channels

4.1 Types of Communication

(i) Line Communication

The oldest point to point communication mode is communication through wires as in earlier telephone and telegraph links.

Types of Line Communication:

  • Two wire transmission lines
  • Coaxial cables
  • Optical fibres

(ii) Space Communication

A new dimension recently added to the space communication is satellite communication.

4.2 Two Wire Transmission Lines

(a) Parallel Wire Lines

The parallel wire line is used where balanced properties are required.

System of conductors radiates \( RF \) energy if the conductor separation becomes appreciably approaching a wavelength half that of the operating frequency.

(b) Twisted Pair Wires Lines

Twisting helps in minimizing electrical interference. They cannot transmit signals over very large distances.

4.3 Co-axial Wire Lines

These wires are used when unbalanced properties are needed. Often used to interconnect a transmitter and an earthed antenna. Co-axial line wires can be used for microwaves and ultra-high frequency waves (1 GHz).

5. Optical Communication

5.1 Advantages of Optical Communication

Key Advantages:

  1. Wide channel bandwidth and large channel carrying capacity because of the use of higher frequencies - \( 10^{14} \, \text{Hz} \) as compared to the electronic communication links.
  2. Low transmission losses. In optical fibres losses per km are less.
  3. Signal security and not accessible to interference. The optical signals is confined to the inside of fibre and cannot be tempered easily. So secret information like banking, defence etc. is more secure.

Optical communication is through carrier optical signals. Easily accessible optical frequencies lie in the range \( 10^{12} \) to \( 10^{16} \, \text{Hz} \). It is very high as compared to the radio frequencies (\( 10^6 \) to \( 10^8 \, \text{Hz} \)) or microwaves (\( 10^9 \) to \( 10^{11} \, \text{Hz} \)).

5.2 Components of an Optical Communication Link

(i) Optical Source and Modulator

Light sources which can be modulated by our information carrying signal.

(ii) Optical Signal Detector or Photo Detector

The optical signal reaching the receiving end has to be detected by a detector which converts light into electrical signal so that the transmitted information may be decoded.

(iii) Cables which can carry optical signal

Light cannot travel along a metallic wire or cable but can pass through a transparent glass, polymers or dielectric. Therefore, optical transmission line called optical fibre is made from any of these materials. Total internal reflection is the principle behind fibre optics.

5.3 Optical Fibre Structure

An optical fibre consists typically of a transparent core fibre of glass of refractive index \( n_1 \) surrounded by a transparent glass sheath or cladding of slightly lower refractive index \( n_2 \), with both enclosed in an opaque protective jacket.

5.4 Acceptance Angle Formula

\[\sin \theta_e = \frac{\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}}{n_0}\]

Where:

  • \( \theta_e \) = entrance angle on the axis of the core
  • \( n_1 \) = refractive index of core
  • \( n_2 \) = refractive index of cladding
  • \( n_0 \) = refractive index of external medium

For air as the external medium (\(n_0 = 1\)), equation reduces to:

\[\sin \theta_e = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}\]
