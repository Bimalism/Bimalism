1. Equation of State of a Perfect Gas

1Ideal gas is a theoretical gas composed of randomly moving point particles with no intermolecular forces.
2Boyle's Law (1662): PV = constant at constant temperature (T).
3Charles's Law (1787): V/T = constant at constant pressure (P).
4Gay-Lussac's Law (1808): P/T = constant at constant volume (V).
5Avogadro's Law (1811): Equal volumes of gases at same T and P contain equal number of molecules.
6Ideal gas equation: PV = nRT, where n = number of moles, R = universal gas constant.
7Alternative form: PV = NkT, where N = number of molecules, k = Boltzmann constant.
8R = 8.314 J/mol·K = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K = 2 cal/mol·K.
9k = R/N_A = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K, where N_A = Avogadro's number = 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹.
10Molar volume at STP (0°C, 1 atm) = 22.4 L for all ideal gases.
11Dalton's Law: Total pressure of gas mixture = sum of partial pressures.
12Ideal gas approximation works best at high temperatures and low pressures.
13Real gases deviate from ideal behavior at high pressures and low temperatures.
14Van der Waals equation corrects for molecular size and intermolecular forces.
15Critical temperature is the highest temperature at which a gas can be liquefied.
16Compressibility factor Z = PV/(nRT) = 1 for ideal gas.
17For real gases, Z < 1 at low T (attractive forces dominate), Z > 1 at high P (repulsive forces dominate).
18Boyle temperature: Temperature at which real gas behaves ideally over wide pressure range.
19Reduced variables: P_r = P/P_c, V_r = V/V_c, T_r = T/T_c for corresponding states principle.
20Ideal gas law can be derived from kinetic theory of gases.

2. Work Done on Compressing a Gas

21Thermodynamic work: W = ∫ P dV (area under P-V curve).
22Sign convention: Work done BY system is positive, work done ON system is negative.
23Isochoric process (ΔV = 0): W = 0, no work done as volume constant.
24Isobaric process (ΔP = 0): W = PΔV = nRΔT.
25Isothermal process (ΔT = 0): W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁).
26For isothermal compression (V₂ < V₁), work is negative (done on system).
27For isothermal expansion (V₂ > V₁), work is positive (done by system).
28Adiabatic process (Q = 0): W = (P₁V₁ - P₂V₂)/(γ-1) = nR(T₁ - T₂)/(γ-1).
29Polytropic process: W = (P₁V₁ - P₂V₂)/(n-1) where n is polytropic index.
30In P-V diagram, area enclosed by cycle = net work done per cycle.
31For same initial and final states, isothermal work > adiabatic work in expansion.
32For same compression ratio, adiabatic work > isothermal work on gas.
33Free expansion: W = 0 (gas expands into vacuum without doing work).
34Work done depends on path, not just initial and final states.
35In cyclic process, net work = area enclosed in P-V diagram.
36For irreversible processes, work calculation requires knowledge of actual P-V relationship.
37Work done in isobaric process appears as rectangle on P-V diagram.
38Work done in isothermal process involves logarithmic function.
39Adiabatic work leads to temperature change even without heat transfer.
40For ideal gas, internal energy change depends only on temperature, not on work path.

3. Kinetic Theory of Gases – Assumptions

41Kinetic theory explains macroscopic gas properties from molecular behavior.
42Postulate 1: Gas consists of large number of identical molecules (N ∼ 10²³).
43Postulate 2: Molecules are point masses with negligible volume compared to container.
44Postulate 3: Molecules are in constant, random, straight-line motion.
45Postulate 4: Collisions between molecules and with walls are perfectly elastic.
46Postulate 5: No intermolecular forces except during collisions.
47Postulate 6: Time of collision is negligible compared to time between collisions.
48Postulate 7: Molecules obey Newton's laws of motion.
49Pressure arises from molecular collisions with container walls.
50Derived pressure formula: P = (1/3) ρ where ρ = density.
51Alternative form: P = (1/3) (N/V) m .
52Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution describes velocity distribution of molecules.
53Distribution function: f(v) = 4π (m/2πkT)^(3/2) v² exp(-mv²/2kT).
54At higher temperatures, distribution broadens and shifts to higher velocities.
55Lighter gases have broader velocity distributions at same temperature.
56Kinetic theory successfully explains gas laws, diffusion, viscosity, and thermal conduction.
57Brownian motion provides experimental evidence for kinetic theory.
58Einstein's explanation of Brownian motion (1905) confirmed atomic theory.
59Mean free path concept emerges naturally from kinetic theory.
60Kinetic theory connects microscopic molecular properties to macroscopic thermodynamics.

4. Kinetic Energy and Temperature

61Average translational kinetic energy per molecule = (3/2) kT.
62Total translational KE of N molecules = (3/2) NkT = (3/2) nRT.
63Temperature is a measure of average molecular kinetic energy.
64At same temperature, all gases have same average translational KE per molecule.
65Root mean square speed: v_rms = √(3kT/m) = √(3RT/M).
66Average speed: = √(8kT/πm) = √(8RT/πM) ≈ 0.921 v_rms.
67Most probable speed: v_mp = √(2kT/m) = √(2RT/M) ≈ 0.816 v_rms.
68Speed ratio: v_rms : : v_mp = √3 : √(8/π) : √2 ≈ 1.732 : 1.596 : 1.414.
69Molecular speeds are proportional to √T and inversely proportional to √M.
70At 0 K (theoretically), all molecular motion ceases (Third Law of Thermodynamics).
71For air (M ≈ 29 g/mol) at 300 K: v_rms ≈ 500 m/s, ≈ 460 m/s, v_mp ≈ 410 m/s.
72For hydrogen (M = 2 g/mol) at 300 K: v_rms ≈ 1900 m/s (much faster than air).
73Escape velocity from Earth ≈ 11.2 km/s; light gases escape more easily from atmospheres.
74Kinetic energy is equally distributed among x, y, z directions: (1/2)mv_x² = (1/2)kT.
75Pressure can be expressed as P = (2/3) × (translational KE per unit volume).
76Maxwell's demon thought experiment challenges Second Law of Thermodynamics.
77Equipartition theorem extends kinetic theory to rotational and vibrational energies.
78Temperature in kinetic theory is measured in Kelvin, starting from absolute zero.
79At higher altitudes, temperature decreases but rms speed depends only on T, not P.
80Sound speed in gases is close to average molecular speed (v_sound ≈ √(γRT/M)).

5. Degrees of Freedom

81Degrees of freedom = number of independent ways a molecule can store energy.
82Translational degrees: 3 (motion along x, y, z axes).
83Rotational degrees: 2 for linear molecules, 3 for nonlinear molecules.
84Vibrational degrees: 1 per vibrational mode (kinetic + potential = 2 contributions).
85Monatomic gases (He, Ne, Ar): f = 3 (only translational).
86Diatomic gases at room temperature (N₂, O₂, H₂): f = 5 (3 trans + 2 rot).
87Diatomic gases at high temperature: f = 7 (adds 1 vibrational mode = 2 contributions).
88Linear triatomic (CO₂, N₂O): f = 5 at room temp (3 trans + 2 rot), f = 7 at high temp.
89Nonlinear triatomic (H₂O, O₃): f = 6 at room temp (3 trans + 3 rot), f = 9 at high temp.
90Polyatomic molecules: generally f = 3N, where N = number of atoms.
91At very low temperatures, rotational and vibrational modes freeze out.
92Characteristic temperatures: Θ_rot = ħ²/(2Ik) for rotation, Θ_vib = ħω/k for vibration.
93For H₂, Θ_rot ≈ 85 K, Θ_vib ≈ 6100 K; for N₂, Θ_rot ≈ 2.9 K, Θ_vib ≈ 3400 K.
94At T ≪ Θ, modes are frozen; at T ≈ Θ, modes partially excited; at T ≫ Θ, fully excited.
95Internal energy U = (f/2) nRT, where f includes all active degrees of freedom.
96For solids, atoms vibrate in 3D: f = 6 (3 kinetic + 3 potential from vibrations).
97Dulong-Petit law: Molar heat capacity of solids ≈ 3R ≈ 25 J/mol·K at high T.
98Einstein model and Debye model improve specific heat predictions for solids.
99Degrees of freedom concept applies to any system with quadratic energy terms.
100Equipartition theorem: Each quadratic term in energy contributes (1/2)kT.

6. Law of Equipartition of Energy & Specific Heat Capacity

101Equipartition theorem: Each degree of freedom contributes (1/2)kT energy per molecule.
102For f active degrees: U = (f/2) nRT, C_v = (f/2) R, C_p = C_v + R = [(f/2)+1]R.
103γ = C_p/C_v = 1 + 2/f.
104Monatomic gas: f=3 → C_v = 3R/2, C_p = 5R/2, γ = 5/3 ≈ 1.67.
105Diatomic at room T: f=5 → C_v = 5R/2, C_p = 7R/2, γ = 7/5 = 1.4.
106Diatomic at high T: f=7 → C_v = 7R/2, C_p = 9R/2, γ = 9/7 ≈ 1.286.
107Nonlinear polyatomic: f=6 → C_v = 3R, C_p = 4R, γ = 4/3 ≈ 1.33.
108Mayer's relation: C_p - C_v = R for ideal gases (derivable from equipartition).
109Adiabatic index γ appears in adiabatic equation: PV^γ = constant.
110Speed of sound in gas: v_sound = √(γRT/M) ∝ √(γT/M).
111For monatomic gases, γ is highest, so sound speed is highest for given T, M.
112Specific heat ratio γ can be measured experimentally using Clément-Desormes method.
113At very low temperatures, C_v → 0 as T → 0 (Third Law of Thermodynamics).
114Quantum mechanics explains why rotational/vibrational modes freeze at low T.
115For real gases, C_p - C_v > R due to volume expansion work against intermolecular forces.
116Specific heat at constant volume: C_v = (∂U/∂T)_v.
117Specific heat at constant pressure: C_p = (∂H/∂T)_p where H = U + PV.
118Ratio γ determines whether adiabatic curve is steeper than isothermal on P-V diagram.
119For air (mostly diatomic), γ ≈ 1.4, used in many engineering calculations.
120Equipartition fails for low-T specific heats, requiring quantum statistical mechanics.

7. Concept of Mean Free Path

121Mean free path λ = average distance traveled by molecule between successive collisions.
122Derivation: λ = 1/(√2 π d² n) where d = molecular diameter, n = number density.
123Alternative form: λ = kT/(√2 π d² P) since n = P/(kT).
124λ ∝ 1/P (inversely proportional to pressure at constant T).
125λ ∝ T (directly proportional to temperature at constant P).
126At STP, λ ≈ 6.6 × 10⁻⁸ m for air molecules (d ≈ 3.7 × 10⁻¹⁰ m).
127Collision frequency Z = average number of collisions per molecule per second = /λ.
128Z = √2 π d² n = √2 π d² n √(8kT/πm).
129At STP, Z ≈ 5 × 10⁹ s⁻¹ for air (each molecule collides ~5 billion times per second!).
130Total collision rate per unit volume = (1/2) n Z (factor 1/2 avoids double counting).
131Knudsen number Kn = λ/L, where L = characteristic length of container.
132Kn ≪ 1: continuum flow; Kn ≫ 1: molecular flow (Knudsen regime).
133In high vacuum (P ∼ 10⁻⁶ torr), λ can be meters or kilometers.
134Mean free path determines transport properties: viscosity, thermal conductivity, diffusion.
135Viscosity η = (1/3) ρ λ, independent of pressure at moderate densities.
136Thermal conductivity κ = (1/3) ρ C_v λ.
137Diffusion coefficient D = (1/3) λ.
138At very low pressures, λ exceeds container size, and transport properties become pressure-dependent.
139In the upper atmosphere, λ becomes large, leading to different fluid dynamics.
140Mean free path concept is crucial in semiconductor fabrication (clean rooms, vacuum chambers).

100+ Non-Repeatable Points - Complete Summary

141Real gases obey ideal gas law only at low pressures and high temperatures.
142Van der Waals constants: 'a' corrects for intermolecular attraction, 'b' for molecular volume.
143Virial equation: PV = nRT(1 + B/V + C/V² + ...), where B, C are virial coefficients.
144Critical point: (∂P/∂V)_T = 0 and (∂²P/∂V²)_T = 0, defines P_c, V_c, T_c.
145Law of corresponding states: All gases have same reduced equation of state.
146Maxwell construction fixes the unphysical loops in van der Waals isotherms below T_c.
147Joule-Thomson coefficient μ = (∂T/∂P)_H determines cooling/warming during throttling.
148Inversion temperature: μ = 0, above which gas warms upon expansion.
149For ideal gas, μ = 0; for real gases, μ > 0 below inversion temperature (cooling).
150Liquefaction of gases utilizes Joule-Thomson effect (Linde process).
151Adiabatic demagnetization achieves temperatures below 1 K.
152Mean free path in mixtures: λ = 1/[√2 π (n₁d₁² + n₂d₂² + ...)].
153Graham's law of effusion: Rate ∝ 1/√M; lighter gases effuse faster.
154Effusion: gas escapes through small hole (hole diameter ≪ λ).
155Diffusion: gas spreads through another gas (governed by Fick's laws).
156Thermal transpiration: pressure difference develops between chambers at different T.
157Barometric formula: P = P₀ exp(-Mgh/RT), pressure decreases exponentially with height.
158Isothermal atmosphere: scale height h₀ = RT/(Mg).
159For Earth's atmosphere, h₀ ≈ 8.5 km (varies with T and gas composition).
