1. Tetravalency of Carbon & Hybridization

  • Carbon has electronic configuration 1s² 2s² 2p², atomic number 6
  • Tetravalent: forms 4 covalent bonds to achieve noble gas configuration
  • sp³ hybridization → tetrahedral (109.5°), CH₄, C₂H₆
  • sp² hybridization → trigonal planar (120°), C₂H₄, benzene
  • sp hybridization → linear (180°), C₂H₂, CO₂
  • Sigma (σ) bond: head-on overlap, pi (π) bond: side-wise overlap
  • Single bond: 1σ, double bond: 1σ + 1π, triple bond: 1σ + 2π
  • Bond length: sp > sp² > sp (more s-character → shorter bond)
  • Bond strength: sp > sp² > sp³ (more s-character → stronger bond)
  • JEE loves: Predict geometry and hybridization of carbon in different compounds

2. Classification of Organic Compounds & Functional Groups

  • Functional group: atom/group responsible for chemical properties
  • Halogen: -X (haloalkanes, haloarenes)
  • Oxygen: alcohols (-OH), ethers (-O-), aldehydes (-CHO), ketones (>C=O), carboxylic acids (-COOH), esters (-COOR)
  • Nitrogen: amines (-NH₂, -NHR, -NR₂), nitriles (-C≡N), nitro (-NO₂)
  • Sulphur: thiols (-SH), thioethers (-SR)
  • Hydrocarbons: alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, aromatic
  • Priority order for naming: -COOH > -SO₃H > esters > acid halides > amides > nitriles > aldehydes > ketones > alcohols > amines > ethers
  • Compounds with C=C, C≡C are unsaturated
  • Aromatic compounds follow Hückel’s rule (4n+2 π electrons)
  • JEE asks: Identify functional group and class of compound

3. Homologous Series

  • Series of compounds with same functional group and similar chemical properties
  • Each member differs by -CH₂- unit
  • General formula: CnH₂n+₂ (alkanes), CnH₂n (alkenes), CnH₂n-₂ (alkynes)
  • Gradation in physical properties (MP, BP increase with molecular mass)
  • Chemical properties almost same within series
  • Example: CH₄, C₂H₆, C₃H₈, C₄H₁₀… (alkanes)
  • Alcohols: CH₃OH, C₂H₅OH, C₃H₇OH…
  • First member often shows slightly different properties
  • Can be prepared by similar methods
  • JEE loves: Identify next member or general formula

4. Isomerism – Structural & Stereoisomerism

  • Compounds with same molecular formula but different arrangement
  • Structural isomerism: chain, position, functional, metamerism, tautomerism
  • Stereoisomerism: geometrical (cis-trans), optical (enantiomers, diastereomers)
  • Chain: n-pentane, isopentane, neopentane
  • Position: 1-chloropropane, 2-chloropropane
  • Functional: C₃H₆O → propanone or propanal
  • Geometrical isomerism in alkenes (restricted rotation around C=C)
  • Optical isomerism: chiral carbon (4 different groups), non-superimposable mirror images
  • Racemic mixture: 1:1 enantiomers, optically inactive
  • JEE favourite: Number of structural/optical isomers of C₄H₁₀, C₅H₁₂, etc.

5. Nomenclature – Trivial & IUPAC

  • IUPAC: longest continuous carbon chain as parent
  • Number chain to give lowest number to functional group/substituent
  • Prefixes: methyl, ethyl, chloro, bromo, nitro, etc.
  • Alkanes → -ane, alkenes → -ene, alkynes → -yne
  • Alcohols → -ol, aldehydes → -al, ketones → -one, acids → -oic acid
  • Multiple same substituents: di-, tri-, tetra-
  • Complex substituents in brackets: (1-methylethyl)
  • Aromatic: benzene, toluene, aniline, phenol, benzoic acid
  • Lowest sum rule for multiple double/triple bonds
  • JEE loves: Write IUPAC name of complex branched compounds

6. Covalent Bond Fission, Reactive Intermediates

  • Homolytic fission: equal sharing → free radicals (one electron each)
  • Heterolytic fission: unequal → carbocation & carbanion
  • Free radical: odd electron, highly reactive, planar
  • Carbocation: positive charge on C, sp², planar
  • Carbanion: negative charge on C, sp³, pyramidal
  • Stability of carbocation: 3° > 2° > 1° > CH₃⁺
  • Stability of free radical: 3° > 2° > 1°
  • Electrophiles: electron deficient (H⁺, NO₂⁺, carbocation, BF₃)
  • Nucleophiles: electron rich (OH⁻, CN⁻, carbanion, amines)
  • JEE asks: Most stable carbocation among given options

7. Electronic Displacement Effects

  • Inductive effect: permanent, through σ bonds, -I and +I groups
  • -I order: -NR₃⁺ > -NO₂ > -CN > -COOH > -F > -Cl > -Br > -I > -OCH₃
  • Electromeric effect: temporary, in presence of reagent, +E or –E
  • Resonance: delocalization of π electrons, stability to benzene, carboxylate ion
  • Resonance increases stability, decreases reactivity
  • Hyperconjugation: delocalization of σ electrons (in alkenes, carbocations)
  • Hyperconjugation ∝ number of α-hydrogens
  • Also called no-bond resonance or Baker-Nathan effect
  • Resonance > Hyperconjugation > Inductive effect in stability
  • JEE favourite: Stability of carbocations using hyperconjugation/resonance

8. Common Types of Organic Reactions

  • Substitution: one group replaced by another (nucleophilic, electrophilic, free radical)
  • Addition: across multiple bonds (electrophilic, nucleophilic, free radical)
  • Elimination: removal of small molecule to form multiple bond (E1, E2)
  • Rearrangement: migration of group to electron-deficient site
  • Nucleophilic substitution: SN1 (carbocation intermediate), SN2 (concerted)
  • Electrophilic aromatic substitution (ArSE): nitration, halogenation, sulphonation
  • Addition in alkenes: Markovnikov’s rule, anti-Markovnikov (peroxide effect)
  • Elimination: Zaitsev’s rule (major product more substituted alkene)
  • Rearrangement examples: Beckmann, Curtius, Hofmann
  • JEE loves: Identify type of reaction and major product

9. JEE Must-Revise Points (High Weightage)

  • Stability: 3° carbocation > 3° free radical > resonance-stabilized > hyperconjugation
  • Hybridization: sp (50%) > sp² (33%) > sp³ (25%) s-character
  • Acidity: -COOH > phenol > H₂O > alcohol > alkyne > alkene > alkane
  • Markovnikov: H adds to carbon with more H, anti in HBr + peroxide
  • IUPAC: lowest number to functional group, then multiple bonds, then substituents
  • Resonance in benzene: 2 equivalent structures, bond length 139 pm
  • Optical isomers: 2ⁿ (n = chiral carbons), meso if plane of symmetry
  • SN1 rate depends only on substrate, SN2 on both substrate & nucleophile
  • Hyperconjugation explains o/p directing nature of alkyl groups
  • Number of hyperconjugative structures = number of α-hydrogens
