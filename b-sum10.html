1. Organisms and Environment & Population Attributes

  • Ecology is the study of interactions among organisms and between organisms and their physical environment.
  • Population: Group of individuals of same species living in a well-defined area at a given time.
  • Natality (birth rate): Number of births per unit population per unit time.
  • Mortality (death rate): Number of deaths per unit population per unit time.
  • Immigration: Influx of individuals into the population; Emigration: Exit of individuals.
  • Population growth = (Births + Immigration) – (Deaths + Emigration).
  • Age pyramid: Graphical representation of age distribution (pre-reproductive, reproductive, post-reproductive).
  • Exponential growth (J-shaped): dN/dt = rN (unlimited resources).
  • Logistic growth (S-shaped): dN/dt = rN (K–N)/K; K = carrying capacity.
  • Population size regulated by density-dependent (competition, predation) and density-independent factors (weather).

2. Population Interactions

  • Mutualism (+/+): Both species benefit (e.g., lichen, pollinators & plants, mycorrhiza).
  • Competition (–/–): Both harmed (intraspecific or interspecific); Gause’s principle – complete competitors cannot coexist.
  • Predation (+/–): Predator kills and eats prey (helps in biological control).
  • Herbivory (+/–): Herbivore eats plant parts.
  • Parasitism (+/–): Parasite benefits, host harmed (ecto/endo-parasites, brood parasitism in cuckoo).
  • Commensalism (+/0): One benefits, other unaffected (e.g., orchid on tree, clownfish & anemone).
  • Amensalism (–/0): One harmed, other unaffected (e.g., Penicillium secretes penicillin).
  • Predation maintains ecosystem balance and evolution of defense mechanisms (camouflage, mimicry).
  • Coevolution seen in predator-prey and host-parasite relationships.
  • Population interactions affect distribution, abundance and evolution of species.

3. Ecosystem – Patterns, Components

  • Ecosystem: Functional unit of nature where living organisms interact with each other and physical environment.
  • Abiotic components: Temperature, light, water, soil, nutrients.
  • Biotic components: Producers (autotrophs), Consumers (primary, secondary, tertiary), Decomposers.
  • Pond, forest, grassland, desert are examples of ecosystems.
  • Stratification: Vertical distribution of species (e.g., in forest: trees, shrubs, herbs).
  • Ecosystems show homeostasis through feedback mechanisms.
  • Four basic components: productivity, decomposition, energy flow, nutrient cycling.
  • Ecological succession: Sequential change in community structure over time.
  • Primary succession: On bare rock; Secondary succession: On previously vegetated area.
  • Ecosystems vary in structure but all have same function (energy flow unidirectional, nutrients cyclic).

4. Productivity & Decomposition

  • Primary productivity: Rate of biomass production by producers (GPP & NPP).
  • GPP = Gross Primary Productivity; NPP = GPP – Respiration (R).
  • Secondary productivity: Rate of biomass formation at consumer level.
  • NPP highest in tropical rainforests, lowest in deserts.
  • Decomposition: Breakdown of dead organic matter by decomposers (bacteria, fungi).
  • Steps: Fragmentation → Leaching → Catabolism → Humification → Mineralisation.
  • Detritivores (earthworms, termites) speed up fragmentation.
  • Rate of decomposition depends on temperature, moisture, oxygen, and quality of detritus.
  • Warm and moist environment favours fastest decomposition.
  • Humus: Dark, amorphous, highly resistant organic matter; improves soil fertility.

5. Energy Flow & Ecological Pyramids

  • Energy flow is unidirectional (sun → producers → consumers).
  • Only ~1% of solar energy is captured by plants.
  • 10% law (Lindeman): Only 10% energy transferred to next trophic level.
  • Pyramid of number: Upright in grassland, inverted in single tree ecosystem.
  • Pyramid of biomass: Upright in terrestrial, inverted in aquatic (phytoplankton → zooplankton).
  • Pyramid of energy: Always upright (energy decreases at successive levels).
  • Trophic levels: Producers (T1), Herbivores (T2), Carnivores (T3, T4).
  • Food chain: Grazing (GFC) and Detritus (DFC); DFC more stable.
  • Food web: Interconnected food chains; increases stability.
  • Standing crop: Biomass or number of organisms at a given time in a trophic level.

6. Biodiversity – Concept & Patterns

  • Biodiversity: Total variability at species, genetic and ecosystem level.
  • Three types: Genetic, Species, Ecological biodiversity.
  • Species richness increases from poles Crystals to equator (latitudinal gradient).
  • Species-area relationship: S = c A^z (z = 0.1–0.2 for small areas, 0.6–1.2 for large).
  • Earth has ~1.7–1.8 million species identified; actual may be 7–8 million.
  • India has ~8.1% of world’s biodiversity (2.4% land area).
  • Alpha diversity: Diversity within community; Beta: Between communities; Gamma: Regional.
  • Rivet popper hypothesis (Paul Ehrlich): Loss of species like rivets weakens ecosystem.
  • David Tilman: Plots with more species showed less year-to-year variation in biomass.
  • More diverse communities are more productive and stable.

7. Importance & Loss of Biodiversity

  • Ecosystem services: Pollination, oxygen production, climate regulation, nutrient cycling.
  • Narrowly utilitarian: Food, fibre, fuel, drugs (25% medicines from plants).
  • Broadly utilitarian: Aesthetic, cultural, ethical values.
  • Ethical argument: We have moral duty to care for all species.
  • Causes of loss: Habitat loss, over-exploitation, alien species, co-extinctions.
  • Evil quartet: Habitat loss & fragmentation, Over-exploitation, Alien species invasion, Co-extinction.
  • Current extinction rate 100–1000 times higher than natural (Sixth mass extinction).
  • IUCN categories: Extinct, Extinct in wild, Critically endangered, Endangered, Vulnerable, etc.
  • Red Data Book: Records endangered plants and animals.
  • Endangered species in India: Tiger, Rhino, Great Indian Bustard, Asiatic Lion, etc.

8. Biodiversity Conservation

  • In-situ conservation: On-site (National parks, Sanctuaries, Biosphere reserves, Sacred groves).
  • Ex-situ conservation: Off-site (Zoos, Botanical gardens, Seed banks, Cryopreservation).
  • India has 18 Biosphere reserves (e.g., Nilgiri, Sunderbans, Nanda Devi).
  • Hotspots: Regions with high species richness & endemism under threat (36 global, 4 in India).
  • India’s hotspots: Western Ghats & Sri Lanka, Indo-Burma, Himalaya, Sundaland.
  • National parks: No human activity allowed except tourism (e.g., Jim Corbett, Kaziranga).
  • Wildlife sanctuaries: Limited human activity allowed (e.g., Mudumalai).
  • Sacred groves: Protected by local communities (e.g., Khasi hills, Western Ghats).
  • Project Tiger (1973), Project Rhino, Project Elephant launched for conservation.
  • Wildlife Protection Act 1972 provides legal protection.
