1. Oscillations

If a particle in periodic motion moves back and forth (or to and fro) over the same path, then its motion is called oscillatory or vibratory.

1.1 Characteristics of Harmonic Motion

Amplitude (A)

The amplitude of oscillations is the maximum displacement of a vibrating body from the position of equilibrium.

Time Period (T)

The time period of oscillations is defined as the time between two successive identical positions passed by the body in the same direction.

Frequency (f)

The frequency of oscillations is the number of cycles of vibrations of a body completed in one second.

\[f = \frac{1}{T}\]

The SI unit of frequency is s⁻¹ or Hz (hertz).

2. Simple Harmonic Motion

Let us consider an oscillatory particle along a straight line whose potential energy function varies as:

\[U(x) = \frac{1}{2} kx^2\]

where k is a constant.

2.1 General Equation of SHM

\[x = A \sin(\omega t + \phi)\]

This is the general equation of SHM, where:

  • A = amplitude
  • ω = angular frequency
  • φ = phase constant

2.2 Differential Equation of SHM

\[\frac{d^2 x}{dt^2} = -\omega^2 x \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{d^2 x}{dt^2} + \omega^2 x = 0\]

This is the standard differential equation of SHM.

2.3 Spring-Mass System

\[T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}\]

Where T is the time period, m is the mass, and k is the spring constant.

2.4 Spring Combinations

Series Combination
\[\frac{1}{k} = \frac{1}{k_1} + \frac{1}{k_2} \Rightarrow k = \frac{k_1 k_2}{k_1 + k_2}\]

Equivalent stiffness for springs in series.

Parallel Combination
\[k = k_1 + k_2\]

Equivalent stiffness for springs in parallel.

2.5 Energy Conservation in SHM

In a spring-mass system, the instantaneous potential energy and kinetic energy are:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} kx^2 = \frac{1}{2} kA^2 \sin^2(\omega t + \phi)\] \[K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} m\omega^2 A^2 \cos^2(\omega t + \phi)\]

Since ω² = k/m, the total mechanical energy is:

\[E = K + U = \frac{1}{2} kA^2 [\sin^2(\omega t + \phi) + \cos^2(\omega t + \phi)] = \frac{1}{2} kA^2\]

Thus, the total energy of SHM is constant and proportional to the square of the amplitude.

Key Points:

  • At x = ±A: K = 0, E = Umax = ½kA² (turning points)
  • At x = 0: U = 0, E = Kmax = ½m(ωA)²

3. Waves

3.1 Wave Function

\[y = A \sin[k(x \pm vt)] \quad \text{or} \quad y = A \sin(kx \pm \omega t)\]

The negative sign is used when the wave travels along the positive x-axis, and vice versa.

3.2 Wave Parameters

Wave Number (k)
\[k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\]

where λ is the wavelength.

Angular Frequency (ω)
\[\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f\]

where T is the time period and f is the frequency.

Wave Velocity (v)
\[v = \frac{\lambda}{T} = \lambda f = \frac{\omega}{k}\]
Amplitude (A)

The maximum displacement of a particle on the medium from the equilibrium position.

3.3 Wave Velocity vs Particle Velocity

Wave velocity is the velocity of the disturbance which propagates through a medium. It only depends on the properties of the medium and is independent of time and position.

Particle velocity is the rate at which particle's displacement vary as a function of time:

\[\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = \pm \omega A \cos(kx \pm \omega t + \phi)\]

3.4 Velocity of Transverse Wave on a String

\[v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}\]

Where T is the tension in the string and μ is the mass per unit length of the string.

3.5 Energy Transported by a Harmonic Wave

The average power transmitted by the wave is:

\[P_{av} = \frac{1}{2} \mu \omega^2 A^2 v\]

Where v = dx/dt is the wave velocity.

The intensity of the wave is:

\[I = \frac{P_{av}}{a} = \frac{1}{2} \rho \omega^2 A^2 v\]

Where ρ is the density and a is the cross-sectional area.

4. Interference and Beats

4.1 Interference: Adding Waves with Phase Difference

Consider two waves travelling on the same string with the same amplitude, wavelength and frequency but with a constant phase difference:

\[y_1 = a \sin(kx - \omega t)\] \[y_2 = a \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi)\] \[y = y_1 + y_2 = a [\sin(kx - \omega t) + \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi)]\]

Using trigonometric identity:

\[y = \left( 2a \cos \frac{\phi}{2} \right) \sin \left( kx - \omega t - \frac{\phi}{2} \right)\]

The term \(2a \cos(\phi/2)\) is interpreted as the new amplitude of the wave:

\[y = A \sin \left( kx - \omega t - \frac{\phi}{2} \right)\] \[\text{where } A = 2a \cos \left( \frac{\phi}{2} \right)\]

4.2 Constructive & Destructive Interference

Constructive Interference

When φ = 2nπ (where n = 0, 1, 2, ...):

\[A_{max} = a_1 + a_2\]

For intensity:

\[I_{max} = (\sqrt{I_1} + \sqrt{I_2})^2\]
Destructive Interference

When φ = (2n - 1)π (where n = 1, 2, 3, ...):

\[A_{min} = |a_1 - a_2|\]

For intensity:

\[I_{min} = (\sqrt{I_1} - \sqrt{I_2})^2\]

4.3 General Case for Different Amplitudes

\[A = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + 2a_1a_2 \cos\phi}\] \[I = I_1 + I_2 + 2\sqrt{I_1I_2} \cos\phi\]

4.4 Beats

\[f_{beat} = |f_1 - f_2|\]

Where f₁ and f₂ are the frequencies of the two interfering waves.

5. Standing Waves

When two waves identical in all respects, but travelling in opposite direction along a straight line, superimpose on each other, standing waves are produced.

5.1 Formation of Standing Waves

Let y₁ = A sin(ωt - kx) and y₂ = A sin(ωt + kx)

\[y = y_1 + y_2 = 2A \cos(kx) \sin(\omega t)\]

The term 2A cos(kx) represents the amplitude of particle located at position x.

5.2 Nodes and Antinodes

Nodes

Positions where amplitude is always zero:

\[kx = 0, \pi, 2\pi, 3\pi, \ldots\] \[x = 0, \frac{\lambda}{2}, \lambda, \frac{3\lambda}{2}, \ldots\]

Distance between consecutive nodes = λ/2

Antinodes

Positions where amplitude is maximum (2A):

\[kx = \frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{3\pi}{2}, \frac{5\pi}{2}, \ldots\] \[x = \frac{\lambda}{4}, \frac{3\lambda}{4}, \frac{5\lambda}{4}, \ldots\]

Distance between consecutive antinodes = λ/2

5.3 Normal Modes on a String

The standing wave patterns possible on the string are called the normal modes.

\[\lambda = \frac{2L}{n} \quad \text{where } n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\] \[f = n \left( \frac{v}{2L} \right) \quad \text{where } n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\]
Mode Name Frequency
n = 1 Fundamental frequency (1st harmonic) f₁ = v/(2L)
n = 2 1st overtone (2nd harmonic) f₂ = v/L
n = 3 2nd overtone (3rd harmonic) f₃ = 3v/(2L)

5.4 Standing Sound Waves in Pipes

Open Organ Pipe

Both ends are open:

\[\lambda = 2L, L, \frac{2L}{3}, \ldots\] \[f = \frac{v}{2L}, \frac{v}{L}, \frac{3v}{2L}, \ldots\]

All harmonics are present.

Closed Organ Pipe

One end closed, one end open:

\[\lambda = 4L, \frac{4L}{3}, \frac{4L}{5}, \ldots\] \[f = \frac{v}{4L}, \frac{3v}{4L}, \frac{5v}{4L}, \ldots\]

Only odd harmonics are present.

5.5 Intensity Level: The Decibel Scale

\[\beta = 10 \log \frac{I}{I_0}\]

Where I is the measured intensity and I₀ is the reference intensity.

5.6 Doppler Effect

The change in frequency due to the motion of the source, observer, or both is called the Doppler Effect.

Key Formulas:

  • Observer moving toward stationary source: \(f' = f \left( \frac{v + v_o}{v} \right)\)
  • Observer moving away from stationary source: \(f' = f \left( \frac{v - v_o}{v} \right)\)
  • Source moving toward stationary observer: \(f' = f \left( \frac{v}{v - v_s} \right)\)
  • Source moving away from stationary observer: \(f' = f \left( \frac{v}{v + v_s} \right)\)
