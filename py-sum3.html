1. Newton's First Law of Motion

Statement

Every body continues to be in its state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled by an external unbalanced force to change that state.

Law of Inertia: Inertia is the inherent property of a body to oppose any change in its state of rest or uniform motion.

25+ Unique Points on Inertia

Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia
Greater mass → Greater inertia → More difficult to change state
Inertia is not a force but a property of matter
Inertia depends only on mass, not on velocity
Inertia of rest: Body resists change from rest to motion
Example: Dust remains on beaten carpet due to inertia of rest
Coin on card trick demonstrates inertia of rest
Inertia of motion: Body resists change from motion to rest
Example: Passengers jerk forward when bus stops suddenly
Seat belts work based on inertia of motion principle
Inertia of direction: Body resists change in direction of motion
Example: Mud flies tangentially from rotating wheel
Stone from sling moves tangent to circular path when released
First law defines force: Force is that which changes state of motion
Aristotle's fallacy: Force needed to maintain motion (incorrect)
Galileo's experiments led to concept of inertia
On frictionless surface, body moves forever with constant velocity
First law holds only in inertial reference frames
No distinction between rest and uniform motion in physics
Equilibrium when net force = 0 (static or dynamic)
Body at rest has zero velocity, not necessarily zero acceleration
Body in uniform motion has zero acceleration
First law provides qualitative definition of force
Momentum conservation derived from first law symmetry
Rotational inertia (moment of inertia) is rotational analog

Reference Frames

Frame TypeDefinitionNewton's LawsExample
InertialZero acceleration or constant velocityValidEarth (approximately), moving train with constant speed
Non-inertialAccelerating or rotatingNot valid (need pseudo forces)Accelerating car, rotating merry-go-round

Pseudo Force Concept:

In non-inertial frame with acceleration A:

F_pseudo = -m A (applied to all bodies in frame)

Example: In accelerating elevator, apparent weight = m(g + a)

2. Newton's Second Law of Motion

Statement

The rate of change of linear momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied external force and takes place in the direction of force.

F = dp/dt = d(mv)/dt
If mass constant: F = m a
1 Newton = 1 kg·m/s²

20+ Key Points on Second Law

Second law gives quantitative measure of force
Force causes acceleration, not velocity
Acceleration is directly proportional to net force
Acceleration is inversely proportional to mass
Direction of acceleration same as direction of net force
For constant force, acceleration is constant
F = ma is vector equation → apply component-wise
Mass in F = ma is inertial mass (not gravitational)
Principle of superposition: Net force = vector sum
Impulse: J = ∫F dt = Δp = m(v - u)
Impulse-momentum theorem more fundamental than F = ma
For variable mass systems: F_ext + v_rel(dm/dt) = m(dv/dt)
Rocket equation: v = u + v_rel ln(m₀/m)
Second law holds only in inertial reference frames
Force measured in Newtons (N) in SI system
1 N = force that gives 1 kg mass 1 m/s² acceleration
Dyne is CGS unit: 1 N = 10⁵ dyne
Weight: W = mg (g varies with location)
Apparent weight differs in accelerating elevators
Free-body diagrams essential for problem solving

Variable Mass Systems

Rocket Propulsion:

Thrust force: F_thrust = v_rel (dm/dt)

Velocity gained: v = v₀ + v_rel ln(m₀/m)

Burnout velocity depends only on exhaust speed and mass ratio

Multi-stage rockets discard empty mass to improve efficiency

Chain Falling on Scale:

For chain of length L, density ρ falling on scale:

Force = weight of fallen portion + impulse due to impacting links

F = ρgx + ρv² where x is fallen length, v = √(2gx)

3. Newton's Third Law of Motion

Statement

To every action, there is always an equal and opposite reaction.

F_AB = -F_BA (Action-Reaction Pair)

15+ Crucial Points on Action-Reaction

Action and reaction forces act on different bodies
They are always equal in magnitude
They are always opposite in direction
They act simultaneously (no time delay)
They never cancel each other (different bodies)
Nature of forces can be different (contact, field)
Example: Book on table: weight (action) → normal (reaction)
Example: Earth attracts apple → apple attracts Earth
Walking: Push ground backward → ground pushes forward
Swimming: Push water backward → water pushes forward
Rocket: Expel gas backward → gas pushes rocket forward
Recoil of gun: Bullet forward → gun backward
Horse-cart problem: Internal forces don't move system
Tension in rope: Equal at both ends if massless rope
Third law holds for all types of forces

Common Misconceptions Clarified

Horse-Cart Problem: Horse pushes ground backward, ground pushes horse forward. This force moves entire system (horse+cart). Force horse exerts on cart and cart exerts on horse are internal.

Why doesn't action-reaction cancel? They act on different bodies, so each body experiences net force.

Tension in rope: For massless rope, tension same throughout. For rope with mass, tension varies.

4. Conservation of Linear Momentum

Law: If the net external force on a system is zero, the total linear momentum of the system remains constant.
For isolated system: Σp_initial = Σp_final
For two-body collision: m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂

Collision Types & Properties

Coefficient of restitution: e = (v₂ - v₁)/(u₁ - u₂)
= Relative speed after / Relative speed before
0 ≤ e ≤ 1 (e=0: perfectly inelastic, e=1: perfectly elastic)

15+ Applications of Momentum Conservation

Recoil of gun: m_gun v_gun = -m_bullet v_bullet
Rocket propulsion: Momentum conservation in exhaust system
Ballistic pendulum: For bullet speed measurement
Collisions in particle physics: Track momentum to identify particles
Air track experiments: Nearly frictionless collisions
Newton's cradle: Demonstrates elastic collisions
Ice skater pulling arms in: Angular momentum conservation
Jumping off boat: Boat moves backward
Firing from moving platform: Add platform velocity
Breaking glass vs rubber ball: e different
Car crashes: Crumple zones increase collision time
Tennis ball vs cricket ball: Different e values
Bouncing ball: Height reduces by factor e² each bounce
Center of mass motion unaffected by internal forces
Variable mass systems: Use momentum conservation approach

One-Dimensional Elastic Collision Formulas:

For masses m₁, m₂ with initial velocities u₁, u₂:

v₁ = [(m₁ - m₂)u₁ + 2m₂u₂] / (m₁ + m₂)

v₂ = [(m₂ - m₁)u₂ + 2m₁u₁] / (m₁ + m₂)

Special cases: m₁ = m₂ → velocities exchange

5. Static and Kinetic Friction

Types of Friction

TypeFormulaCharacteristicsDirection
Static Frictionf_s ≤ μ_s NSelf-adjusting, prevents motionOpposes impending motion
Limiting Frictionf_L = μ_s NMaximum static frictionAt threshold of motion
Kinetic Frictionf_k = μ_k NConstant during motionOpposes relative motion
Rolling Frictionf_r = μ_r NSmallest, due to deformationOpposes rolling motion
μ_s > μ_k > μ_r (Generally)
Angle of friction: tan λ = μ_s
Angle of repose: θ = λ (tan θ = μ_s)

20+ Friction Facts & Applications

Static friction adjusts from 0 to μ_sN to prevent motion
Kinetic friction constant during motion (μ_kN)
μ_s typically 20-30% greater than μ_k
Friction independent of contact area (for hard surfaces)
Friction depends on nature of surfaces, not weight
Lubrication reduces μ by introducing fluid layer
Static friction can act even when no relative motion
Walking possible due to static friction
Braking: Static friction for maximum deceleration
Skidding occurs when kinetic friction takes over
Angle of repose = maximum slope without sliding
Sand heaps naturally at angle of repose
Rolling friction much smaller than sliding friction
Ball bearings reduce friction by replacing sliding with rolling
Friction causes heating and wear
Friction necessary for acceleration (car tires)
Ice has low μ (≈0.03) making slipping easy
Friction force parallel to surface, normal force perpendicular
On inclined plane: f = mg sinθ at angle of repose
Fluid friction (drag) depends on velocity, shape

Block on Inclined Plane:

For block on incline at angle θ:

If tan θ < μ_s: Block doesn't slide (f = mg sinθ)

If tan θ > μ_s: Block slides with a = g(sinθ - μ_k cosθ)

Critical angle: θ_c = tan⁻¹(μ_s) = angle of repose

Always directed toward center of circle

Centripetal force is not a new force but net radial inward force
Centrifugal force is pseudo-force in rotating frame
For UCM, speed constant but velocity changes (direction changes)
Acceleration directed toward center (centripetal acceleration)
Angular velocity ω = dθ/dt = v/r (constant for UCM)
Time period T = 2πr/v = 2π/ω
Frequency f = 1/T = ω/(2π)
Vertical circle: Speed varies due to gravity
Minimum speed at top: v_min = √(gr) for complete circle
Minimum speed at bottom: v_bottom = √(5gr) for complete loop
Tension difference top-bottom = 6mg for complete loop
Conical pendulum period independent of bob mass
Banking allows higher speeds without relying on friction
Centripetal acceleration a_c = v²/r = ω²r
Non-uniform circular motion: Tangential acceleration present

Vertical Circle Energy Analysis:

Using energy conservation: ½mv_bottom² = ½mv_top² + mg(2r)

At top: T_top + mg = mv_top²/r

Minimum at top: T_top = 0 → v_top = √(gr)

Then v_bottom = √(v_top² + 4gr) = √(5gr)

T_bottom = mg + mv_bottom²/r = 6mg

7. Equilibrium of Forces

Conditions for Equilibrium:
1. ΣF = 0 (Translational equilibrium)
2. Στ = 0 (Rotational equilibrium)

Types of Equilibrium

TypeConditionPotential EnergyExample
StableReturns to original after displacementMinimumBall at bottom of bowl
UnstableMoves away after displacementMaximumBall on hill top
NeutralStays in new positionConstantBall on flat surface

Force Resolution Methods

Component method: Resolve forces along x, y, z axes
ΣF_x = 0, ΣF_y = 0, ΣF_z = 0 for translational equilibrium
Lami's theorem: For 3 concurrent forces in equilibrium
F₁/sinα = F₂/sinβ = F₃/sinγ where α,β,γ are angles between forces
Polygon law: Vector sum of forces forms closed polygon
Triangle law: For 3 forces, they form closed triangle
Free body diagram essential for problem solving
Tension in string: Along string direction
Normal force: Perpendicular to surface
Friction: Parallel to surface, opposes motion
Lami's Theorem (3 concurrent forces):
F₁/sin(π - α) = F₂/sin(π - β) = F₃/sin(π - γ)
or F₁/sinα = F₂/sinβ = F₃/sinγ

Ladder Problem:

Ladder of length L, mass m leaning against wall at angle θ:

Forces: Weight (mg down at center), Normal from wall (N_w horizontal), Normal from floor (N_f vertical), Friction from floor (f horizontal)

Equations: ΣF_x = 0: f = N_w; ΣF_y = 0: N_f = mg

Στ = 0 about bottom: mg(L/2 cosθ) = N_w(L sinθ)

Condition for no slipping: f ≤ μ N_f

Minimum angle: θ_min = tan⁻¹(1/(2μ))

8. Advanced Applications & Problem Types

Pulley Systems

Massless, frictionless pulley: Tension same on both sides
With pulley mass or friction: Tensions different
Atwood machine: a = (m₁ - m₂)g/(m₁ + m₂)
Tension in Atwood: T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁ + m₂)
Multiple pulley systems: Mechanical advantage
Movable pulley: Tension supports half the load
Constraint relation: Length of string constant
Acceleration relation from string constraint

Variable Acceleration Problems

Force as function of time: F(t) → integrate for v, x
Force as function of velocity: F(v) → separate variables
Force as function of position: F(x) → use work-energy
Air resistance: F = -kv (linear) or F = -kv² (quadratic)
Terminal velocity when net force = 0
Simple harmonic motion: F = -kx → a = -ω²x
Damped oscillations: F = -kx - bv

Non-Inertial Frame Problems

Add pseudo force F_pseudo = -ma_frame
Direction opposite to frame acceleration
Examples: Accelerating elevator, rotating frame
Rotating frame: Centrifugal + Coriolis forces
Centrifugal force: F_cf = mω²r outward
Coriolis force: F_cor = 2mv × ω
Apparent gravity: g_eff = g - a (vector difference)

Accelerating Elevator:

Elevator accelerating upward with acceleration a:

Apparent weight: W' = m(g + a)

If accelerating downward: W' = m(g - a)

Free fall (a = g): Weightless (W' = 0)

Pendulum in elevator: T = 2π√(L/g_eff) where g_eff = g ± a

9. Quick Revision & Must-Know Facts

Core Laws in One Place

  • 1st Law: Inertia - No force → constant velocity
  • 2nd Law: F = dp/dt = ma (for constant m)
  • 3rd Law: Action = -Reaction (different bodies)
  • Momentum Conservation: If F_ext = 0, Σp = constant

Formula Summary

• Impulse: J = Δp = F_avg Δt
• Friction: f_s ≤ μ_s N, f_k = μ_k N
• Centripetal force: F_c = mv²/r = mω²r
• Banking angle: tan θ = v²/(rg) (no friction)
• Vertical circle: v_top_min = √(gr), v_bottom_min = √(5gr)
• Coefficient of restitution: e = (v₂ - v₁)/(u₁ - u₂)
• Rocket: v = u + v_rel ln(m₀/m)
• Atwood machine: a = (m₁ - m₂)g/(m₁ + m₂)
• Tension in Atwood: T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁ + m₂)
• Angle of repose: tan θ = μ_s

Problem Solving Strategy

1. Identify all forces (draw free-body diagram)
2. Choose coordinate system (often along acceleration)
3. Apply ΣF = ma component-wise
4. Solve equations simultaneously
5. Check units and reasonableness
6. For systems: Consider constraints
7. For collisions: Use momentum conservation
8. For circular motion: Radial F_net = mv²/r

Common Pitfalls to Avoid:

  • Action-reaction act on different bodies (don't cancel)
  • Normal force not always equal to mg
  • Static friction adjusts (not always μ_sN)
  • Centripetal force is net radial force, not separate force
  • Tension in massless rope same throughout
  • Weightlessness means N = 0, not g = 0
  • In elastic collisions, relative speed same before/after
