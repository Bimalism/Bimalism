1. Transition Elements – General Introduction

  • d-block: Elements with incomplete d-orbitals in atom or ion
  • General config: (n–1)d¹⁻¹⁰ ns¹⁻² (Zn, Cd, Hg not transition)
  • Zn group excluded: Stable d¹⁰, no variable oxidation state
  • Found in middle of periodic table (Group 3 to 12)
  • Form coloured ions due to d-d transition
  • Paramagnetic due to unpaired d-electrons
  • Form complexes easily (vacant d-orbitals)
  • Show catalytic property (variable oxidation states)
  • Form interstitial compounds & alloys
  • JEE loves: Why Zn is not a transition element?

2. Physical Properties of 3d Series

  • Atomic radii decrease Sc → Zn (poor shielding of d-electrons)
  • Mn, Zn show anomaly due to half & fully filled d-orbitals
  • Melting point: Highest at middle (Cr, Mo), low at ends (Zn, Cd, Hg)
  • Ionization enthalpy: Increases across period, but irregular
  • Density increases from Sc to Cu (more protons + compact size)
  • All are hard, high tensile strength except Zn, Cd, Hg
  • High enthalpy of atomization due to strong metallic bonding
  • Metallic luster and good conductors of heat & electricity
  • Most have BCC/FCC/HCP structure
  • JEE favourite: Trend of atomic radius & melting point

3. Chemical Properties of 3d Series

  • Variable oxidation states: Due to small energy gap between (n–1)d and ns
  • Maximum oxidation state = Group number (except Fe, Co, Ni)
  • Higher oxidation states more stable towards right (covalent)
  • Lower states more stable towards left (ionic)
  • Oxides: Basic in low OS, acidic in high OS (Mn₂O₇ acidic)
  • Form coloured compounds (except d⁰ and d¹⁰ ions)
  • Magnetic: Paramagnetic (unpaired e⁻), diamagnetic (d¹⁰)
  • Complex formation: Due to small size, high charge, vacant d-orbitals
  • Interstitial compounds (C, N, H trapped), non-stoichiometric
  • Alloy formation due to similar size

4. K₂Cr₂O₇ and KMnO₄ – Preparation & Properties

  • K₂Cr₂O₇ from chromite ore: FeCr₂O₄ → Na₂CrO₄ → K₂Cr₂O₇
  • KMnO₄ from pyrolusite: MnO₂ → K₂MnO₄ → KMnO₄ (alkaline oxidative fusion)
  • K₂Cr₂O₇ orange, Cr₂O₇²⁻ ⇌ 2CrO₄²⁻ (acidic ↔ basic)
  • KMnO₄ purple, strong oxidizing agent in all media
  • Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 14H⁺ + 6e⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O (acidic medium)
  • MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O (acidic)
  • In neutral/weakly basic: MnO₄⁻ → MnO₂ (3e⁻ change)
  • K₂Cr₂O₇ used in tanning, volumetric analysis
  • KMnO₄ self-indicator, decolourizes at end point
  • JEE loves: Colour change in titration & oxidizing action

5. Lanthanoids (4f Series)

  • Electronic config: [Xe] 4f¹⁻¹⁴ 5d⁰⁻¹ 6s²
  • Common oxidation state +3 (most stable)
  • Ce⁴⁺, Eu²⁺, Tb⁴⁺, Yb²⁺ due to stable f⁰, f⁷, f¹⁴ config
  • Lanthanoid contraction: Steady decrease in size from La to Lu
  • Cause: Poor shielding of 4f electrons → increased Z_eff
  • Consequences: Zr–Hf similar size, basicity decreases La → Lu
  • Highly electropositive, react with water slowly
  • Oxides and hydroxides are basic (basicity ↓ down series)
  • Form few complexes (larger size, high coordination)
  • JEE favourite: Lanthanoid contraction & its consequences

6. Actinoids (5f Series)

  • Electronic config: [Rn] 5f¹⁻¹⁴ 6d⁰⁻¹ 7s²
  • Show +3 to +6 oxidation states (more variable than lanthanoids)
  • Most stable state +3, but U, Np, Pu show higher states
  • Actinoid contraction more than lanthanoid (5f poorer shielding)
  • Highly radioactive (except Th, U)
  • More reactive than lanthanoids, react with hot water
  • Form more coloured compounds and complexes
  • Paramagnetic, ions are coloured
  • Used in nuclear reactors (U-235, Pu-239)
  • JEE asks: Difference in oxidation states & reactivity

7. JEE Must-Revise Points (Daily Revision)

  • Zn, Cd, Hg not transition elements (d¹⁰, no variable OS)
  • Highest oxidation state in 3d series: Mn(+7), Cr(+6)
  • KMnO₄ in acidic medium → 5e⁻ change, neutral → 3e⁻
  • Lanthanoid contraction → Zr ≈ Hf size
  • Colour of ions: d⁰ and d¹⁰ → colourless
  • K₂Cr₂O₇ in acid ⇌ orange, in base ⇌ yellow
  • Mn shows maximum oxidation states (+2 to +7)
  • Actinoids more reactive and variable OS than lanthanoids
  • Magnetic moment ∝ √n(n+2), n = unpaired electrons
  • Catalytic property due to variable oxidation states
