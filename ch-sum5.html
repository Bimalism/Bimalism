1. Concentration Units

  • Molarity (M) = moles of solute / volume of solution in litres
  • Molality (m) = moles of solute / kg of solvent (temperature independent)
  • Mole fraction (x) = moles component / total moles (sum = 1)
  • Mass % = (mass solute / mass solution) × 100
  • Volume % = (volume solute / volume solution) × 100
  • M × d = m × (1000 / 1000 + mM₂) → interconversion formula
  • Molarity decreases with increase in temperature (volume expands)
  • Molality preferred in colligative properties
  • Parts per million (ppm) = (mass solute / mass solution) × 10⁶
  • JEE loves conversion between M, m, % and mole fraction

2. Types of Solutions

  • Ideal solution: Obeys Raoult’s law at all T, P; ΔH_mix = 0, ΔV_mix = 0
  • Examples: Benzene + Toluene, n-Hexane + n-Heptane
  • Non-ideal: Deviates from Raoult’s law
  • Positive deviation: ΔH_mix > 0, weaker interactions (Ethanol + Water)
  • Negative deviation: ΔH_mix < 0, stronger interactions (Acetone + Chloroform)
  • Azeotropes: Constant boiling mixtures (95.6% ethanol + water)
  • Maximum boiling azeotrope → negative deviation
  • Minimum boiling azeotrope → positive deviation
  • Solubility of gases decreases with increase in temperature
  • Henry’s law: P = K_H × x (for dilute gas solutions)

3. Raoult’s Law – Ideal Solutions

  • P_A = P°_A × x_A, P_B = P°_B × x_B (for volatile components)
  • Total vapour pressure P = P°_A x_A + P°_B x_B
  • P vs x plot is linear for ideal solutions
  • Raoult’s law is limiting law for dilute solutions
  • Relative lowering of vapour pressure = (P° – P)/P° = x_solute
  • For non-volatile solute: P = P°_solvent × x_solvent
  • ΔP/P° = mole fraction of solute (colligative property)
  • Ideal solutions obey Raoult’s law over entire composition range
  • Liquid pairs with similar molecular size & forces form ideal solutions
  • JEE asks plotting P vs x and identifying ideal behaviour

4. Non-Ideal Solutions & Azeotropes

  • Positive deviation: P_total > P_ideal (weaker A–B than A–A, B–B)
  • Negative deviation: P_total < P_ideal (stronger A–B interactions)
  • Positive deviation → endothermic mixing, volume expansion
  • Negative deviation → exothermic mixing, volume contraction
  • Maximum boiling azeotrope: HCl + H₂O (20.2% HCl), HNO₃ + H₂O
  • Minimum boiling azeotrope: Ethanol + Water (95.6% ethanol)
  • In positive deviation, vapour richer in more volatile component
  • Non-ideal curves show maximum/minimum in P vs x plot
  • Azeotrope cannot be separated by fractional distillation
  • JEE favourite: Identify type of deviation from ΔH_mix sign

5. Colligative Properties

  • Depend only on number of solute particles (not nature)
  • Relative lowering of VP: ΔP/P° = i × n₂/(n₁ + n₂) ≈ i × m/M₁
  • Elevation in boiling point: ΔT_b = i × K_b × m
  • Depression in freezing point: ΔT_f = i × K_f × m
  • Osmotic pressure: π = i × CRT = i × (n/V) RT
  • All colligative properties ∝ molality × van’t Hoff factor
  • K_b and K_f are solvent constants
  • Water: K_f = 1.86, K_b = 0.512; Camphor: K_f = 39.7 (high ΔT_f)
  • Reverse osmosis used in desalination
  • JEE asks calculation of molecular mass from any colligative property
Molecular mass from colligative property:
M₂ = (i × K_b × w₂ × 1000) / (ΔT_b × w₁) → Same for all four

6. van’t Hoff Factor (i)

  • i = Observed colligative property / Normal colligative property
  • i = Total moles of particles after dissociation/association / Initial moles
  • For NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → i = 2 (theoretical)
  • For Al₂(SO₄)₃ → 2Al³⁺ + 3SO₄²⁻ → i = 5
  • Degree of dissociation α = (i – 1)/(n – 1) where n = number of ions
  • For association (e.g., dimer): i = 1 – α + α/2
  • i < theoretical at high concentration due to ion pairing
  • Modified equations: π = iCRT, ΔT_f = i K_f m
  • van’t Hoff factor explains abnormal molar mass
  • JEE loves calculating α from i and vice-versa

7. Abnormal Molar Mass & Applications

  • If i > 1 → observed M < actual M (dissociation)
  • If i < 1 → observed M > actual M (association)
  • Examples: Benzoic acid in benzene → dimer → i = 0.5
  • Electrolytes in water show i > 1
  • KCl, NaCl → i ≈ 1.9 (not 2) due to inter-ionic attraction
  • Debye-Hückel theory explains deviation at high concentration
  • Osmometry, cryoscopy, ebullioscopy used to find molar mass
  • Isotonic solutions have same osmotic pressure
  • Human blood is isotonic with 0.9% NaCl (physiological saline)
  • JEE asks: Why molar mass of NaCl appears less than actual?

8. JEE Most Important Points (Revise Daily)

  • Molality independent of temperature, molarity decreases with T
  • ΔP/P° = x_solute = i × (moles solute / total moles)
  • ΔT_b = i K_b m, ΔT_f = i K_f m, π = i CRT
  • van’t Hoff i = observed / calculated colligative effect
  • Positive deviation → min boiling azeotrope
  • Negative deviation → max boiling azeotrope
  • α = (i – 1)/(n – 1) for electrolytes
  • M_obs = M_actual / i → abnormal molar mass
  • Henry’s law: P = K_H x
  • Reverse osmosis: solvent moves from high to low π
