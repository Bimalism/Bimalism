The Mosquito Threat

Water is essential for human life, but when stagnant, it becomes a breeding ground for disease-carrying mosquitoes that threaten our families.

Critical Problem

We live not only with our families but also with mosquitoes that breed in our immediate surroundings. Proper water management is not just about convenience—it's a matter of public health.

Key Fact

Water stored for more than 7 days provides the perfect environment for mosquitoes to multiply. Even a 1.5mm gap in water containers is enough for mosquitoes to enter and lay eggs.

Common Breeding Sites:

  • Coconut shells, disposable cups, and plastic containers
  • Water collection in flower pots, plant saucers, and ornamental vases
  • Stagnant water in blocked drains, gutters, and window sunshades
  • Uncovered water tanks, sumps, and storage containers
  • Discarded tires, buckets, and any water-holding debris

From Your Documents

Based on the official health department materials, here are the essential points every community member should know:

The 7-Day Rule

Mosquitoes take approximately 7 days to develop from egg to adult. If you eliminate standing water every 7 days, you break their lifecycle.

The 30-Day Impact

Adult mosquitoes live for about 30 days. During this time, a single mosquito can infect multiple people with serious diseases.

Day Biter Alert

Aedes mosquitoes, which spread Dengue and Chikungunya, bite during daytime. Protection is needed even during the day, not just at night.

Critical Intervention Point

The first 7 days are the most critical for intervention. If standing water is eliminated within this period, the mosquito lifecycle is broken and adults never emerge to spread disease.

Diseases Spread by Mosquitoes

Mosquitoes are vectors for multiple serious diseases that affect millions worldwide

Malaria

Caused by Plasmodium parasites transmitted through bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. Symptoms include fever, headache, chills, and vomiting.

Anopheles mosquito

Dengue

Viral infection causing severe flu-like illness. Can develop into potentially lethal dengue hemorrhagic fever. Also known as "breakbone fever" due to severe muscle and joint pain.

Aedes mosquito

Chikungunya

Viral disease transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes. Causes fever and severe joint pain that can persist for months or years. The name means "to become contorted" describing stooped posture of sufferers.

Aedes mosquito

Filaria (Lymphatic Filariasis)

Parasitic disease caused by microscopic worms transmitted by Culex mosquitoes. Can lead to abnormal enlargement of body parts (lymphedema) causing pain and disability.

Culex mosquito

Japanese Encephalitis

Viral brain infection transmitted by Culex mosquitoes. Can cause inflammation of the brain (encephalitis) leading to high fever, headache, disorientation, coma, and death.

Culex mosquito

Zika Virus

Additional mosquito-borne disease transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes. Can cause birth defects in pregnant women and is linked to Guillain-Barré syndrome.

Aedes mosquito

Correct vs Incorrect Practices

Based on Health Department guidelines from your documents

Incorrect Practices (To Avoid) Correct Practices (To Implement)
Leaving coconut shells upright where rainwater can collect Keep coconut shells turned upside down to prevent water collection
Using disposable plastic cups that can collect water Properly dispose of or store disposable containers
Leaving water containers uncovered and open Keep all water pots closed with proper fitting lids
Allowing old tires to collect rainwater Make holes in tires for water drainage or store them properly
Letting water stagnate in ornamental vases for weeks Change water in vases and decorative containers once a week
Not maintaining soak pits and septic tanks properly Ensure proper maintenance without 1.5mm gaps, cover vent pipes with nets
Leaving sumps and overhead tanks unsealed Properly seal all water storage without leaving 1.5mm gaps

Aedes Mosquito

  • Biting Time: Day biter (especially early morning and late afternoon)
  • Breeding Site: Clean stagnant water in containers, flower pots, coconut shells
  • Diseases: Dengue, Chikungunya, Zika, Yellow Fever
  • Habitat: Urban areas, inside and around homes
  • Identification: Black with white stripes on legs and body

Anopheles Mosquito

  • Biting Time: Most active at dusk and dawn
  • Breeding Site: Clean water, ponds, puddles, uncovered tanks
  • Diseases: Malaria
  • Habitat: Rural and urban areas, prefers shaded breeding sites
  • Identification: Pale and dark marks on wings, sits at 45-degree angle

Culex Mosquito

  • Biting Time: Night biter
  • Breeding Site: Polluted water, drains, septic tanks, cesspools
  • Diseases: Filaria, Japanese Encephalitis, West Nile Virus
  • Habitat: Urban areas with poor sanitation
  • Identification: Brownish with unmarked wings, sits parallel to surface

Complete Prevention Strategy

A multi-layered approach to protect your family and community

Weekly Prevention Checklist

Inspect Property

Check all potential water collection sites around your home

Empty Containers

Drain water from flower pots, plant saucers, pet bowls

Cover Water Storage

Ensure all water containers have tight-fitting lids

Clean Gutters & Drains

Remove debris that can cause water stagnation

Dispose of Trash

Properly discard containers that can hold water

Maintain Swimming Pools

Keep properly chlorinated or covered when not in use

7-Day Community Action Plan

A step-by-step guide to implementing mosquito control in your community

1

Awareness Campaign

Educate community members about the 7-day lifecycle and 30-day impact. Distribute informational materials.

2

Community Cleanup

Organize a neighborhood cleanup to remove potential breeding sites like discarded containers and tires.

3

Water Management

Implement proper water storage practices and ensure all containers are covered or emptied weekly.

4

Monitoring System

Establish a community monitoring system to check for breeding sites and report stagnant water.

5

Ongoing Maintenance

Create a schedule for weekly inspections and maintenance to prevent mosquito breeding.
